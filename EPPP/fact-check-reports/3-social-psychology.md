### Finding 1 - JUDGMENT
**Claim (Base Rate Fallacy section, ~line 48):** "social anxiety affects only about 7% of the population (the base rate)"
**OE verdict:** Accurate as the 12-month prevalence. DSM-5-TR gives ~7% 12-month prevalence of social anxiety disorder in U.S. adults, matching the NCS-R figure of 7.1%. Lifetime prevalence is higher (about 12-13%). So the 7% figure is correct for 12-month prevalence but is not the lifetime rate. The lesson's use of "7% of the population" is the standard EPPP-accepted number and is not an error; flagging only because the population-level lifetime figure is higher.
*Cite: DSM-5-TR (2022); Stein & Stein, Lancet 2008; Leichsenring & Leweke, NEJM 2017.*
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** Optional clarity tweak, not required: "social anxiety affects about 7% of adults in a given year (the base rate)".

Verified against standard references (high confidence, not separately OE-queried): Milgram agentic state, situational variations (rundown office, same-room proximity, phone orders, defiant peers), and psychiatrists' pre-study estimate (~1 in 1,000 to max shock 450V); Burger 2009 replication stopping at 150V with 70% continuing vs Milgram baseline 82.5% and no significant difference; Sherif autokinetic effect; Asch 1951 line-judgment conformity; informational vs normative influence and private acceptance vs public compliance; foot-in-the-door and door-in-the-face (perceptual contrast, reciprocity norm) compliance techniques; Brehm & Brehm 1981 psychological reactance. Numbers and attributions accurate.

### Finding 1 - MISLEADING
**Claim (Milgram section, ~lines 32 and 65; Misconception 2, ~line 167):** "Stanley Milgram's research in 1974..." and "Milgram's studies happened in the 1970s" and "Milgram's 1970s studies"
**OE verdict (verified against standard references):** The Milgram obedience experiments were conducted in 1961-1962 at Yale, and the first results were published in 1963 (Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology). The year 1974 is when Milgram's book "Obedience to Authority" was published, not when the studies were run. Citing "1974" as the research date and calling them "1970s studies" is a date error. Burger explicitly framed his 2009 work as replicating a study from the 1960s.
*Cite: Standard references; Milgram (1963), Milgram (1974 book); Burger, American Psychologist 2009.*
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** Change "Stanley Milgram's research in 1974" to "Stanley Milgram's research, run in the early 1960s (and written up in his 1974 book)". In Misconception 2, change "Milgram's 1970s studies" to "Milgram's 1960s studies". In the Modern Replications intro, change "Milgram's studies happened in the 1970s" to "Milgram's studies happened in the early 1960s".

**Excerpt sync:** grep questionsGPT for excerpts quoting "1974" or "1970s" tied to Milgram.
