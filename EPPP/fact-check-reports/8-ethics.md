## 8-practice-issues.md
URL: /resources/topics/8-ethical-legal-professional-issues/professional-issues

Verified against standard references (high confidence, not separately OE-queried): the Four Ds of malpractice (duty, dereliction, damages, direct cause); the insanity-vs-competency distinction (insanity = mental state at time of offense; competency = present ability to understand charges and assist counsel); fact-witness vs expert-witness roles; equivocal-death and suicide psychological autopsies; APA inclusive-language guidelines; subpoena response steps; PSYPACT structure (E.Passport + APIT for telepsychology; IPC + TAP for up to 30 days temporary in-person practice); APA Commission on Accreditation as the specialized accreditor; JARS reporting standards; ASPPB supervision concepts. OE not applicable (non-clinical).

### Finding 1 - MISLEADING
**Claim ("Legal Terms That Trip People Up", ~line 95):** "the insanity defense is used in only 1% of criminal cases and succeeds about 25% of the time."
**Verdict:** These are the classic, widely-cited figures from the National Institute of Mental Health / Eight-State insanity study (Callahan et al., 1991), and they are the numbers the EPPP traditionally tests. They are defensible as the exam-accepted answer. The caveat: the "~25% success rate" applies to cases where the defense is actually raised, and success varies a lot by jurisdiction. The 1%/25% pairing is fine for the exam but is a rough national average, not a precise current statistic.
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** "the insanity defense is used in only about 1% of criminal cases, and when it is used, it succeeds roughly a quarter of the time (the exact rate changes by state)."

**Excerpt sync:** grep questionsGPT for excerpts quoting the insanity 1% / 25% line.

### Finding 2 - JUDGMENT
**Claim ("Most disciplinary actions", ~line 35):** ranked top-ten list of disciplinary-action reasons "based on data from 1974 to 2023."
**Verdict:** ASPPB does track disciplinary-action data and the listed categories (unprofessional conduct, sexual misconduct, negligence, dual relationships, etc.) are the standard ones taught for the EPPP. The exact rank order and the specific "1974 to 2023" window are not something I can confirm against a published authoritative source; the categories are right but the precise ordering is presented with more certainty than the public data supports. Flagging as JUDGMENT, not an error, because the categories match the exam-accepted teaching.
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** none required; consider softening "here are the top ten reasons" to "these are among the most common reasons" if precise rank order cannot be sourced.

### Finding 3 - JUDGMENT
**Claim ("Primary vs. Delegated Supervisors", ~line 208):** "Keep detailed supervision records for 7 years or until licensure (whichever is longer)."
**Verdict:** Supervision-record retention is set by state boards and varies; the "7 years or until licensure" figure tracks common board guidance and ASPPB-style recommendations, but it is not a single universal rule. Defensible as the commonly taught standard. JUDGMENT, not an error.
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** none required; the "(whichever is longer)" framing already signals it is a guideline.

All other checked claims accurate.

### Finding 1 - JUDGMENT
**Claim ("Making Ethical Decisions", ~line 28):** attributes the listed 6-step decision model to "Fisher's ethical decision-making model."
**Verdict:** Celia Fisher (author of "Decoding the Ethics Code") is a real, well-known authority and does present an ethical decision-making sequence very close to this. No correction needed; flagged only because the precise step list is a simplification of her published model.
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** none required.

**Claim ("Emerging Areas", ~line 156):** "The APA's Guidelines for Telepsychology (2024)..."

The substance of the claim (stay current, document informed consent when effectiveness data are incomplete) is correct. Risk: a wrong year on a named guideline could be tested.
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** "The APA's telepsychology guidance recommends that psychologists stay current with evolving competencies through research and literature." (Drop the specific year unless the exact edition can be confirmed.)

## 8-standards-3-4.md
URL: /resources/topics/8-ethical-legal-professional-issues/apa-ethics-code-standards-3-4

Verified against standard references (high confidence, not separately OE-queried): Standard 3.01 (unfair discrimination), 3.02 (sexual harassment two-part definition), 3.05 (multiple relationships; not all prohibited, only those that could impair objectivity/risk harm), 3.06 (conflict of interest), 3.07 (third-party requests), 3.10 (informed consent), 3.12 (interruption of services); Standard 4.02 (discussing limits of confidentiality), 4.05 (disclosures), 4.06 (consultations), 4.07 (use of confidential info for didactic purposes). Gottlieb's three-factor model (power, duration, clarity of termination). Tarasoff duty-to-protect elements. Confidentiality (ethical/clinical, held by psychologist) vs privilege (legal right, held by client, court proceedings only). HIPAA governs client record access; minor records pass to the patient at majority. No-suicide contracts lack evidence and do not confer legal protection. OE not applicable (non-clinical).

### Finding 1 - MISLEADING
**Claim ("Confidentiality versus Privilege", ~line 238):** "All 50 states recognize some form of psychotherapist-client privilege."
**Verdict:** Correct and supported by Jaffee v. Redmond (1996), which established psychotherapist-patient privilege in federal courts; all states also recognize some form of the privilege. The statement is accurate. The only nuance worth noting for the exam is that the federal privilege rests on Jaffee v. Redmond, a case the lesson does not name. Not an error; flagged so a Jaffee reference could be added if desired.
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** optional addition: "The U.S. Supreme Court backed this privilege in federal courts in the case Jaffee v. Redmond (1996)."

### Finding 2 - JUDGMENT
**Claim ("Gottlieb's Decision-Making Model", ~line 49):** attributes the power/duration/termination three-factor model to "Gottlieb."
**Verdict:** Correct attribution. Michael Gottlieb's 1993 model for avoiding exploitative dual relationships uses exactly these three dimensions (power, duration, clarity of termination). Accurate. JUDGMENT tier only because the lesson omits the year.
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** none required.

### Finding 3 - MISLEADING
**Claim ("Tarasoff Duty", ~line 195):** frames the duty as one of "duty to protect" but the DICTATOR mnemonic and surrounding text use "warn," "imminent," and a fixed element list.
**Verdict:** Substantively correct. The original Tarasoff I (1974) framed a "duty to warn"; Tarasoff II (1976) reframed it as the broader "duty to protect" (which can be satisfied by warning, notifying police, hospitalizing, etc.). The lesson correctly uses "duty to protect" and lists protect-style actions, which is the modern/EPPP-accepted framing. The word "imminent" is commonly taught but not every jurisdiction's statute uses it. Minor risk only. Not an error.
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** none required; the lesson already says "depending on jurisdiction."

All other checked claims accurate.

## 8-standards-5-6.md
URL: /resources/topics/8-ethical-legal-professional-issues/apa-ethics-code-standards-5-6

Verified against standard references (high confidence, not separately OE-queried): Standard 5.01 (avoidance of false/deceptive statements; degree-credential rule, regionally accredited OR basis for licensure), 5.02 (statements by others), 5.04 (media presentations), 5.05 (testimonials from current clients/vulnerable persons prohibited), 5.06 (in-person solicitation, with disaster-relief and family-participation exceptions); Standard 6.01 (documentation/records), 6.02 (confidentiality of records), 6.03 (withholding records for nonpayment), 6.04 (fees and financial arrangements; 6.04(e) collections after warning), 6.05 (barter), 6.06 (accuracy in reports to payors), 6.07 (referrals and fees, based on services not the referral itself). HIPAA generally bars withholding records for nonpayment. OE not applicable (non-clinical).

### Finding 1 - MISLEADING
**Claim ("How Long to Keep Records", ~line 142):** "For minors: Until 3 years after they reach the age of majority (typically 21 in most states)."
**Verdict:** The retention figures match the APA Record Keeping Guidelines (2007): keep full records 7 years after the last service date for adults, and for minors 3 years after the minor reaches the age of majority. BUT the parenthetical "(typically 21 in most states)" is wrong. The age of majority is 18 in the large majority of U.S. states (only a few, such as Alabama and Nebraska at 19, and Mississippi at 21, differ). Saying majority is "typically 21" is FACTUAL-WRONG as stated and could mislead test-takers, since the standard EPPP age of majority is 18. (The math still works: 18 + 3 = age 21, which may be the source of the confusion.)
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** "For minors: keep records until 3 years after the child reaches the age of majority (usually age 18, so often until about age 21)."

**Excerpt sync:** grep questionsGPT for excerpts quoting "typically 21" or the minor record-retention line.

### Finding 2 - JUDGMENT
**Claim ("How Long to Keep Records", ~line 139):** retention figures attributed to "APA's Record Keeping Guidelines."
**Verdict:** Correct. The 2007 APA Record Keeping Guidelines give 7 years (adults) and 3 years past majority (minors) as the recommended minimums, while noting state law may require longer. Accurate. JUDGMENT tier only because these are guideline recommendations, not Ethics Code rules (the lesson correctly says so).
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** none required.

All other checked claims accurate.

## 8-standards-7-8.md
URL: /resources/topics/8-ethical-legal-professional-issues/apa-ethics-code-standards-7-8

Verified against standard references (high confidence, not separately OE-queried): Standard 7.02/7.03 (accuracy in program descriptions and course syllabi), 7.04 (mandatory disclosure of personal info, two conditions), 7.05 (mandatory individual/group therapy; students may choose unaffiliated therapist; evaluating faculty cannot provide it), 7.06 (assessing student performance on established, relevant criteria), 7.07 (no sexual relationships with students/supervisees under evaluative authority); Standard 8.01 (institutional/IRB approval), 8.02 (informed consent to research), 8.04 (offering inducements; equitable alternatives for course credit), 8.05 (dispensing with informed consent), 8.07 (deception), 8.09 (humane care of animals), 8.10 (reporting results; correcting errors), 8.11 (plagiarism), 8.12 (publication credit; student as principal author on dissertation-based articles), 8.13 (duplicate publication), 8.14 (sharing data for verification), 8.15 (reviewers). Research = systematic investigation designed to contribute to generalizable knowledge (federal Common Rule definition). IRB full/expedited/exempt review categories. OE not applicable (non-clinical).

### Finding 1 - MISLEADING
**Claim ("Getting Institutional Approval", ~line 89 and Misconception 3, ~line 216):** "you must... get IRB approval before making modifications to your study," and "Even minor modifications to approved or expedited research require IRB approval before implementation."
**Verdict:** Mostly correct and the EPPP-accepted teaching: under the Common Rule, changes to approved research generally need IRB review/approval before implementation, with the one exception (immediate changes to eliminate apparent hazards) the lesson correctly notes. The nuance: the 2018 revised Common Rule lets some minimal-risk modifications go through expedited review rather than full board review, and truly exempt research is not held to the same continuing-modification requirements. So "even minor modifications... require IRB approval" is slightly over-broad but defensible as the cautious, exam-correct answer.
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** none strictly required; if tightening: "Most changes to approved research need IRB review before you start them, except immediate changes to remove a danger to participants."

### Finding 2 - JUDGMENT
**Claim ("Sharing Data for Verification", ~line 192):** "APA journal submission guidelines: At least 5 years after publication" and "HIPAA... At least 6 years."
**Verdict:** Both figures match standard references: APA requires retaining raw data for at least 5 years after publication; HIPAA's records-retention requirement is 6 years. Accurate. JUDGMENT tier only because retention periods are guideline/regulatory and vary by institution (the lesson says so).
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** none required.

### Finding 3 - JUDGMENT
**Claim ("Informed Consent for Research", ~line 127):** child dissent rules; "when research doesn't offer direct health benefits to the child, the child's dissent usually overrides parental consent."
**Verdict:** Correct statement of federal Subpart D (45 CFR 46) logic on child assent/dissent. Accurate and a commonly tested point. Not an error.
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** none required.

All other checked claims accurate.
