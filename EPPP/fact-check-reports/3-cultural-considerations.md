### Finding 1 - FACTUAL-WRONG
**Claim (Healthy Cultural Paranoia section, approx lines 55-59):** "Ridley distinguished between two types of suspicion... **Functional paranoia** is unhealthy. It's pervasive distrust toward everyone... **Healthy cultural paranoia** is different. It's a rational response to real experiences of prejudice and discrimination."
**OE verdict:** The labels are flipped. In Ridley's typology, "functional paranoia" IS the healthy, adaptive, reality-based cultural mistrust (often used as a synonym for "cultural paranoia"). The unhealthy, generalized, clinical type is called "pathological paranoia," not "functional paranoia." Ridley uses two independent dimensions (cultural mistrust x pathological paranoia) yielding four categories: nonparanoia, cultural paranoia, pathological paranoia, and confluent paranoia. The lesson's contrast of "functional (unhealthy)" vs "healthy cultural" mislabels the term students are tested on.
*Cite: Whaley AL, Confluent Paranoia in African American Psychiatric Patients: An Empirical Study of Ridley's Typology, J Abnorm Psychol. 2002;111(4):568-77; DSM-5-TR note that guarded behavior in oppressed groups should not be confused with paranoid traits.*
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** "Ridley said there are two kinds of distrust. **Pathological (or dysfunctional) paranoia** is unhealthy. It is distrust of everyone, no matter their background. A client with this kind of paranoia will not open up to any therapist. **Functional paranoia (also called healthy cultural paranoia)** is different. It is a smart, fair response to real prejudice and discrimination. A Black client with functional paranoia might open up to a Black therapist but hold back with a White therapist. That is not a sickness. It is learned caution based on real experience."

### Finding 2 - JUDGMENT
**Claim (Own-Race Bias section, approx line 177):** "This affects everyone, though it's slightly stronger among White Americans than Black Americans."
**OE verdict:** The own-race bias / cross-race effect is robust and well-replicated. The specific "stronger in White than Black Americans" framing is the common textbook simplification, but current literature is more nuanced: the effect often does NOT reliably appear for minority-race participants in majority-White settings, likely because they get heavy exposure to White faces. So it is less "Whites have a stronger bias" and more "minority-race status and differential exposure moderate the effect." The exposure/perceptual-expertise explanation is also only partial (contact-to-bias correlation is weak, r approx -0.15); current accounts are interactionist (exposure plus social motivation). Flagging as JUDGMENT, not an error, because the lesson's version is the standard EPPP-tested claim.
*Cite: Toredi D et al., The Impact of Minority-Race Status on the Cross-Race Effect: A Critical Review, Perspect Psychol Sci. 2025; Singh B et al., Does Cross-Race Contact Improve Cross-Race Face Perception? A Meta-Analysis, Pers Soc Psychol Bull. 2022;48(6):865-887.*
**Suggested fix (lesson-voice):** Optional, only if you want to soften the absolute. "This affects everyone. Some studies show it is a bit stronger for White Americans, but newer research suggests it depends a lot on how much exposure a person has had to other-race faces." Keep the original if matching the EPPP answer is the goal.

**Excerpt sync:** if reworded, grep questionsGPT for "slightly stronger among White Americans."

### Other checked claims (verified accurate)
- **LGBTQ stats (approx lines 247-251):** "more than twice the rate of mental disorders... bisexual individuals at highest risk," higher service use, and the gendered disclosure effect (lesbian women who disclose show improved mental health; gay men who recently disclosed show higher depression/anxiety risk) all supported. *Cite: Wittgens C et al., Acta Psychiatr Scand. 2022;145(4):357-372; Pachankis JE, Cochran SD, Mays VM, J Consult Clin Psychol. 2015;83(5):890-901.* The premature-termination claim is the weakest link (some large cohort data show LGB clients have lower attrition once engaged), but it remains the EPPP-accepted framing, so not flagged.
- **Integration paradox (approx lines 147-153):** supported, including both proposed mechanisms (more institutional contact, more cognitive tools to label discrimination). *Cite: de Vroome T, Martinovic B, Verkuyten M, Cultur Divers Ethnic Minor Psychol. 2014;20(2):166-75.*
- **Tight vs loose cultures and Big Five (approx line 143):** directionally supported (tight = higher conscientiousness, lower openness; loose = reverse). These are ecological/country-level tendencies with large within-culture variation, but the lesson's claim is fine as a tendency. Not flagged.
- **Culturally adapted interventions (approx line 217):** supported. They generally improve outcomes vs unadapted treatment, work better by adding features, and help most for low-acculturation / non-English-fluent clients. *Cite: Soto A et al., J Clin Psychol. 2018;74(11):1907-1923; Arundell LL et al., Clin Psychol Rev. 2021;88:102063.*
- **Older adults (approx lines 254-257):** supported. Most mental disorder rates are lower in older adults (neurocognitive disorders excepted) and somatic presentation of depression is more common. *Cite: APA Psychological Practice With Older Adults 2024; Gum AM et al., Am J Geriatr Psychiatry. 2009;17(9):769-81.*
- **KABC-II and Raven's as culture-reduced (approx line 261):** supported as relatively culture-reduced with smaller score gaps than verbal IQ tests. Note: neither is truly "culture-free" and the lesson's stronger phrase "aren't affected by English proficiency or cultural learning" is an overstatement (SES, norms, and stereotype threat still affect scores). Borderline MISLEADING but matches the EPPP shorthand; left unflagged as a finding, noted here.
