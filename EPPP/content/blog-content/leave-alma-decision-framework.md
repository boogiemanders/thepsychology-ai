# Should You Leave Alma After the Aetna Rate Cut? A PhD Decision Framework

The Aetna cut hit on May 20. By the next morning, the Alma Providers Facebook group had a post titled: "I am a psychologist and my Aetna rates dropped by 43.5%. Goodbye Alma."

It got 17 reactions in 10 hours. The comments were a wall of "same."

The instinct to leave is rational. The decision is more complicated.

I am a clinical psychologist. I also run thepsychology.ai. I have spent the last six months interviewing providers about platform economics, building out a matching system, and watching this exact scenario play out in slow motion across the industry. This post is the framework I would use if I were on Alma right now.

## Start with the actual math

The "should I leave Alma" question gets framed as a values discussion. It is really an arithmetic problem.

Three numbers matter:

1. **What Alma is paying you now per session.**
2. **What Aetna pays directly per session, in your state, at your license tier.**
3. **What it costs you to handle billing, credentialing, and patient acquisition yourself.**

If number 2 minus number 3 is bigger than number 1, you should leave. If it is smaller, you should stay. Everything else is noise.

Here is the rough data from public reports and provider interviews:

| Channel | 90834 (45 min) | 90837 (60 min) | Doctoral premium |
|---------|----------------|----------------|------------------|
| Alma (after July 15, 2026) | $113.00 | $113.00 | Gone |
| Direct Aetna contract (PhD/PsyD) | ~$125-$140 | ~$144-$175 | Still present |

One Oregon therapist who left Alma documented the contrast clearly. On a 90834 session, Alma was paying her $79 while collecting $125 from Aetna. The platform was taking $46 per session. After credentialing directly, she kept the full $125.

For a doctoral provider with a maintained credential premium, the gap is larger.

## The credentialing reality

This is the part that stops most people from leaving. Direct credentialing takes 60-120 days. Some providers report 180. During that window, you can see Aetna patients through Alma but not directly. You are in transition.

Things to know:

**You can stay on Alma during credentialing.** Most providers do. There is no contractual penalty for being credentialed both ways during the gap. You just route Aetna patients through whichever channel pays better in the moment.

**Your existing patients can switch over once you are credentialed direct.** They do not lose continuity. They do need to be re-enrolled in your direct billing system, which means new paperwork and a new card on file. Most patients handle this without issue.

**Credentialing is mostly paperwork, not negotiation.** Aetna has a behavioral health provider application. You submit license, NPI, malpractice certificate, education, and CAQH profile. They verify, then send a contract with a fee schedule. You sign or counter.

**The fee schedule is not always negotiable, but it sometimes is.** If you can show high volume or specific specialty value (bilingual, evidence-based modality, underserved population), you can sometimes negotiate up. Providers report 5-15% gains on negotiation.

## When leaving is the obvious move

The framework collapses cleanly in these scenarios:

**You bill mostly 90837 to Aetna patients.** The 43.6% cut hits the heart of your practice. Direct contract gets you back to something like the old rate. Math: clear win.

**You see 15+ Aetna patients per week.** The per-session gain compounds. Even $20 more per session times 15 sessions is $300/week. Across a year, that is $15,000+ in recovered revenue.

**You are comfortable with billing and admin.** Either you handle it yourself or you have a billing service charging 6-8% of revenue. Either way is dramatically cheaper than Alma's effective cut.

**Aetna's direct PhD panel is open in your state.** Check this first. New York and California are mostly open. Some states have closed panels at certain license tiers. If the panel is closed, leaving Alma means leaving Aetna patients behind.

**You have other referral sources.** If your panel is full or your reputation drives word-of-mouth referrals, you do not need Alma's patient acquisition. The platform fee was buying you visibility, and you do not need it.

## When staying is the right call

Less popular take, but real:

**Aetna is a small slice of your panel.** If only 10% of your patients have Aetna, the cut is annoying but not existential. Staying on Alma for the convenience may still pencil out.

**Your Aetna panel is closed direct.** Some areas, especially saturated metros, have closed direct panels at the doctoral tier. Alma may be your only access. In this case, you accept the lower rate or drop Aetna patients entirely.

**You are new in practice and need patient flow.** Alma's patient acquisition engine is real. If you are still building your panel, the visibility may be worth the cut. Reassess in 12-18 months when your direct-referral pipeline is stronger.

**You hate billing and will not do it.** Some providers genuinely do not want to handle eligibility verification, claims submission, denial follow-up, and ERA reconciliation. If hiring a billing service still nets out worse than Alma, stay. (This is rare. Run the numbers.)

**You see a lot of 90832 (30-min) sessions or 90791 evals.** The Aetna cut leaves those rates untouched. If your practice leans short or eval-heavy, the cut is mostly noise to you.

## The middle path: stay and drop Aetna

This is what a meaningful number of providers are doing, based on forum discussion.

You keep your Alma subscription. You remove Aetna from your accepted insurance list inside Alma. You see the other payers through the platform, where the rates have not been cut.

Pros:
- You keep platform conveniences for everything else.
- You do not lose the Cigna, Anthem, BCBS, or other patients you see through Alma.
- You sidestep the 43.6% cut without the credentialing process.

Cons:
- You lose your existing Aetna patients (or route them to cash pay or other channels).
- You bet that Alma's other payers do not follow Aetna's lead. That bet is not free.

The 2024 Optum-Headway precedent suggests other payers may follow. The middle path buys you time without committing.

## The credentialing parallel-process play

If you decide to leave but want to minimize the gap, here is the actual move:

1. **Start direct Aetna credentialing today.** Submit CAQH, license, malpractice cert. Expect 60-120 days.
2. **Keep seeing Aetna patients through Alma until July 14, 2026.** Get paid the old rate while you can.
3. **From July 15 forward, decide per case.** If your direct contract has closed, see Aetna patients through Alma at the cut rate. If not, route them to direct.
4. **As soon as direct is live, transition all new Aetna patients to direct.** Existing patients move as they renew or as opportunity allows.
5. **Once your direct Aetna load is stable, decide whether Alma still earns its $125/month for other payers.** That is a separate question.

This minimizes revenue disruption while building the new path. Most providers I have spoken to who left Alma followed something close to this sequence.

## What about other platforms?

Headway and Grow Therapy work on a percentage model. They take 20-30% of every reimbursement. Optum cut their rates by up to 30% in late 2024 and the platforms passed the cut through.

Switching from Alma to Headway because of the Aetna cut is moving sideways, not forward. The platform structure is the issue, not the brand.

## A small note on contracts and confidentiality

Alma's provider contract reportedly contains language stating that sharing specific rates publicly is a violation. The Facebook post that prompted this article only shared a percentage, not dollar amounts. That was smart.

If you want to vent publicly, share the percentage. Keep the rate sheet to your own records and any private peer group conversations. The contract terms vary, so check your specific agreement.

## What this is really about

The Aetna cut is going to keep happening, in some form, from some payer, on some platform. The reason is structural.

When a platform sits between a clinician and a payer, the platform's incentive is to negotiate the best rate it can get from the payer while paying the clinician the least it can. The clinician has no leverage in either direction. The payer cannot pressure the clinician directly; it pressures the platform. The platform cannot resist; it passes the pressure through.

The only way out is to remove the platform from the middle.

That can mean direct contracting. It can mean private pay. It can mean a different kind of platform — one that does not sit on the payer relationship at all.

This question — "should I leave Alma" — is the small version of a bigger question: how do you build a sustainable doctoral psychology practice in an environment where insurance platforms have structural advantages over you?

The answer for a lot of providers is going to be a hybrid: some direct insurance, some private pay, less platform dependency.
