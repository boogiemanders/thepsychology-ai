# How to Become a Licensed Psychologist: Full Path 2026

If you are searching how to become a licensed psychologist, here is the honest answer up front: it is a long road, usually somewhere between eight and twelve years after high school. There is no shortcut that skips the doctorate, and there is no state that licenses you without one. But the path is clear, and once you can see the whole thing laid out, it stops feeling like a fog and starts feeling like a checklist.

I have walked this road myself. I am a licensed psychologist, and I remember how confusing the early steps were when I was figuring it out. So this post is the map I wish I had: every step from your first college class to the day you can legally call yourself a psychologist, in order, with no fluff.

This is the full path in the United States. Canada is similar in shape (doctorate, supervised hours, the EPPP in most provinces), but the details are set by each province, so check your provincial regulatory body if you are north of the border. Everything below is US-focused.

## Step 1: Earn a bachelor's degree

The first step is a four-year bachelor's degree. A lot of people assume you must major in psychology, but that is not strictly true. Many doctoral programs accept students from related majors like neuroscience, sociology, biology, or even something further afield, as long as you have the prerequisites.

What actually matters at this stage:

- **Prerequisite courses.** Intro psychology, statistics, research methods, and a few core content courses (abnormal, developmental, social) cover most program requirements.
- **GPA.** Doctoral admissions are competitive, especially for funded PhD programs. A strong GPA keeps doors open.
- **Research experience.** This is the quiet difference-maker. Getting into a research lab, even washing test tubes at first, signals that you can do the work and gives you a letter writer who actually knows you.

You do not need your whole career mapped out as a freshman. You just need the prerequisites, the grades, and some real exposure to the field before you apply.

## Step 2: Earn a doctoral degree (PhD or PsyD)

This is the big one. To be licensed as a psychologist, you need a doctorate. A master's degree is not enough for the psychologist title in the US (it can qualify you for other counseling licenses, but that is a different career). The doctorate usually takes four to seven years.

Here is the choice that confuses most people: **PhD versus PsyD.**

- **PhD (Doctor of Philosophy).** Research-heavy. You will run studies, write a dissertation, and learn statistics deeply. PhD programs tend to have small cohorts, competitive admissions, and frequently offer funding (tuition waivers plus a stipend). The classic scientist-practitioner model.
- **PsyD (Doctor of Psychology).** Practice-heavy. More clinical hours, less research emphasis. PsyD programs often have larger cohorts and are more likely to be self-funded, which is part of why they cost more.

Within both, you will also pick a focus, most commonly **clinical** or **counseling** psychology. Clinical leans toward assessment and treating more severe disorders; counseling leans toward wellness, adjustment, and life transitions. The overlap is large, and both lead to licensure.

One thing matters more than the PhD-versus-PsyD label: **APA accreditation.** Attend an APA-accredited program if you possibly can. It smooths internship matching, satisfies licensing boards in every state, and is correlated with better outcomes down the line, including on the licensing exam. Degree type also quietly shapes your odds on that exam later. ## Step 3: Complete a doctoral internship (the match)

Near the end of your doctoral program, you complete a year-long predoctoral internship. This is full-time supervised clinical work, and for most students it is a required part of the degree.

The catch is how you get one. Internships are awarded through a national matching system (run by APPIC), similar to how medical residencies work. You apply, interview, rank your choices, the sites rank candidates, and a computer matches you. Aim for an **APA-accredited internship**, because some states require it for licensure and it keeps your later options open.

The match can be stressful, and not everyone matches on the first round. But it is a known, structured step. Plan for it early in your doctoral program rather than scrambling in your final year.

## Step 4: Complete supervised postdoctoral hours

After the doctorate, most states require a stretch of supervised professional experience before you can be fully licensed. This is often called postdoctoral or supervised practice hours.

Here is where things get state-specific, so read your own state board's rules carefully:

- Many states require roughly **1,500 to 2,000 supervised hours** after the degree.
- Some states count hours you earned during the doctorate toward the total.
- A growing number of states have **dropped the postdoc requirement** entirely, letting you license off your predoctoral training plus the exam.

The supervision itself matters: it must usually be under a licensed psychologist, with regular face-to-face oversight. This is the stage where you are functioning as a psychologist in everything but the final title, building the judgment that classroom work cannot teach.

## Step 5: Pass the EPPP (the national licensing exam)

Every path runs through this gate. The **EPPP** (Examination for Professional Practice in Psychology) is the national licensing exam, written by ASPPB and delivered through Pearson VUE testing centers. It is the one standardized step that is the same no matter which state you are in.

Here is what the current exam looks like:

- **8 content domains** spanning your entire doctoral education, from biological bases to ethics.
- **225 questions**, delivered in **4 hours and 15 minutes**.
- Scored on a **scaled score from 200 to 800**, not a raw percentage.
- The **ASPPB-recommended passing score is 500**. Most states adopt it, though some set their own cutoff. (We track who uses what in our guide to the [EPPP passing score by state](/blog/eppp-passing-score-by-state).)
- The **overall first-time pass rate runs roughly 78 to 82%**.

That last number deserves a second look. Roughly one in five first-time test takers does not pass, and these are people who already finished a doctorate and an internship. The exam does not test raw intelligence; it tests broad, application-based knowledge under time pressure, and it rewards deliberate preparation over clinical experience alone. ## Step 6: Apply for state licensure (and any jurisprudence exam)

Passing the EPPP does not automatically make you licensed. Licensure is granted by the **state board**, and you have to apply to the specific state where you want to practice. The board verifies your degree, your internship, your supervised hours, and your EPPP score against its own requirements.

On top of the EPPP, **many states require a jurisprudence exam.** This is a separate test on that state's specific laws and ethics rules for psychologists. It is usually much shorter and more straightforward than the EPPP, but it is mandatory where it applies, so do not skip checking for it. Some states use an oral exam or other state-specific component instead.

Because every state runs its own process, the smartest move is to read your target state board's licensing page early, before you finish your hours, so there are no surprises at the finish line. If you might move between states later, look into ASPPB's mobility credentials (like the CPQ) that make cross-state licensure easier.

## Step 7: Maintain your license

Getting licensed is not the end. To keep the license, every state requires **continuing education (CE)**, a set number of credit hours you complete during each renewal period (often every one to two years). CE usually includes specific required topics like ethics and, in many states, things like cultural competence or suicide risk assessment.

It is a manageable, ongoing commitment, not another mountain. But it is the reason the license is a living credential and not a one-time trophy.

## The honest timeline

Add it up and the path looks something like this:

1. Bachelor's degree: about 4 years.
2. Doctoral degree: about 4 to 7 years.
3. Doctoral internship: 1 year (usually inside the doctorate).
4. Postdoctoral supervised hours: roughly 1 to 2 years, depending on state.
5. EPPP plus state application: weeks to months.
6. Jurisprudence exam where required: short.

Realistically, most people are looking at **eight to twelve years** from starting college to full licensure. It is a serious commitment of time and money. But the destination is a stable, well-compensated career doing work that genuinely helps people, and every step is concrete and achievable when you take them one at a time.

## FAQ

**How long does it take to become a licensed psychologist?**
Usually 8 to 12 years after high school: about 4 years for a bachelor's, 4 to 7 for the doctorate (internship included), then 1 to 2 years of supervised postdoctoral hours in most states, plus the time to pass the EPPP and finish your state application.

**Do I need a PhD, or is a PsyD enough?**
Either one works for licensure. A PhD is research-heavy and often funded; a PsyD is practice-heavy and usually self-funded. What matters most is that the program is APA-accredited and that it fits the career you want.

**Can I become a licensed psychologist with just a master's degree?**
No. The psychologist title in the US requires a doctorate. A master's can qualify you for related licenses (like licensed professional counselor or marriage and family therapist), but those are different credentials with different scopes.

**Is the EPPP hard to pass?**
The first-time pass rate is roughly 78 to 82%, so most prepared candidates pass, but about one in five does not on the first try. The difficulty comes from breadth and the application-based format, not raw intelligence. Diagnostic-driven, practice-question-based prep is what closes the gap.
