# EPPP Study Hours by Domain: How to Allocate 200 Hours Across 8 Areas

Here is how most people study for the EPPP. They open a prep book at chapter one. They spend three weeks on biological bases because that is what is up first. Then three weeks on cognitive-affective. Then three weeks on social and cultural. They move through the table of contents in order. Equal time per chapter. Roughly 25 hours each.

This is dumb. I will explain why in a minute.

The EPPP is not a course. It is a weighted exam. Some domains count for 16 percent of your score. Some count for 7. If you give them equal time, you are spending the same hours on a domain that can earn you 16 points as you are on one that can earn you 7. You are also ignoring the part that matters most: how good you already are at each one.

You do not need more hours. You need smarter hours. Here is the method that actually works.

## The Mistake: Chapter-Order Study Plans

Every commercial prep program ships with a study plan. Most of them are some version of "spend roughly equal time on each domain, in chapter order." That plan is built for the convenience of the publisher, not for your score.

Two things are wrong with it:

**It ignores domain weight.** Ethics and Assessment are each worth 16 percent of the exam. Research Methods is worth 7. Spending the same hours on Research Methods as on Ethics is bad math. If you can move your Research Methods score from 60 to 80, you gain 1.4 points (20 percentage points times 7 percent weight). If you can move Ethics from 60 to 80, you gain 3.2 points. Same effort. More than double the return.

**It ignores where you actually need help.** If you already score 85 percent on Lifespan Development and 45 percent on Biological Bases, spending 25 hours on each is upside-down. The 85 percent has nowhere to grow. The 45 percent has a giant runway.

A study plan that does not weight by domain importance AND your current performance is leaving 30 to 50 points on the table. That is the gap between a 480 (fail) and a 540 (comfortable pass).

## The Fix: Opportunity Score

Here is the math. It is simple enough to do on a napkin.

**Opportunity score = (100 - your current score) x domain weight**

Translation: how much room you have to grow, multiplied by how much that growth matters.

A domain where you score 50 percent and counts for 16 percent of the exam:
(100 - 50) x 16 = **800**

A domain where you score 80 percent and counts for 7 percent of the exam:
(100 - 80) x 7 = **140**

The first domain is 5.7 times more valuable per study hour. Spend your time there.

Here is a fictional but realistic diagnostic from a candidate I will call Sam. Sam is a PsyD graduate, six months out, no prep yet. Sam takes a full diagnostic and gets these domain scores.

Sam will still hit Lifespan. Lifespan is not skipped. It just does not get the same hours as Assessment because Sam is already at 75 percent there and Assessment is at 55 percent.

## How to Use Your Allocated Hours

You have 36 hours for Assessment. Now what?

Within each domain, follow this rough split:

**~40 percent on content review.** Read or watch lessons on the high-yield topics for that domain. For Assessment, that means psychometrics (reliability, validity, sensitivity, specificity), major instruments (MMPI, WAIS, BDI), and DSM differential diagnosis.

**~40 percent on practice questions.** Application-style, vignette-based. Untimed first, then timed. Review every wrong answer. If you cannot explain why a distractor was wrong, you do not understand the concept.

**~20 percent on synthesis and review.** Flashcards on what you missed. Cross-domain questions (Assessment + Ethics overlap on assessment ethics, for example). Re-test on weak subtopics.

For a 36-hour Assessment block over 8 weeks, that is roughly:
- 14 hours content review
- 14 hours practice questions
- 8 hours synthesis

For a 15-hour Lifespan Development block over the same period, it is:
- 6 hours content review
- 6 hours practice questions
- 3 hours synthesis

The split is the same. The volume shifts.

## Reassess Every 3 to 4 Weeks

Opportunity scores are not static. As you improve, they shift.

Run a fresh practice exam every 3 to 4 weeks. Recalculate your opportunity score for each domain. Adjust your remaining hours.

What usually happens is the domains you were weakest in (highest opportunity scores) improve fastest, because you were spending the most time there. After 6 to 8 weeks, the order may flip. The domain that was your top priority drops to the middle. A domain you were ignoring because you "had it" may need attention because you forgot it.

This is the loop. Diagnostic, allocate, study, reassess, reallocate.

Static study plans cannot do this. Adaptive ones do it automatically.

## Common Mistakes Even Smart Candidates Make

A few patterns I see over and over:

**Studying what feels productive instead of what is needed.** Getting questions right feels good. Studying material you already know is comfortable. That is exactly why it is the wrong place to spend hours.

**Treating Ethics as easy and skipping it.** Ethics is widely considered the most "learnable" domain. It is also worth 16 percent of the exam. Read the APA Ethics Code in full. Time spent here converts almost directly to points.

**Overstudying Bio Bases out of fear.** Biological Bases is one of the hardest-feeling domains. It is also one of the lower-weighted ones (10 percent). Get to "good enough" here, not perfect. Stop at the point of diminishing returns.

**Skipping Research Methods because it is "only 7 percent."** Even at 7 percent, that is 15 to 16 questions. If you go 5/15, you lost roughly 7 percentage points on the overall exam. Get to 65 to 70 percent here. Then move on.

**Not building stamina.** All your studying might be in 30-minute blocks. The exam is 4 hours and 15 minutes. Do at least two full-length practice exams before the real one.
