# Aetna Just Cut Alma Psychologist Rates by 43.6%. Here's the Real Number Behind July 15, 2026.

On May 20, 2026, Alma told its providers that Aetna was changing how it pays them.

The email was polite. The numbers were not.

If you bill 90837 (a 60-minute psychotherapy session) through Alma to an Aetna patient, your rate drops from **$200.31 to $113.00** on July 15, 2026.

That is a 43.6% cut.

Not a typo. Forty-three point six.

The shorter session code, 90834 (45 minutes), drops from **$164.00 to $113.00** — a 31.1% cut.

I am a clinical psychologist. I have been watching this story develop on Student Doctor Network, in private Facebook groups, and in my own inbox. There is a lot of fog in how this is being communicated. This post cuts through it.

## What is actually changing

Aetna told Alma two things, effective July 15, 2026:

1. **Extended sessions will no longer pay more than standard ones.** A 60-minute session (90837) will now pay the same as a 45-minute session (90834).
2. **Doctoral providers will be paid the same as master's-level providers.** The premium for PhDs and PsyDs is gone — on the Alma channel.

Here is the actual rate sheet, as confirmed by providers in the Alma portal:

| CPT Code | Service | Current Rate | New Rate (July 15) | Change |
|----------|---------|--------------|---------------------|--------|
| 90791 | Psychiatric Diagnostic Eval | $183.70 | $183.70 | No change |
| 90832 | Psychotherapy, 30 min | $67.88 | $67.88 | No change |
| 90834 | Psychotherapy, 45 min | $164.00 | $113.00 | **-31.1%** |
| 90837 | Psychotherapy, 60 min | $200.31 | $113.00 | **-43.6%** |
| 90839 | Crisis psychotherapy, 60 min | $146.09 | $146.09 | No change |
| 90840 | Crisis, each additional 30 min | $59.08 | $59.08 | No change |
| 90846 | Family without patient | $112.82 | $112.82 | No change |
| 90847 | Family with patient | $139.97 | $139.97 | No change |

Read that table again. Aetna is not cutting psychology broadly.

They are cutting **standard outpatient psychotherapy in the 45-60 minute range**. Evaluations, crisis work, family sessions, 30-minute check-ins — untouched.

The bread-and-butter clinical hour is the target.

## The math on what this does to your week

Let's say you run a typical caseload through Alma. Twenty Aetna sessions per week, mostly 90837. Before the change, that was:

20 sessions × $200.31 = **$4,006.20 per week** from Aetna

After July 15:

20 sessions × $113.00 = **$2,260.00 per week** from Aetna

That is **$1,746.20 less per week**. About $90,000 per year. For the same work, the same patients, the same clinical license, the same evidence-based care.

This is not a small efficiency adjustment. This is a 43.6% pay cut.

## Why this is the design, not a glitch

Look at the new rate structure. $113.00 for a 45-minute session is $2.51 per minute. $113.00 for a 60-minute session is $1.88 per minute.

Aetna is now actively penalizing you for spending more time with a patient.

The policy signal is clear: shorter sessions, more volume, faster turnover. The exact opposite of what doctoral training teaches you about complex cases, trauma work, or assessment-informed therapy.

If you treat patients who need 60 minutes, Aetna's new structure says you are doing it wrong.

## What the people in the room are saying

**Alma** publicly said: "We disagree with these changes." That is rare from a platform. It tells you the cut is steep enough that Alma is worried about losing providers.

**The American Psychological Association**, through Dr. Stephen Gillaspy: "When reimbursement rates are cut, psychologists face difficult choices about whether they can afford to remain in-network for their patients. The bottom line is this impacts access to care."

**Aetna's official line**: "Our overarching reimbursement policies related to behavioral health providers and license levels have not changed, nor have our policies related to the reimbursement for mental health services."

Read that one again. Aetna is saying their *overall* policy has not changed. Which is technically true. They did not lower the rate they pay you on a direct contract. They lowered what they pay **Alma**. Alma then has to pass that cut to you.

The fingerprints are on the platform, not the payer. That is the design.

**Psychologists on Student Doctor Network and in Alma's own provider community** are not subtle:

- "I am a psychologist and my Aetna rates dropped by 43.5%. Goodbye Alma." (Posted to the Alma Providers group)
- "Why would I accept less pay when you get a cost of living increase?"
- Multiple providers reporting they are canceling Alma and credentialing directly with Aetna.

This is the same pattern UnitedHealth-Optum ran with Headway and Grow Therapy in late 2024. One New York clinical psychologist reported losing $28,000 in annual income overnight when Optum did it. The playbook is now repeating.

## Why doctoral providers get hit hardest

The cut has two layers, and they stack:

**Layer 1**: The 90837-to-90834 equalization hits anyone who bills extended sessions. That is a lot of clinicians, but it hits doctoral providers harder because doctoral practice patterns skew toward longer, more complex work.

**Layer 2**: The license-tier equalization removes the credential premium entirely. A LCSW and a PhD doing the exact same 60-minute session now bill the same and get paid the same.

Doctoral training takes 5 to 7 years. Add internship, postdoc, EPPP. Add board licensing fees. Add malpractice premiums that scale with credential. None of that is reflected in the new Alma-via-Aetna pay schedule.

This is the "double whammy" providers are talking about in the forums. The math is just the visible part.

## What this does not change

A few important things to keep in mind:

**Direct Aetna contracts are not affected** — as of this writing. Aetna's statement specifically said overarching policies have not changed. If you hold a direct contract with Aetna, your fee schedule is your fee schedule. The cut applies to the Alma channel.

**Other insurers on Alma are not affected** — yet. This is an Aetna-specific change. But the 2024 Optum precedent should be a warning. Once one major payer demonstrates the platform-channel cut works, others tend to follow.

**Other CPT codes are untouched** — for now. Diagnostic evaluations, crisis codes, family therapy, and 30-minute sessions stay where they were. If your practice mix happens to lean those directions, the impact is smaller. Most outpatient practices do not lean that way.

## What providers are doing about it

Three patterns are emerging across the forums:

**1. Leaving Alma entirely.** Providers are credentialing directly with Aetna and the other payers. This recovers the full reimbursement Alma was taking and (so far) escapes the rate cut. Credentialing takes 60-120 days.

**2. Dropping Aetna specifically.** Some providers are staying on Alma but removing Aetna from their insurance list. They keep the platform's convenience for other payers and just refuse the 43.6% cut.

**3. Shifting toward private pay.** A growing number of doctoral providers are using this as the trigger to build out a cash-pay practice. Private pay rates for doctoral psychologists in major markets run $200-$400 per session. No platform fee. No payer cut. No 43.6% surprises.

The pattern is the same: providers are realizing the platform-payer-provider triangle is structurally tilted against them, and they are looking for a different geometry.

## The bigger story

This is not really about Aetna. It is about the model.

When you sign on with Alma, Headway, or Grow Therapy, you do not hold the contract with the insurance company. The platform does. You are a subcontractor. When the payer squeezes the platform, you eat the squeeze. You do not have a seat at the table.

The selling point of platforms has always been convenience: they handle credentialing, billing, eligibility checks, and patient acquisition. Fair trade. But the price of that convenience has been climbing, and now the convenience model has a structural vulnerability that the 2024 Optum cuts and this 2026 Aetna cut have made impossible to ignore.

You can sit inside the platform and absorb the cuts. You can leave and rebuild your panel directly. Or you can pivot away from the insurance dependency entirely.
