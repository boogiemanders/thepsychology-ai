# EPPP Cost Breakdown 2026: True Total Spend (With Hidden Fees)

The EPPP costs $600. That is the number on the ASPPB website. That is also the number that gets quoted in every "how to get licensed" guide. And it is not the number you are actually going to spend.

Here is the truth: when you add the Pearson VUE sitting fee, your state board application, study materials, and the very real chance you have to sit at least twice, the total spend usually lands between $1,500 and $3,000. For people who retake more than once, it climbs past $4,000 fast.

The most expensive part is not the test. It is failing the test. Each fail costs you another $691.88 in nonrefundable fees, plus another three to six months of study time, plus another three to six months of postponed licensure income. That last part is where the real money is.

This post lays out the full bill. The line items you see on the receipt and the ones you do not.

## The Sticker: $691.88 Per Sitting

Every time you sit for the EPPP, you owe two organizations:

**ASPPB (the exam owner): $600.** This is the registration fee for EPPP Part 1. Nonrefundable. You pay it through the ASPPB candidate portal after your state board authorizes you to test.

**Pearson VUE (the test center): $91.88.** This is the sitting fee. Also nonrefundable. You pay it when you schedule your appointment.

Total per attempt: **$691.88**. Both fees reset every time you sit, whether you pass or not.

If you reschedule less than 31 days out, you pay the $91.88 again. If you no-show, you lose all of it. Show up. Bring your ID.

## State Application Fees (Varies)

Before you can register for the EPPP at all, your state psychology board has to authorize you. That requires a separate application with its own fee. These vary widely.

| State (sample) | Approximate App Fee |
|---------|---------------------|
| Indiana | $50 |
| California | $231 (initial licensure request, additional steps have their own fees) |
| South Carolina | $250 (nonrefundable application/temp permit) |
| New York | $79.56 (Pearson VUE scheduling fee for NY, plus state licensure fees separately) |

A few notes on this. State fee structures are convoluted. Most states charge an initial application fee ($50 to $400 range), and many add a separate licensure fee once you pass ($100 to $500 range). Some states also charge for fingerprinting, jurisprudence exams, or background checks. A few require malpractice or supervision documentation that costs money to produce.

Realistic range for the state-board side of things: **$150 to $700** before you sit for the EPPP. Plan for another $100 to $500 after you pass for your actual license. Check your state board's website for the current line items. Do not assume what your friend in another state paid is what you will pay.

## The Real Cost Most People Ignore: Failing Once

Here is the math nobody puts on the spreadsheet.

If you fail the EPPP on attempt one and pass on attempt two, your direct cost goes up by **$691.88**, the full sitting fee again, paid out of pocket.

But the direct cost is not the big number. The big number is the income you do not earn while you study for the retake. Postdocs make somewhere between $40,000 and $60,000. Once you are licensed, your earning power jumps. Each month of delay to licensure is roughly $3,000 to $8,000 in earnings you postponed, depending on your specialty and market.

A typical retake adds 3 to 6 months of delay. That is **$9,000 to $48,000** in delayed earnings.

| Scenario | Direct Fees | Delayed Income (3 mo) |
|---------|-------------|-----------------------|
| Pass first try | $691.88 | $0 |
| Fail once, pass second | $1,383.76 | $9,000 to $24,000 |
| Fail twice, pass third | $2,075.64 | $18,000 to $48,000 |

This is the real cost of failing. Not the $691.88 you see on the credit card statement. The compound delay to your career.

## How to Cut the Bill Without Cutting Your Odds

If your budget is tight, here is where to spend and where to save.

**Worth spending on:**

- A diagnostic exam before you build your study plan. You cannot prioritize what you have not measured.
- Application-style practice questions that match the real exam format.
- A full-length, 225-question simulation before your real attempt. Stamina is a separate skill from knowledge.

**Worth saving on:**

- Premium prep packages with features you will not use. Most people use about 30 percent of what a $1,500 package includes.
- "Audio review" courses if you do not actually learn by audio. Be honest about your learning style.
- Multiple prep programs in parallel. Pick one. Use it fully. Adding a second program usually means studying both at half capacity.

**Not optional:**

- Sleep, exercise, and rest days during prep. Burnout is a budget item. You just do not see it on the credit card statement.
