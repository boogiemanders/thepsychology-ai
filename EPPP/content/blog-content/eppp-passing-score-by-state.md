# EPPP Passing Score by State (2026): What You Actually Need to Pass

When I was studying for the EPPP, I spent an entire afternoon trying to figure out what score I actually needed to pass in my state. I read maybe a dozen blog posts. Half of them said 500. One said 550 for New York. Another said Georgia required 70%. A few said it varied wildly by jurisdiction. I closed my laptop more confused than when I opened it.

So I went to the source. I pulled the official 2024 ASPPB Candidate Handbook, called my state board, and cross-checked against the actual licensing authority pages for every jurisdiction I could find.

Here is what almost no other blog post will tell you straight: **the EPPP passing score for independent licensure is the same in every US state and territory. It is 500.**

That is not a guess. That is a direct quote, in different words, from ASPPB itself. The variations you see online either confuse the scale (New York's "75" is the same 500 expressed differently) or apply to supervised practice / master's-level licensure, which is a separate question.

This post breaks down the real numbers: the scaled score, the score range, what 500 actually means in terms of questions correct, where the genuine state-by-state variation lives, and what to do if you are still anxious about your specific jurisdiction.

## The Single Most Important Sentence from the Official ASPPB Handbook

The May 2024 EPPP Candidate Handbook says this on page 14, word for word:

> "At the present time, all licensing authorities accept the ASPPB recommended passing score for independent practice on the EPPP (Part 1-Knowledge), but there is some variation among licensing authorities regarding the passing score for supervised practice."

Two things to internalize.

First: the cutoff for independent licensure is universal. Every state board, every US territory, every Canadian provincial college that uses the EPPP accepts the ASPPB recommended score of 500 for full, independent practice. If you are pursuing standard doctoral-level licensure as a psychologist, your target is 500. Full stop.

Second: variation exists, but it lives in two specific places. Supervised practice cutoffs (some boards use 450, some do not allow supervised licensure at all), and master's-level limited licensure (a small handful of states like Michigan and Vermont). These are not paths most doctoral candidates need to worry about.

The "different cutoffs by state" narrative is mostly an artifact of older blog posts citing each other, or of confusing different score scales used by different boards in their internal reporting.

## What the Scaled Score Actually Means

Per the official handbook, EPPP scores are scaled from **200 to 800**. The Part 1 (Knowledge) exam contains 225 items, of which 175 are scored and 50 are unscored pretest items. The Part 2 (Skills) exam contains 170 items, of which 130 are scored.

The scaled score is a statistical conversion of your raw score (number correct out of 175) onto the 200 to 800 scale. The conversion adjusts for the difficulty of the specific form you sat for. If your form was harder, fewer questions correct will land you at 500. If your form was easier, you need more correct. ASPPB calls this equating, and it is the same logic the GRE, MCAT, and SAT use.

A scaled score of 500 corresponds to roughly **70 percent of scored items correct**, or about 122 to 123 out of 175. This is a rough rule of thumb. The exact raw cutoff varies by form because of the equating process.

For Part 2 (Skills), the same recommended scores apply: 500 for independent practice, 450 for supervised practice.

**Where real variation exists:** the EPPP (Part 2-Skills) is only required in jurisdictions that have adopted Part 2. Not every board has. The list of adopting boards changes; check ASPPB directly for the current list.

## The New York "75" Myth, Explained

The most common piece of misinformation online is that New York requires a 550, or that New York has a different passing standard. Neither is true.

New York State Education Department, on its official Psychology license requirements page, says you must earn "a converted score of at least 75 as determined by the State Board for Psychology."

That 75 is **not** out of 100. It is New York's internal scale label for the same ASPPB scaled score of 500. New York adopts the standard ASPPB cutoff. It just reports it on its own internal scale because of how the state board's score reporting infrastructure works.

If you are licensing in New York, your target on the actual exam is the same 500 you would aim for in any other state.

## The Supervised Practice Variation, and Why Most Doctoral Candidates Can Ignore It

If you have read this far you might be wondering: where is the 450 cutoff in all of this?

ASPPB recommends 450 as the cutoff for **supervised practice**. This applies in jurisdictions that license psychologists at a non-doctoral or non-independent level. Examples:

- **Michigan** offers a limited license for master's-level practitioners. The cutoff for master's-level limited licensure is 450 on the EPPP. For doctoral-level full licensure, the cutoff is still 500.
- **Vermont** offers a master's-level psychologist credential with different requirements.
- A handful of provincial colleges in Canada permit supervised practice with a lower EPPP threshold during a candidacy period.

If you completed a doctorate (PhD, PsyD, EdD) in psychology and you are seeking standard independent licensure as a psychologist, the 450 cutoff does not apply to you. Your target is 500.

If you are pursuing a master's-level limited license or working in one of the small number of jurisdictions that distinguish supervised from independent psychologist licensure, check your specific board's rules. Even then, the underlying EPPP standard is set by ASPPB.

## What Your Score Report Will Look Like

Per the handbook, you receive an unofficial score at the test center immediately after completing the exam. This score is on the 200 to 800 scaled-score scale. Your licensing authority receives your official score within 10 days.

**If you score 500 or higher:** the score report goes to your board, and the board determines whether you have met the requirements for licensure. Independent licensure is approved (assuming you meet all other requirements like supervised hours and jurisprudence exams).

**If you score below 500:** you automatically receive a domain-by-domain performance breakdown showing how you scored on each of the eight knowledge content areas. This is free and only generated for sub-500 scores. Keep this report. It cannot be regenerated, and it is the single most valuable diagnostic tool for planning a retake.

## What This Means For You

Three practical implications.

**One: stop worrying about state-specific cutoffs.** If you are doctoral-level seeking independent licensure, your target is 500 everywhere. Spend that mental energy on actual preparation instead.

**Two: do not be misled by the score scale a board uses to report results.** New York's 75, the 200 to 800 scaled score, the 70 percent rule of thumb, all of these point to the same underlying ASPPB cutoff. The exam is the same. The standard is the same.

**Three: if you are anxious about your state, call your board.** State psychology board contact information is published by ASPPB at asppb.net under Board Contact Information. A five-minute phone call to your specific board confirms your situation in writing. This is faster and more reliable than any blog post, including this one.
