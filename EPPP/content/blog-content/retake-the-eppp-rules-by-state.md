# How Many Times Can You Retake the EPPP? Rules + Pass Rates by Attempt

Short answer: yes, you can retake the EPPP. ASPPB lets you sit four times in any rolling 12-month period. Most jurisdictions will let you keep going after that. So if you failed, you are not done.

But here is the part nobody wants to tell you: the data on retakers is rough. First-time pass rates sit around 78 to 82 percent. By the fourth attempt, that number is below 55. The exam does not get harder. The candidate does not get dumber. Something else breaks.

What breaks is usually the study method. Most people who fail go home, rest for a month, then open the exact same prep book that did not work the first time. They do the same flashcards. They take the same practice quizzes. They walk back into Pearson VUE four months later and miss the cutoff by another 5 points. Then they repeat. This is the loop.

This post is the honest version of the retake conversation. The rules, the data, and what actually changes the trajectory.

## How Many Times Can You Retake the EPPP?

ASPPB sets one hard cap and lets states layer on the rest.

**The ASPPB rule:** You may not take the EPPP more than four times in any rolling 12-month period. That is universal. It does not matter which state board you applied through. If you sat in January, March, and June, you have one attempt left before next January.

**The waiting period:** ASPPB itself does not require a fixed wait between attempts. But in practice, Pearson VUE scheduling and most state boards push you to about 60 days between sittings. Some states require more. None of them, in my reading, require less.

**The lifetime cap:** Most jurisdictions do not impose a lifetime cap on attempts. A handful do, and a handful will require additional remediation (extra coursework, supervision, or a formal retake plan) after a certain number of failures. The only way to know your state's rule is to read your board's regulations directly or call them. Do not trust forum posts. Rules change.

**What this means in plain English:** You have four shots a year. After two fails, most state boards will want to see something different in your application. After three or four, some will require formal remediation. The clock matters more than people think.

## How State Rules Vary

I will not give you a 50-state table here because the data is fragile and rules update without notice. Faking confidence on something this important is not worth it.

What I can tell you is the shape of the variation. Boards generally fall into one of three buckets.

**Bucket 1: Follow ASPPB, no extra rules.** These states accept the 4-in-12-months cap and otherwise let you keep trying. Most states sit here.

**Bucket 2: Add a waiting period or attempt cap.** Some boards require a longer wait (90 days or more) between attempts, or cap lifetime attempts (commonly at 6 or 8), or require you to submit a remediation plan after a certain number of failures.

**Bucket 3: Require a formal retake petition.** A small number of boards require you to apply for permission to retake after each failure, sometimes with documentation of additional preparation or supervision.

Find your state board's psychology licensure page. Search for "EPPP retake" or "examination policy." If you cannot find it in five minutes, call them. Boards answer questions like this. It is their job.

## Pass Rates by Attempt: The Data

This is the table that should change your study plan, not your mood.

| Attempt | Approximate Pass Rate |
|---------|----------------------|
| First | 78 to 82 percent |
| Second | 65 to 72 percent |
| Third | 55 to 65 percent |
| Fourth or later | Below 55 percent |

Look at that drop. From first to fourth attempt, your odds roughly halve. The exam is the same. The candidate is, on paper, the same. What changed?

A few things compound:

**Demoralization.** Failing the EPPP is brutal. Your supervisor knows. Your cohort knows. You are running out of money. You walk into the next attempt with cognitive load that did not exist the first time.

**Same broken method.** Most retakers re-buy the same prep program and grind harder. Harder is not different. If your study method missed 50 questions the first time, it will miss roughly the same 50 the second time, plus new ones because you forgot what you already knew.

**Test anxiety stacks.** First fail teaches your nervous system that this exam is dangerous. Second fail confirms it. By attempt three, your amygdala is running the show before you finish reading the first vignette.

**Burnout.** People do not sleep, exercise, or rest between attempts. They study harder, eat worse, and lose hours of cognitive function on test day to something they did to themselves.

If you are reading this after a fail, none of this is a verdict on you. It is a description of a system. The system can be changed.

## How Long Should You Wait Before Retaking?

The ASPPB rule lets you sit again as soon as Pearson VUE has an open slot, often around 60 days later. Most candidates I have talked to who passed on attempt two waited longer than that, usually 3 to 4 months.

The reason is not study time. It is recovery. You need enough distance from the fail to walk into the next attempt without the previous fail running in the background. If you can sit again in 8 weeks and feel ready, do it. If you cannot, do not force it.

The only wrong answer is sitting again with the same plan that failed.

If you have failed twice using a major commercial prep program, the program is not the answer. ## What If You Already Failed Three or Four Times?

This is rare, but it happens. If you are here, two things are true.

First, your state board may require remediation, additional supervision, or a formal retake plan before your next attempt. Read your board's policy. Get the rules in writing.

Second, the strategy has to change in a way it has not yet. At this point, I usually recommend candidates work with a coach in addition to an adaptive prep platform. Not because the content is harder, but because the test-day execution is the bottleneck, not the knowledge. A coach can spot what you cannot see in yourself.

You can still pass. The data on attempt four and beyond looks scary, but it is averaged across people who kept doing the same thing. People who change the system pass.
