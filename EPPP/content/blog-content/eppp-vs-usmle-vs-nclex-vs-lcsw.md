# EPPP vs USMLE vs NCLEX vs LCSW: Which Licensing Exam is Hardest?

Every clinical field has its rite of passage. Doctors get the USMLE. Nurses get the NCLEX. Social workers get the ASWB. Psychologists get the EPPP. Each one decides whether you can practice. Each one inspires the same Reddit posts at 2 a.m. about whether the exam is actually fair.

If you are a psychology grad student about to sit for the EPPP and somebody told you it is "basically the medical boards for psychologists," that is half right. The stakes feel similar. The exam structure does not. The USMLE is a different animal in scale and difficulty. The EPPP has its own traps that the other exams do not, and pretending otherwise does no one any favors.

This is the side-by-side. Pass rates, cost, format, study hours, and what is actually hard about each one. No ranking war. Just the data, the differences, and an honest read on where the EPPP sits in this lineup.

## The Cheat Sheet

| | EPPP (Part 1) | USMLE Step 1 | NCLEX-RN | ASWB Clinical (LCSW) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Profession** | Psychologist | Physician | Registered Nurse | Clinical Social Worker |
| **First-time pass rate** | 78 to 82 percent | ~92.8 percent (US MD) | ~86.7 percent (US-educated) | ~75 percent |
| **Scoring** | Scaled, 500 passing | Pass/fail (since 2022) | Pass/fail (CAT) | Scaled, ~90 to 107 correct of 150 |
| **Number of questions** | 225 | 280 | 75 to 145 (adaptive) | 170 (150 scored) |
| **Time limit** | 4 hr 15 min | 8 hours | Up to 5 hours | 4 hours |
| **Format** | Multiple choice | Multiple choice | Adaptive, mixed formats | Multiple choice |
| **Exam fee** | $600 + $91.88 sitting | $695 (+$210 international) | $200 | $260 |
| **Typical study hours** | 200 to 400 | 400 to 800+ | 100 to 200 | 100 to 150 |
| **When you take it** | After doctorate | Med school year 2 | After nursing program | After MSW + supervised hours |

A few takeaways jump off this table before we get into the weeds.

**USMLE Step 1 is the longest exam by hours and the most study-intensive.** Eight hours of testing. Med students routinely study 400 to 800 hours, full-time, for one to three months. Nothing else on this list compares.

**ASWB Clinical has the lowest first-time pass rate.** Around 75 percent. That is a hair below the EPPP's 78 to 82 percent.

**NCLEX is the shortest and cheapest, with the most format variation.** Computer adaptive testing means it can end in 75 questions or run to 145. That sounds easier. It is not. The CAT is calibrating to your actual ability the whole time, so you spend the entire exam at the edge of what you can do.

**The EPPP sits in the middle.** Not the hardest. Not the cheapest. Has its own specific challenges that the others do not.

## EPPP (Part 1): The Breadth Exam

**Pass rate:** 78 to 82 percent first-time.
**Cost:** $600 ASPPB fee + $91.88 Pearson VUE sitting fee per attempt. Both nonrefundable.
**Format:** 225 multiple-choice questions, 4 hours and 15 minutes.
**Scoring:** Scaled score of 200 to 800. Passing is 500.

**What makes it hard:**

The EPPP tests eight content domains spanning your entire doctoral education. Biological bases of behavior, cognitive-affective bases, social and cultural bases, lifespan development, assessment and diagnosis, treatment and intervention, research methods and statistics, ethics and professional issues. Most of these are subjects you studied years ago and have not thought about since.

The exam format is application-based, not recall. You will not be asked "What is operant conditioning?" You will be asked which intervention a behavior therapist would recommend for a specific client described in a three-paragraph vignette. Knowing the concept is necessary. Recognizing it inside a scenario, under time pressure, is the actual skill.

Stamina is the third trap. 225 questions in 4 hours and 15 minutes works out to about 68 seconds per question. By question 150, focus deteriorates. Most candidates lose points to fatigue, not to ignorance.

**What makes it manageable:** The content is finite and well-documented. Pass rates are above 75 percent for first-time test takers. Adaptive prep that targets your weakest domains can move scores significantly in 8 to 12 weeks.

## USMLE Step 1: The Endurance Exam

**Pass rate:** ~92.8 percent first-time for US MD students. Lower for international medical graduates (~75 percent first-time, ~54 percent for repeaters).
**Cost:** $695 base, plus $210 international surcharge if testing outside the US or Canada. ECFMG fees apply for IMGs.
**Format:** 280 multiple-choice questions across seven 60-minute blocks. 8 hours total.
**Scoring:** Pass/fail since January 2022.

**What makes it hard:**

Scale. Eight hours in a chair. 280 questions. Most med students study 400 to 800 hours, often full-time during a 6 to 12 week "dedicated study period." Many describe it as the hardest academic experience of their lives.

The breadth is wider than the EPPP's. Anatomy, biochemistry, physiology, pharmacology, pathology, microbiology, immunology, behavioral sciences. Step 1 covers the entire preclinical curriculum.

Question style mixes recall, application, and image-based identification. You will be shown histology slides, EKG strips, and CT scans and asked to make calls. The format penalizes anyone who has not done thousands of practice questions.

**What makes it manageable for US MDs:** US medical schools are highly competitive coming in, and the curriculum aligns directly with Step 1 content. The 92.8 percent first-time pass rate reflects that alignment. For IMGs, the picture is rougher.

**The honest take:** USMLE Step 1 is the hardest exam on this list by raw difficulty and total study burden. Anyone who tells you the EPPP is "harder than the medical boards" has not sat the USMLE.

## NCLEX-RN: The Adaptive Exam

**Pass rate:** 69.1 percent total in 2025. ~86.7 percent for first-time US-educated candidates. ~52.7 percent for repeaters.
**Cost:** $200 to register.
**Format:** Computer adaptive test (CAT). Between 75 and 145 questions. Up to 5 hours.
**Scoring:** Pass/fail. The CAT ends when it has determined your competency level with 95 percent confidence.

**What makes it hard:**

The CAT design is psychologically brutal. Every question is calibrated to the edge of your ability. If you get one right, the next is harder. If you get one wrong, it adjusts. You spend the entire exam feeling like you are failing because the questions are always at your limit. That is by design.

The exam can end abruptly. Some test takers finish in 75 questions and walk out terrified. Others go all 145 and feel just as uncertain. There is no real-time score, no comfort feedback, no idea where you stand until the result posts.

Question formats vary. Standard multiple choice, select-all-that-apply, fill-in-the-blank, ordered response. Each format requires a slightly different mental approach.

**What makes it manageable:** Lower study hour requirement than the EPPP or USMLE. The content is clinically oriented and maps directly to nursing school. First-time US pass rates above 86 percent are solid.

**The honest take:** Difficulty-wise, NCLEX-RN is lower than the EPPP or USMLE on raw content. The CAT format is what makes it psychologically punishing. People often pass and still feel like they failed.

## ASWB Clinical (LCSW): The Most-Failed Exam Per Capita

**Pass rate:** ~75 percent first-time.
**Cost:** $260 to register.
**Format:** 170 multiple-choice questions (150 scored, 20 unscored pretest items). 4 hours.
**Scoring:** Scaled. Passing requires roughly 90 to 107 correct of 150 scored, depending on the exam version.

**What makes it hard:**

The ASWB Clinical exam has the lowest first-time pass rate on this list (~75 percent). Published ASWB data has also documented significant disparities in pass rates across racial and ethnic groups, similar to the EPPP, which has prompted ongoing debate about exam fairness and content bias.

Content tests clinical knowledge across human development, diversity, assessment, intervention, supervision, and ethics. The breadth is closer to the EPPP than to the NCLEX.

The application format is similar to the EPPP's: scenario-based questions where you have to choose the best clinical action, not just the technically correct answer.

**What makes it manageable:** Shorter study window than the EPPP. Lower fee. Content overlaps significantly with MSW coursework, which is more recent for most candidates than doctoral psychology coursework is for EPPP test takers.

**The honest take:** ASWB Clinical is comparable to the EPPP in difficulty per hour of testing. The lower pass rate is the headline number. The pass rate gap between demographic groups is a serious ongoing concern in the social work field, and the exam structure is currently under review.

## Difficulty: An Honest Ranking

Here is my read, based on study hours, pass rates, breadth, and format challenge.

| Rank | Exam | Why |
|------|------|-----|
| 1 | **USMLE Step 1** | Most study hours, longest test, broadest content. Highest stakes for residency match. |
| 2 | **EPPP Part 1** | Wide breadth across 8 domains. Application format. Stamina demand. Lower first-time pass rate than USMLE. |
| 3 | **ASWB Clinical** | Lowest first-time pass rate, but shorter exam and narrower content scope than EPPP or USMLE. |
| 4 | **NCLEX-RN** | Shortest study burden. Highest first-time pass rate for US-educated candidates. CAT format is psychologically hard but content load is lower. |

A few caveats.

This ranking is by raw difficulty. It does not weight career consequence, financial impact, or emotional burden, which all vary by individual.

ASWB's lower pass rate and the EPPP's higher pass rate could swap if you weight pass rate over study hours. Reasonable people will rank these two differently.

NCLEX-RN's CAT format is more psychologically demanding than its pass rate suggests. If you put format difficulty above content difficulty, NCLEX could move up.

USMLE Step 1 going pass/fail in 2022 reduced some pressure but did not change the underlying difficulty.

## Why People Get This Wrong

A few common myths from forums and grad school cohorts:

**"The EPPP is the medical boards of psychology."** Not quite. The USMLE Step 1 is 280 questions over 8 hours after 400 to 800 hours of dedicated study. The EPPP is 225 questions in 4 hours and 15 minutes after 200 to 400 hours. Same shape, different scale.

**"NCLEX is easy because it can end in 75 questions."** A 75-question NCLEX means the CAT had 95 percent confidence in your result, in either direction. Short tests are not easier. They are just decisive.

**"LCSW is the easiest because social work is easier than psychology."** The first-time pass rate is the lowest on this list. The content is comparable to the EPPP in breadth and application format. The "easier profession" framing is wrong and a little condescending.

**"If you can pass the EPPP, you can pass any of these."** Sort of. The EPPP tests breadth across 8 doctoral-level domains. That is a real skill. But it does not prepare you for USMLE-style anatomy or NCLEX-style CAT pressure. Each exam tests its own thing.

## What This Means for You as an EPPP Candidate

If you are about to sit for the EPPP and you have been doom-scrolling comparisons:

The EPPP is not the hardest exam in healthcare. It is also not easy. 1 in 5 first-time candidates fails. That is not a casual number.

The specific traps are breadth (8 domains, much of which you have not touched in years), application format (scenario-based, not recall), and stamina (225 questions in one sitting). Your prep has to address all three.

You do not need 600 hours like a med student. You need roughly 200 to 400 hours of focused, diagnostic-driven prep. The candidates who pass on the first try are not the smartest. They are the ones who measured their gaps early and spent their time where it mattered.
