# Digital EF Assessment Tools by Age Population — Pediatric vs. Geriatric

## Purpose
Companion to [digital-vr-ef-assessment-research.md](./digital-vr-ef-assessment-research.md) and [competitive-ef-assessment-teardown.md](./competitive-ef-assessment-teardown.md). Validated digital executive function tools differ substantially between pediatric and geriatric populations, reflecting distinct cognitive assessment needs and technological considerations for each age group. This file catalogs the specific tools validated for each population so the build roadmap can match the target user.

---

## 1. Validated Tools for Children and Adolescents

### NIH Toolbox for Assessment of Neurological and Behavioral Function
Among the most validated digital tools for pediatric populations. Includes three EF measures:
- **List Sorting Working Memory Test**
- **Dimensional Change Card Sorting Test**
- **Flanker Test of Attention and Inhibition**

These tests display medium-sized correlations with established clinical neuropsychological measures and comparable detection of impairment in children and adolescents tested in outpatient neuropsychology settings.[1]

### Cambridge Neuropsychological Test Automated Battery (CANTAB)
The most frequently used computerized cognitive test battery in children and adolescents, accounting for a substantial portion of studies in a scoping review of 564 peer-reviewed articles.[2] However, **only 13% of studies using computerized cognitive test batteries reported psychometric properties**, indicating a need for more validation work.[2]

### Ice Cream VR Test
Virtual reality tool specifically designed for children and adolescents aged 8–16, with normative data from **821 participants across Spain**.[3] Factor analysis identified three EF domains:
- Planning
- Learning
- Flexibility

Demonstrates suitable internal consistency and validity, though further studies are needed to determine sensitivity and specificity in clinical populations.[3]

### FREE Battery (Free Research Executive Evaluation)
Open-access battery validated for remote, examiner-mediated administration in 9–15 year-olds.[4] Includes tests of:
- **Updating**: 2-Back, Number Memory
- **Inhibition**: Stroop Victoria, Stroop Happy-Sad
- **Switching**: Color Shape, Category Switch

Executive measures did not differ statistically between online and in-person tested participants, with small effect sizes, demonstrating feasibility for remote assessment.[4]

### BADS-C (Behavioral Assessment of the Dysexecutive Syndrome for Children)
Standardized test for ages 8–16 demonstrating external and ecological validity.[5] Exploratory factor analysis yielded a two-factor structure reflecting monitoring and abstract reasoning processes. BADS-C scores showed significant relationships with everyday functioning and differentiated children in upper versus lower quartiles on behavioral measures.[5]

### Tablet-based Trail Making Test
Validated in pediatric populations, though participants tend to perform faster on digital platforms compared to paper versions, necessitating platform-specific normative data.[6][7]

## 2. Validated Tools for Older Adults

### Digital Clock and Recall (DCR) — Linus Health
Demonstrates strong criterion and convergent validity with the Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA) in primary care settings for adults ≥65.[8] Successfully discriminates impairment using traditional MoCA cutoffs, with strong agreement rates and correlations between components, particularly for total scores. Exploratory factor analysis revealed factors comprising memory subtests and information processing/spatial reasoning components.[8]

### SMART (Survey for Memory, Attention, and Reaction Time)
Web-based, self-administered tool validated in community-dwelling older adults, including those with mild cognitive impairment (MCI).[9] Four tasks assessing visual memory, attention/processing speed, and executive functioning.
- Total completion time had the strongest relationship with global cognition (β = -0.47)
- EF subtests showed the strongest relationship with cognitive status
- Test-retest reliability ranged from fair to excellent (ICC = 0.50–0.76)[9]

### Tele-Free-Cog
Telephone-adapted cognitive assessment with strong focus on functional abilities and executive function.[10] At a threshold of 19 (out of 24):
- **87% sensitivity**
- **100% specificity** for detecting dementia

Suitable for remote assessment in older adults.[10]

### Three-Module Digital Assessment Tool
Targets executive function, memory binding, and spatial navigation.[11]
- **72% accuracy** distinguishing MCI from cognitively normal older adults (ages 56–89)
- **AUC = 0.807** (excellent screening power)
- Surpassed the paper-and-pencil MMSE
- Independent validation achieved **93% accuracy** predicting MCI[11]

### Virtual Reality Everyday Assessment Lab (VR-EAL)
Immersive VR neuropsychological battery validated for assessing prospective memory, episodic memory, attention, and executive functions.[12] VR-EAL scores correlated significantly with equivalent paper-and-pencil tests, while participants reported the VR tasks as significantly more ecologically valid and pleasant, with shorter administration time.[12]

### VR-based interventions for MCI
For older adults with MCI, VR interventions show significant improvements:[13]
- Memory (SMD 0.2)
- Attention and information processing speed (SMD 0.25)
- Executive function (SMD 0.22)

Immersive VR had significant impact on improving attention/information processing speed (SMD 0.25) and executive function (SMD 0.25). VR cognitive training resulted in significant improvements in attention and information processing speed (SMD 0.31).[13]

## 3. Cross-Age Considerations

### Virtual reality tools
Demonstrate validity across age groups but with different applications:
- **In children**, VR tools like Ice Cream focus on planning, learning, and flexibility in game-like scenarios[3]
- **In older adults**, VR interventions emphasize cognitive training and dual-task training for MCI populations[14][13]
- Semi-immersive and non-immersive VR appear **more effective than fully immersive VR** for improving cognitive flexibility in older adults with MCI[14]

### Tablet-based platforms
Show promise across ages:
- NIH Toolbox works well in pediatric populations[1]
- TabCAT demonstrates good convergent validity in older adults[15]
- Platform-specific normative data are essential — digital versions often yield faster performance times than paper versions[6][7]

### Psychometric considerations vary by age
- **For children**: only 13% of computerized battery studies report psychometric properties — substantial validation gaps[2]
- **For older adults**: brief instruments (≤5 minutes) typically achieve sensitivity ≥0.75 and specificity ≥0.80 for detecting dementia[16]
- Quality of evidence for digital tools in older adults ranges from moderate to low based on GRADE criteria, with stronger evidence for memory and attention outcomes than for other cognitive domains[13]
