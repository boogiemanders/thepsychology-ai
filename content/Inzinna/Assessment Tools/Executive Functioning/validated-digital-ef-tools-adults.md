# Validated Digital EF Tools for Adults (18–65 and Beyond)

## Purpose
Third file in the EF research series. Covers validated digital executive function tools for working-age adults (18–65), though many studies extend into older adult samples. Catalogs tablet-based batteries, computerized platforms, virtual reality assessments, brief screeners, and consensus-recommended tests with strong psychometric properties across clinical and research settings.

## 1. Tablet-Based Executive Function Batteries

### TabCAT-EXAMINER (Tablet-based Cognitive Assessment Tool – EXAMINER)
Recently validated tablet-based EF battery developed from the NIH EXAMINER.[1] Tested in **2,135 individuals** (mean age 65.58) including controls and participants with neurodegenerative syndromes.
- Composite score demonstrates high test-retest reliability
- Associates in expected directions with independent EF tests, demographics, disease severity, and diagnosis
- Greater baseline disease severity correlates with more rapid longitudinal decline
- Supports use in observational research and clinical trials[1]

### Oxford Cognitive Screen-Plus (OCS-Plus)
Tablet-based screen requiring ~24 minutes, designed to assess domain-general cognition with fine-grained measures of memory and executive function.[2] Validated in **320 neurologically healthy adults** (mean age 62.66).
- Demonstrates predicted convergent and divergent validity
- High internal consistency for most measures
- Strong test-retest reliability
- Available as Android app with automated reporting of executive attention and memory impairments[2]

### Integrated Cognitive Screening Platform (ICSP)
Self-administered, tablet-based tool comprising five cognitive tasks (immediate and delayed memory, attention, sensory perception, and executive function) completed within five minutes.[3] In 126 participants (76 with MCI, 50 controls):
- Reaction times and accuracy in sensory perception and EF tasks achieved high classification performance
- **AUC: 0.915 (training set); 0.821 (validation set)**
- Demonstrates accuracy and scalability for MCI detection[3]

## 2. Computer-Based Batteries

### NIH Toolbox Cognition Battery (NIHTB-CB)
Contains two computer-based EF instruments validated for ages 3–85:[4]
- **Dimensional Change Card Sort** — cognitive flexibility
- **Flanker Task** — inhibitory control and selective attention

In 268 healthy adults (ages 20–85):
- Excellent sensitivity to age-related changes
- Excellent test-retest reliability (N=89 retested after 7–21 days)
- Adequate-to-good convergent and discriminant validity
- Supports use in epidemiologic and clinical studies[4]

### Uniform Data Set v3.0 Executive Function Composite Score (UDS3-EF)
Developed using item-response theory in **3,507 controls** from the National Alzheimer's Coordinating Center dataset.[5]
- Good-to-excellent model fit (CFI = 0.99; RMSEA = 0.057)
- Differentiates across diagnostic groups: controls > MCI > AD-dementia ≈ behavioral variant FTD (P < .001)[5]

### Santé-Cerveau Digital Tool (SCD-T)
Includes validated questionnaires and neuropsychological tests:[6]
- **5 Word Test** — episodic memory
- **Trail Making Test** — executive functions
- **Number Coding Test** adapted from Digit Symbol Substitution — global intellectual efficiency

Tested in 65 elderly controls, 64 patients with neurodegenerative diseases, 20 post-COVID-19 patients:
- Correlation coefficients with standard equivalents: **0.84 verbal memory, -0.60 executive functions, 0.72 global intellectual efficiency**
- Machine learning classifier: **96.8% sensitivity, 90.7% specificity** for detecting cognitive disorders
- Good-to-excellent acceptability even in prodromal and mild dementia[6]

### Number Symbol Coding Task (NSCT)
Brief **90-second** EF task incorporating attention, planning, and set-switching.[7] In 320 patient-caregiver dyads (mean age 75.3):
- Moderate-to-strong correlations with clinical and neuropsychological measures
- Strongest associations with measures containing executive components (Judgment/Problem Solving box of CDR, Decision Making/Problem Solving domain of QDRS, Trail Making B)
- Excellent discrimination (**AUC: 0.866**)
- Optimal cutoff of 36: sensitivity 0.880, specificity 0.759
- Combined with structured interview tools: AUC improved to 0.908[7]

## 3. Virtual Reality-Based Tools

### Virtual Reality Everyday Assessment Lab (VR-EAL)
Immersive VR neuropsychological battery assessing prospective memory, episodic memory, attention, and executive functions.[8] In 41 participants (18 gamers, 23 non-gamers):
- Scores correlated significantly with equivalent paper-and-pencil tests
- Participants rated VR tasks as significantly more ecologically valid and pleasant
- Shorter administration time
- Does not induce cybersickness[8]

### EXIT 360° (Executive-functions Innovative Tool 360°)
Uses 360° videos delivered via mobile-powered VR headset for multidimensional evaluation of executive dysfunctions in everyday contexts.[9][10]
- Engages patients in everyday subtasks within 360° daily life environments
- Clinicians obtain ecologically valid information about planning and problem-solving
- Psychometric calibration shows significant correlations to traditional neuropsychological tests
- ~8-minute completion time[9][10]

### VR-based Stroop Test (VRST)
Simulates a real-life clothing-sorting task involving incongruent word-color stimuli.[11] In 413 older adults (224 healthy controls, 189 with MCI):
- 3D trajectory length: **AUC 0.981**
- Hesitation latency: **AUC 0.967**
- Both effectively differentiated MCI from controls — surpassing the MoCA-K (AUC: 0.962)
- Executive function rather than motor abilities contributed to performance differences[11]

### Immersive Trail Making-VR
Adapts the Color-Trails Test for both large-scale VR (DOME-CTT) and portable head-mounted display (HMD-CTT) systems.[12] In 147 healthy adults across three age groups:
- DOME-CTT: r = 0.58–0.71 with original pencil-and-paper CTT
- HMD-CTT: r = 0.62–0.69
- Test-retest reliability (ICC: 0.60–0.89)
- Good discriminant validity (AUC: 0.70–0.92)
- Captures spatiotemporal characteristics of upper-limb movements
- Trails B shows delayed, more erratic acceleration/deceleration consistent with greater executive demands[12]

### Three VR Environments for Executive Function
Assesses specific EF aspects through immersive scenarios.[13] Performance scores and kinematic measures (speed, duration, distance traveled by hand) correlate both with traditional assessment methods and with each other. Embodied, situated approach offers advantages for cognitive assessment; kinematic tracking provides additional quality dimensions beyond traditional scoring.[13]

## 4. Brief Screening Tools

Four brief screening tools demonstrate appropriate psychometric properties for executive dysfunction in neurodegenerative conditions:[14]

- **Executive Interview-25 (EXIT 25)**
- **Frontal Assessment Battery (FAB)**
- **INECO Frontal Screening (IFS)**
- **FRONTIER Executive Screen (FES)**

Sensitive to executive impairments in several neurodegenerative conditions, though more research is needed regarding normative data, neural correlates, and direct comparisons to determine optimal use cases.[14]

## 5. Consensus-Recommended Tests

The International Society for Vascular Behavioural and Cognitive Disorders consensus harmonized battery for adults includes:[15]

**Executive function measures:**
- Trail Making Test B (3 minutes)
- Digit Span Backward (3 minutes)
- Letter fluency (4 minutes)

**Alternative/supplemental tests:**
- Stroop-Interference
- Category Fluency Switching
- Hayling Sentence Completion
- Color Trails B

Harmonized across major research protocols (NINDS-CSN, UDS3, CUDS); selected for lower language demands and education influence to facilitate assessment across diverse populations.[15]

## 6. Psychometric Considerations

### Reliability
Test-retest reliability for digital EF measures ranges from good to excellent, with composite scores generally outperforming individual tests.[4][1][5][8][12]

### Validity
Digital tools demonstrate moderate-to-strong convergent validity with traditional tests and discriminant validity across diagnostic groups.[2][7][4][6][3][1][5][8][12][11] VR-based tools offer enhanced ecological validity compared to paper-and-pencil tests while maintaining psychometric rigor.[8][13][10][11]

### Efficiency
Digital platforms offer practical advantages including automated scoring, shorter administration times, and enhanced statistical power for detecting change. Some composite scores require **40–60% smaller sample sizes** than individual tests for clinical trials.[7][5][8]
