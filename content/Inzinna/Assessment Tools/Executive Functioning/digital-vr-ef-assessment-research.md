# Digital, VR, and Mobile EF Assessment — Research Evidence

## Purpose
Complement to [competitive-ef-assessment-teardown.md](./competitive-ef-assessment-teardown.md). Where the teardown catalogs what's broken in CPT-3, TOVA, and Pearson, this file catalogs what's validated and working in digital, VR, and mobile EF assessment — the evidence base for what to build.

---

## Summary

Digital and virtual reality-based tools for executive function assessment show promising psychometric properties and offer advantages over traditional paper-and-pencil tests, including enhanced ecological validity, automated scoring, and the ability to capture rich kinematic data.[1][2][3] Research supports the validity and reliability of digital versions of classic tests like the **Continuous Performance Test (CPT), Trail Making Test (TMT), and D-KEFS subtests** (including Tower), though new normative data may be needed for digital platforms.[4][5][6][7]

---

## 1. Digital Versions of Traditional Executive Function Tests

### Trail Making Test (TMT)
Digital and tablet-based versions of the TMT demonstrate significant correlations with paper versions, though participants tend to perform faster on digital platforms.[5][6] A validation study of 108 healthy adults found that digital TMT performance correlated significantly with the paper version and with other executive function tasks measuring inhibition, working memory, and flexibility, though new normative data are recommended.[6] Videoconference-integrated digital TMT better captures the cognitive demands of the written form compared to oral administration, with stronger correlations (r = 0.71) with in-person written TMT-B.[8] Computerized versions also enable detailed analysis of eye and hand movements, revealing internal measures of working memory and potential markers of inhibitory control.[9]

### Continuous Performance Test (CPT)
The CPT-IP (Identical Pairs version) has been successfully adapted for digital administration and shows validity in assessing attention and vigilance.[10] Digital platforms enable more precise measurement of reaction times and error patterns compared to traditional administration.

### D-KEFS Battery
The D-KEFS, which includes subtests like Trail Making, Tower (based on Tower of Hanoi), Twenty Questions, Verbal Fluency, and Color-Word Interference, has been studied in both traditional and virtual reality formats.[4][11][12][13] A study comparing D-KEFS tests with virtual reality tools found that VR-based assessments (such as VR-Stroop) correlated with traditional D-KEFS measures and more accurately reflected everyday behavioral executive function than paper-pencil tasks.[4] The D-KEFS Tower Test demonstrates a multidimensional three-factor structure (first-move-time, excess moves, rule violations), supporting the use of multiple metrics rather than a single score.[11] Digital platforms like Philips IntelliSpace Cognition have demonstrated fair to excellent agreement with analog D-KEFS versions, with measurement invariance supporting equal loadings on underlying cognitive domains.[7]

---

## 2. Virtual Reality and 360° Video-Based Tools

VR-based assessments provide enhanced ecological validity by immersing individuals in complex, real-life scenarios.[1][2][3] A systematic review identified 100 studies demonstrating that VR-based tools are promising for ecological assessment and treatment of executive functions in healthy subjects and clinical populations.[1] The **EXIT 360°** tool uses 360° videos delivered via VR headset to assess executive functions in everyday contexts, completing assessment in approximately 8 minutes with significant correlations to traditional neuropsychological tests.[14] VR environments enable tracking of kinematic information (speed, duration, distance traveled by hand), which correlates with both traditional assessment methods and performance scores, offering additional dimensions for cognitive assessment.[3]

---

## 3. Smartphone and Tablet-Based Platforms

### Mobile platforms
Offer scalable alternatives for executive function assessment. The **Mobile Toolbox (MTB)** includes measures of executive functioning and processing speed (Arrow Matching, Shape-Color Sorting, Number-Symbol Match) that demonstrate high internal consistency, strong test-retest reliability, and moderate correlations with comparable NIH Toolbox measures.[15] Smartphone-based cognitive testing shows strong convergent validity with disease severity, traditional neuropsychological measures, and brain imaging findings, with smartphone app composites outperforming the Montreal Cognitive Assessment in detecting prodromal frontotemporal lobar degeneration.[16]

### Tablet-based assessments
Tools like **TabCAT (Tablet-based Cognitive Assessment Tool)** demonstrate good convergent validity with traditional pen-and-paper tests, with executive function tasks (particularly Match) showing the strongest ability to differentiate clinical groups and predict amyloid PET positivity.[17] The **Philips IntelliSpace Cognition** platform showed fair to excellent relative agreement with analog versions across multiple tests, with absolute equivalence for several measures.[7]

---

## 4. Psychometric Considerations

### Reliability
Test-retest reliability varies by specific measure, with global cognition composites generally showing excellent reliability (ICCs > 0.8), while individual test scores may show more variable reliability.[18][19] Composite scores from D-KEFS timed subtests demonstrate good to excellent internal consistency and test-retest reliability (0.809–0.901 for speed composites), surpassing individual test scores.[13]

### Validity
Digital assessments show moderate to strong convergent validity with traditional tests, though the strength varies by specific measure and cognitive domain.[8][15][7][20][18] Concurrent validity is supported by significant correlations between digital and paper versions, though some studies find that conventional paper-and-pencil norms are not directly applicable to digital versions, with **up to 34% of healthy controls showing abnormal performance when paper norms are applied to digital tests**.[21]

### Practical considerations
Digital assessments are generally feasible and well-accepted by patients, with completion rates around **94% in acquired brain injury populations**.[21] Most participants report enjoyment and find instructions easy to understand, though some experience performance anxiety (38%) or difficulty concentrating (17%) in remote testing environments.[18] Familiarity with tablets does not significantly impact test performance, which is important for neuropsychological assessment.[21] Operating system differences (iOS vs. Android) can affect performance on some measures but do not impact validity or reliability.[15]

---

## 5. Clinical Implementation

### Advantages
- Standardized stimulus presentation
- Automated scoring
- Reduced inter-examiner variability
- Enhanced accessibility for rural and under-resourced communities
- Ability to conduct frequent longitudinal monitoring[19][22]

### Challenges
- Need for platform-specific normative data
- Ensuring robustness across devices and operating systems
- Addressing variability in screen sizes and response interface latencies[6][21][22]
