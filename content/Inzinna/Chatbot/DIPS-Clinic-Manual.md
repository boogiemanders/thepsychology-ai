# Ethical Principles {#ethical-principles}

# 

As a Professional Limited Liability Company (PLLC) practicing **psychology** in New York State, Dr. Inzinna Psychological Services (DIPS) is legally and professionally bound to uphold the highest ethical standards in our work. All clinicians practicing under DIPS are expected to adhere to the Ethical Principles of Psychologists and Code of Conduct established by the American Psychological Association (APA), which set forth both aspirational principles and enforceable standards for competent, respectful, and responsible practice. In addition, we are accountable to the New York State Education Law and the Office of the Professions, which regulate professional licensure and professional conduct. Together, these ethical and legal frameworks ensure that our practice safeguards client welfare, protects confidentiality, maintains professional integrity, and promotes the highest quality of psychological care.

Because our team includes psychologists, social workers, mental health counselors, and marriage and family therapists—sometimes at the pre-licensed level under supervision—clinicians are also expected to uphold the ethical codes of their respective professional organizations (e.g., NASW, ACA, AAMFT) as applicable. When differences arise between codes, clinicians are required to follow whichever standard is higher or more protective of the client.

At DIPS, these obligations are not only external requirements but also embedded in our internal policies for supervision, documentation, informed consent, billing, and risk management. By following both the APA Code of Ethics and their own professional standards, alongside DIPS’s practice policies, clinicians demonstrate a shared commitment to ethical excellence and to building a culture of accountability, transparency, and trust in all therapeutic relationships. The APA’s Code of Ethics can be accessed here: [https://www.apa.org/ethics/code](https://www.apa.org/ethics/code)

# Services Offered {#services-offered}

At Dr. Inzinna Psychological Services (DIPS), we provide comprehensive mental health services to children, adolescents, adults, couples, and families. Our clinicians are trained across a variety of evidence-based and integrative approaches, including cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), dialectical behavior therapy (DBT), psychodynamic therapy, trauma-focused interventions, couples therapy, and family systems approaches. The DIPS Training Manual outlines our Cognitive Behavioral Psychoanalysis CBP – an integrative, transdiagnostic, and process-based psychotherapy approach that accounts for affordability, artificial intelligence, and telehealth; it is a model available to all clinicians trained at DIPS. All services are delivered virtually or in-person either by licensed clinicians or by pre-licensed professionals under the close supervision of licensed psychologists.

### Individual Therapy {#individual-therapy}

We offer psychotherapy tailored to the needs of each client, addressing concerns such as depression, anxiety, trauma, relationship challenges, identity development, grief and loss, life transitions, and stress management. Our work is collaborative and relational, supporting clients in building insight, resilience, and lasting change.

### Couples & Family Therapy {#couples-&-family-therapy}

Couples and families can benefit from therapy to improve communication, resolve conflict, strengthen bonds, and navigate challenges together. Our clinicians draw from Gottman-informed approaches, Emotionally Focused Therapy, and Structural Family Therapy, and Object Relations Couples Therapy, while tailoring treatment to each couple or family’s needs.

### Child & Adolescent Therapy {#child-&-adolescent-therapy}

We provide age-appropriate services for children and teens, including play therapy, CBT for youth, and family-involved interventions. Common concerns include school stress, anxiety, depression, behavioral difficulties, trauma, ADHD, and peer or family conflict. Parent collaboration is an essential part of our child and adolescent services.

### Group Therapy & Skills Training {#group-therapy-&-skills-training}

We periodically offer groups for clients with shared concerns, such as anxiety management, social skills, or DBT-informed coping skills. Groups provide support, connection, and practical strategies within a structured setting.

### Psychological Assessment {#psychological-assessment}

DIPS clinicians conduct psychological, educational, and diagnostic evaluations for children, adolescents, and adults. Assessments may address learning differences, ADHD, emotional functioning, or treatment planning. Our reports are designed to be clear, useful, and actionable for clients, families, and other providers.

### Scope of Care {#scope-of-care}

While we serve a broad range of clients and presenting concerns, we are not able to provide services for individuals who require acute crisis stabilization, are actively suicidal or homicidal, or whose primary concerns involve unmanaged psychosis or substance dependence. In such cases, we will provide referrals to higher levels of care (see Referrals section or go to: [Full Directory of Offices of Mental Health in NYS](https://my.omh.ny.gov/analytics/saw.dll?PortalPages&PortalPath=/shared/Mental%20Health%20Program%20Directory/_portal/Mental%20Health%20Program%20Directory&page=Full%20Directory&Action=Navigate)).

DIPS maintains a robust supervision and support structure, which allows us to provide treatment to individuals presenting with certain higher-risk concerns. Specifically, we are able to accept clients with **non-life-threatening eating disorders, psychotic disorders, self-harm behaviors, and substance use concerns**, provided that:

1. The presenting concerns fall within the **scope of clinical competence** of the treating provider and their licensed supervisor.  
2. Adequate supervision and consultation are available to support ethical, evidence-based treatment.  
3. The client does not present an **imminent risk to self or others** that would require a higher level of care 

### Referral and Exclusion Criteria {#referral-and-exclusion-criteria}

While DIPS strives to provide comprehensive, accessible, and ethical care, certain clinical presentations may exceed our scope of services. In such cases, our priority is to **ensure client safety and direct them to the most appropriate level of care**.

Clients will be referred to an alternative provider, program, or higher level of care if they present with:

* **Imminent risk of harm to self or others** requiring 24-hour monitoring, inpatient, or crisis-level intervention.  
* **Severe, unstable psychotic disorders** (e.g., acute schizophrenia with significant impairment in reality testing or functioning) not manageable within an outpatient setting.  
* **Severe or life-threatening eating disorders** requiring medical stabilization or intensive nutritional monitoring.  
* **Active, severe substance dependence** requiring medical detoxification or inpatient rehabilitation.  
* **Ongoing domestic violence, child abuse/neglect, or other safety concerns** requiring specialized crisis or protective services.  
* **Active legal cases** (e.g., child custody disputes, personal injury, or ongoing litigation) where psychological services may present a conflict of interest.  
* **Cognitive or medical impairments** that require specialized care beyond the training and resources available at DIPS.

In addition, clients may be referred out if their treatment needs **fall outside the scope of competence** of both their provider and available supervisors. DIPS providers and supervisors will collaborate with the client, whenever possible, to facilitate a **warm handoff to appropriate services** and ensure continuity of care.

# HIPAA Privacy and Security Practices {#hipaa-privacy-and-security-practices}

Dr. Inzinna Psychological Services (DIPS) is committed to providing the highest quality of care while maintaining full compliance with the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) and the privacy laws of New York State. All client information obtained in the course of treatment, whether verbal, written, or electronic, is considered *Protected Health Information (PHI)*. This information is safeguarded to ensure confidentiality, promote trust in the therapeutic relationship, and allow for continuity of care.

Each client (or the parent/legal guardian of a minor) receives a copy of our **Notice of Privacy Practices** (which can be viewed on Simple Practice) at the outset of services. This document explains how DIPS may use or disclose PHI, the circumstances under which authorization is required, and the situations in which information may be shared without authorization (e.g., suspected child abuse/neglect, elder abuse, danger to self or others, or as required by law). Clients are asked to sign an **Acknowledgment of Receipt of the Notice of Privacy Practices**, which becomes part of their permanent clinical record. Informed **Consent for Services** is also required prior to the initiation of treatment.

### **HIPAA Security Rule** {#hipaa-security-rule}

In addition to privacy protections, DIPS complies with the [HIPAA Security Rule](https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/security/index.html), which governs the security of electronic protected health information (ePHI). Our clinicians are responsible for taking reasonable steps to ensure that PHI is not accessed, disclosed, or stored inappropriately. These protections apply to all devices, communications, and records.

### **Security Guidelines for Clinicians at DIPS** {#security-guidelines-for-clinicians-at-dips}

* **Secure Platforms Only:** Clinical documentation, telehealth sessions, and communications involving PHI must only be conducted on HIPAA-compliant, secure platforms approved by DIPS.

* **Access Control:** PHI must never be accessed in public or unsecured locations. Clinicians should not leave electronic records open or unattended.

* **No Unauthorized Storage:** PHI may not be stored on personal devices, flash drives, or external storage systems outside of approved secure systems.

* **Professional Boundaries:** Identifiable client information should never be discussed in public spaces, including hallways, elevators, or while using unsecured phones.

* **Confidential Records:** Any written or electronic clinical material must be stored securely. Printed documents containing PHI should be safeguarded and filed promptly; no documents may be left unattended.

* **Recordings:** If audio or video recordings are made for treatment purposes, they must be encrypted and stored within approved secure systems, with explicit written consent from the client.

By following these privacy and security practices, DIPS ensures compliance with federal and state law, while also protecting the integrity of the therapeutic relationship and upholding the ethical standards of the profession.

# **Mandated Reporting** {#mandated-reporting}

It is **mandatory** that you complete [The New York State Office of Children and Family Services FREE 24/7 online Mandated Reporter Training in Child Abuse and Neglect/Maltreatment Identification](https://ocfs.ny.gov/programs/cps/mandated-reporter-training.php) **before** seeing clients.

As a New York State–licensed Professional Limited Liability Company (PLLC), all clinicians working within Dr. Inzinna Psychological Services (DIPS)—including psychologists, mental health counselors, social workers, and marriage and family therapists, whether licensed or pre-licensed under supervision—are designated mandated reporters under New York State law.

This means that if, in the course of professional duties, a clinician has reasonable cause to suspect that a child is being abused or neglected, or that a vulnerable adult (such as an elderly or disabled person) is being abused, neglected, or exploited, the clinician is legally and ethically obligated to make a report to the appropriate state authority (e.g., the New York Statewide Central Register of Child Abuse and Maltreatment or Adult Protective Services).

Failure to comply with mandated reporting requirements can result in legal penalties, professional discipline by the New York State Office of the Professions, and possible consequences for one’s license or permit. DIPS supervisors will provide guidance to pre-licensed staff in navigating reporting obligations, but the legal responsibility ultimately rests with the individual clinician. All incidents of mandated reporting must be reported to Dr. Inzinna, regardless of the status of a clinician’s license. 

Clinicians should:

* Immediately consult their supervisor if they suspect abuse, neglect, or exploitation.  
* Document the concern, discussion, and actions taken in accordance with DIPS record-keeping procedures.  
* Complete the report promptly using the appropriate state reporting hotline or online system.

Mandated reporting is not optional; it is a legal and ethical duty designed to protect vulnerable individuals from harm. DIPS supports clinicians in fulfilling this responsibility and provides supervision, consultation, and training on how to identify and respond to potential abuse or neglect.

# **Confidentiality & Information Sharing** {#confidentiality-&-information-sharing}

At Dr. Inzinna Psychological Services (DIPS), confidentiality is a cornerstone of ethical practice. All staff must comply with HIPAA, New York State law, and professional ethics codes (APA, NASW, NASP, etc., as applicable).

Core Guidelines

* Client Information: All records, notes, and identifying information are confidential. Do not discuss clients outside of supervision, treatment team meetings, or authorized consultation.  
* Storage: All records must be stored in SimplePractice, which meets HIPAA standards for secure electronic health records. Do not download or store PHI on personal devices.  
* Communication:  
  * Email, phone, or text should only be used for administrative scheduling, not for clinical content.  
  * Use only the practice’s HIPAA-compliant systems for telehealth and documentation.  
* Release of Information (ROI): Written consent is required before sharing any information with outside providers, schools, or family members (except in cases covered under mandated reporting, emergencies, or as required by law).  
* Minimum Necessary Rule: When disclosure is required, staff must share only the minimum necessary information to accomplish the purpose.  
* Supervision & Consultation: Supervision is an appropriate forum to discuss cases, but identifying details should be limited, and all conversations should be professional and confidential.  
* Psychotherapists with limited permits must provide clients with the supervisor’s contact information (as outlined in the informed consent).

Breach Procedures

* Any suspected or actual breach of confidentiality must be reported immediately to Dr. Inzinna. This includes misdirected emails, unauthorized access, or lost devices containing PHI.

# **Emergency and Crisis Procedures** {#emergency-and-crisis-procedures}

The safety of our clients is our highest priority. DIPS maintains clear procedures for identifying, responding to, and documenting emergencies in compliance with New York State law and professional ethical standards. **We must confirm the patient's location/address at the beginning of every session.** 

All clinicians are expected to follow the steps below when confronted with an emergency:

### **1\. Immediate Action** {#1.-immediate-action}

* If life-threatening (imminent risk of harm to self or others, medical crisis, active psychosis with safety concerns):  
  * Call **911 immediately**.  
  * Stay with the client or on the call until emergency responders arrive, unless unsafe to do so.  
  * Provide responders with essential clinical information (name, DOB, presenting issue, immediate risk factors).

### **2\. Notify Supervisor and Dr. Inzinna** {#2.-notify-supervisor-and-dr.-inzinna}

* All emergencies must be communicated to the supervising psychologist (for pre-licensed providers) and to Dr. Inzinna (for licensed clinicians).  
* Notification should occur as soon as safely possible, ideally within the same day.

### **3\. Documentation Requirements** {#3.-documentation-requirements}

* Document the incident in SimplePractice within **24 hours**.  
* Note the **sequence of events**, **risk assessment**, **actions taken** (including 911 call, hospital, supervisor notified, family contacted), and **outcome**.  
* For pre-licensed providers, supervisors will review and co-sign emergency documentation.

### **4\. Family/Support Notification** {#4.-family/support-notification}

* When appropriate and with consideration of HIPAA and NYS law, notify the client’s **emergency contact** (listed at intake).  
* If the client is a minor, parents/guardians must be contacted unless doing so would increase risk of harm (consult with supervisor in such cases).

### **5\. Post-Crisis Follow-Up** {#5.-post-crisis-follow-up}

* Check in with the client within **24–48 hours** if possible.  
* Schedule a follow-up session or coordinate a higher level of care (e.g., partial hospitalization, IOP, inpatient).  
* Supervisor/practice lead should review whether additional supports are needed (safety plan update, psychiatry referral, case management).

### **6\. Internal Review** {#6.-internal-review}

* After any emergency, a brief case review should be conducted in supervision or staff meeting. This is to improve system-wide learning and ensure protocol compliance.

### **7\. Ongoing Documentation** {#7.-ongoing-documentation}

All emergencies must be documented in detail in the clinical record, including:

* Risk assessment findings  
* Interventions attempted  
* Referrals made  
* Follow-up plans  
* Notifications to supervisor (if applicable)

TIP: Always close the loop. You are not done until there is a plan for ongoing care.

# **Crisis Protocols (Beyond Emergencies)** {#crisis-protocols-(beyond-emergencies)}

At DIPS, we recognize that clients may experience **acute psychological distress or escalating risk** that does not meet the threshold of an immediate emergency. These situations require clear, consistent protocols to ensure client safety and continuity of care.

**Definition of a Crisis**  
 A crisis may include:

* Intense suicidal ideation without immediate plan or intent.  
* Escalating self-harm urges.  
* Severe anxiety or panic that disrupts functioning.  
* Significant deterioration in functioning (e.g., inability to attend work/school).  
* Interpersonal or family crises where the client requests urgent support.

**Clinician Responsibilities**

1. **Assessment**  
   * Conduct a risk assessment (suicidality, homicidality, self-harm, psychosis, functional impairment).  
   * Document all findings clearly in the client’s chart.  
2. **Intervention**  
   * Provide supportive crisis counseling and stabilization.  
   * Develop or update a **safety plan** with the client (means restriction, coping strategies, crisis contacts).  
     1. Safety Plan Template can be found by going to patient’s chart → click “New” → Click “Other document” →  Select “Safety Plan”  
   * Increase session frequency or extend session length if clinically appropriate.  
   * Consider referral to a higher level of care if the client cannot be stabilized outpatient.  
3. **Between-Session Crisis Calls**  
   * DIPS is **not a 24/7 service**. Clinicians are not required to be available outside of scheduled sessions.  
   * Clients should be reminded of their **personal crisis plan** and given crisis resources at intake (e.g., 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline, local hotlines, ER instructions).  
   * If a client reaches out between sessions and the clinician is available, brief crisis support may be provided. This must be documented in the client record.  
   * If the clinician is unavailable, the client should be directed (via voicemail/email auto-reply) to use emergency and crisis lines.  
4. **Collaboration**  
   * When appropriate, involve family members, supports, or other providers (with client consent) in the crisis plan.  
   * Coordinate with psychiatry if medication support may be warranted.

**Documentation Requirements**  
 Every crisis encounter (in session or between sessions) must include:

* Risk assessment findings.  
* Interventions provided (including safety plan updates).  
* Referrals or crisis resources given.  
* Clinical rationale for continuing outpatient treatment vs. referral.

**Ethical/Legal Guidance**

* If at any point the client is assessed to be at imminent risk of harm, follow **Emergency Procedures** (call 911, arrange hospitalization, or direct client to nearest ER).  
* Clinicians must balance availability with boundaries — it is not ethical to promise 24/7 crisis coverage that the practice cannot provide.

**Crisis Resources for Clients** (provide at intake and in informed consent):

* 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (24/7, nationwide).  
* Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741\.  
* Local county crisis hotlines.  
* Nearest ER or 911 for immediate emergencies.

# **Boundaries in Communication and Availability** {#boundaries-in-communication-and-availability}

We encourage open and supportive communication while maintaining professional boundaries that protect both clients and clinicians.

**Approved Communication Methods**

* **Secure Messaging in Simple Practice** is the preferred method for administrative questions, scheduling, or brief updates.  
* **Phone Calls** may be used for scheduling or urgent (but non-emergency) matters.  
* **Email** is available but secure messaging in Simple Practice is preferred.

**Response Times**

* Clinicians will make every effort to respond to messages within **2–3 business days**.  
* Messages are not monitored continuously; therefore, clients should not use them for emergencies or urgent clinical needs.

**Clinical Boundaries**

* Providers do not provide therapy via text, email, or voicemail. These communications are for scheduling and brief updates only.  
* Clinicians may decline or redirect clinical conversations initiated outside of sessions to protect therapeutic integrity and maintain HIPAA compliance.

**Crisis and Emergency Communications**

* Clients experiencing a **life-threatening emergency** should call 911 or go to the nearest ER.  
* Clients experiencing a **crisis** should follow the **Crisis Protocols** and use 988 or other designated hotlines.

# Supervision and Scope of Practice {#supervision-and-scope-of-practice}

DIPS is a New York State PLLC licensed to practice psychology. As such, all services provided fall within the professional scope of practice defined by the **New York State Education Law** and the regulations of the **Office of the Professions** for each discipline:

* **Psychologists**: Licensed psychologists may independently provide psychotherapy, psychological testing, diagnosis, and treatment planning.

* **Social Workers**: Licensed Clinical Social Workers may practice psychotherapy independently. Licensed Master Social Workers may provide psychotherapy only under supervision of an LCSW, psychologist, or psychiatrist.

* **Mental Health Counselors (LMHCs)**: Licensed providers may independently practice psychotherapy. Pre-licensed (permit) counselors must be supervised.

* **Marriage and Family Therapists (LMFTs)**: Licensed LMFTs may practice independently; permit holders must be supervised.

* **Pre-licensed or permit-holding clinicians** may only engage in activities permitted under their limited permits and always under supervision.

* **Unlicensed trainees/interns** may not provide independent psychotherapy; their clinical activity is strictly under the oversight of a licensed supervisor.

All clinicians must practice within the limits of their **training, competence, and license/permit**. When cases are outside of a clinician’s competence, the clinician must:

* Consult with their supervisor, and  
* Refer out when appropriate.

# **Professional Boundaries and Dual Relationships** {#professional-boundaries-and-dual-relationships}

At DIPS, clinicians must maintain clear, professional boundaries with all clients to protect the therapeutic relationship, prevent conflicts of interest, and uphold ethical and legal standards. The therapeutic relationship is inherently unequal, and any use of that relationship for personal, financial, or social gain is strictly prohibited.

### Dual and Multiple Relationships {#dual-and-multiple-relationships}

* A **dual relationship** occurs when a clinician has both a professional role and another role with a client, former client, or someone closely associated with them. Examples include but are not limited to:  
  * Personal friendships or romantic/sexual relationships  
  * Business or financial relationships  
  * Providing services to close friends, family members, or employees  
* Clinicians should **avoid entering into dual or multiple relationships** that could impair objectivity, competence, or effectiveness, or that risk exploitation or harm to the client.  
* Where unavoidable (e.g., in small communities or overlapping professional roles), clinicians must document the potential conflict, consult with supervisors, and take steps to protect client welfare.

### Prohibited Relationships {#prohibited-relationships}

* **Sexual or romantic relationships** with current clients, or with individuals known to be close relatives, guardians, or significant others of current clients, are prohibited.  
* **Sexual relationships with former clients** are prohibited for at least two years following termination of therapy, and only under rare circumstances that can be clearly demonstrated as non-exploitative (APA Standard 10.08; NYS Office of the Professions considers such conduct presumptively unethical).

* Clinicians may not employ or supervise clients, nor may they engage clients in outside business ventures.

### Gifts and Financial Boundaries {#gifts-and-financial-boundaries}

* Clinicians must not solicit or accept significant gifts, favors, or services from clients, as this may create a conflict of interest or compromise professional judgment.  
* Small tokens (e.g., a thank-you card, nominally valued items) may be accepted only if refusal would cause harm to the therapeutic alliance; such instances should be discussed in supervision.  
* All financial transactions must follow clinic policies and be handled through approved billing systems—clinicians may not accept direct personal payment from clients.

### Supervisory Boundaries {#supervisory-boundaries}

* Supervisors and supervisees must maintain professional boundaries at all times. Romantic or sexual relationships between supervisors and supervisees are prohibited.  
* Supervisors should remain mindful of the dual role of evaluator and mentor, and should ensure transparency, fairness, and documentation in evaluations.

### Social Media and Public Boundaries {#social-media-and-public-boundaries}

* Clinicians may not “friend,” “follow,” or otherwise engage with clients or former clients on personal social media accounts.  
* Any professional online presence must adhere to clinic policies, avoid PHI, and protect therapeutic boundaries.

### Reporting and Consultation {#reporting-and-consultation}

* Clinicians who find themselves in potential dual relationship situations must immediately seek supervision or consultation.  
* Any breaches or concerns about boundary violations must be documented and reported to DIPS leadership.

# **DIPS AI Usage Policy** {#dips-ai-usage-policy}

At DIPS, we strive to enable thoughtful, ethical use of artificial intelligence (AI) tools that improves care and operations **without** compromising client privacy, professional judgment, or legal/ethical duties. This policy complements the APA Ethics Code and New York professional rules for psychologists. [**Mandatory Readings:** Artificial Intelligence and the Field of Psychology](https://www.apa.org/about/policy/statement-artificial-intelligence.pdf?utm_source=chatgpt.com) \+ [Record Keeping Guidelines for Psychologists](https://www.op.nysed.gov/professions/psychology/professional-practice/recordkeeping-guidelines-psychologists)

## **Core Principles** {#core-principles}

* **Ethics first.** AI augments, *never* replaces, clinician judgment and responsibility. Follow APA’s policy statement (Aug 2024\) and APA’s clinical **Ethical Guidance for AI** (June 2025\) (see [Artificial Intelligence and the Field of Psychology](https://www.apa.org/about/policy/statement-artificial-intelligence.pdf?utm_source=chatgpt.com)).

* **Confidentiality & records.** New York OP guidance on telepractice and recordkeeping applies to any technology used in practice—including AI systems and vendors (see [Office of the Professions Guidance](https://www.op.nysed.gov/professions/psychology/professional-practice/telepractice)). 

## **Definitions** {#definitions}

* **AI tool:** Any software that generates text, summaries, predictions, or recommendations (e.g., large language models, note-assistants).

* **PHI:** Protected Health Information under HIPAA (includes any client-identifying data).

* **De-identified data:** Client information stripped of all identifiers so the client cannot reasonably be identified.

## 

## 

## **Permitted Uses (no PHI)** {#permitted-uses-(no-phi)}

Clinicians may use AI tools **only without PHI** for: drafting psychoeducation handouts; brainstorming treatment homework ideas; summarizing de-identified literature or workflows; writing non-client marketing/communications; and supporting their own clinical reasoning by exploring ethical considerations, diagnostic frameworks, or treatment planning options in a de-identified and general manner. In these instances, AI is understood as an adjunct to professional judgment, not a substitute for it. Clinicians remain solely responsible for all clinical decisions and must apply their own expertise, supervision, and ethical standards when interpreting or applying AI-generated material.

To promote accountability and professional development, clinicians are expected to **disclose in supervision** when AI tools have been consulted for clinical reasoning, including ethics, diagnosis, or treatment planning. Supervisors will help evaluate the appropriateness of the tool’s input and ensure that any AI use remains aligned with professional standards. The APA’s recent guidance on artificial intelligence emphasizes transparency, human oversight, and risk mitigation whenever AI is used in the context of patient care.

**Mandatory Reading:** [Ethical Guidance for AI in  the Professional Practice of Health Service Psychology](https://www.apa.org/topics/artificial-intelligence-machine-learning/ethical-guidance-professional-practice.pdf?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

### 

### **AI Use Quick Rules**  {#ai-use-quick-rules}

### Permitted: {#permitted:}

* Use AI **without PHI** for **general tasks**: psychoeducation handouts, treatment homework ideas, policy drafting, summarizing research, or workflow outlines.  
* Use AI to **support your thinking** (e.g., brainstorming ethics, diagnostic frameworks, or treatment options) in a **de-identified, general way**.  
* **Disclose AI use in supervision** if consulted for clinical reasoning (ethics, diagnosis, treatment planning).

  ### Not Permitted: {#not-permitted:}

* Enter **any PHI** into consumer AI tools (names, dates, addresses, unique details).  
* Rely on AI as the **sole basis** for diagnosis, treatment planning, or ethical decisions.  
* Store or copy AI outputs with identifying information in client charts.  
* Assume AI outputs are always correct — they may contain bias, errors, or outdated info.  
  Use AI secretly. Transparency in supervision and, where relevant, with patients is required.

**Remember:** AI can help you think, but it cannot replace your professional judgment, supervision, or ethical obligations.

### **Examples** {#examples}

* ✔️ Researching general guidelines for mandated reporters.  
* ✔️ Summarizing published research into handouts (no PHI).  
* ❌ Diagnosis with symptoms of a patient.  
* ❌ Using PHI to create a treatment plan for a specific patient.

# Risk Management {#risk-management}

### **Overview** {#overview}

Risk management within the DIPS framework is grounded in our ethical commitment to protect clients, clinicians, and the integrity of the therapeutic process. As an integrative, transdiagnostic training model, DIPS recognizes that effective psychotherapy requires both clinical depth and operational safety. Our risk management protocols are designed to ensure that all clinical and administrative practices align with legal, ethical, and professional standards as defined by state licensing boards, professional organizations (APA, NASW, ACA), and our liability insurer.

### **Purpose** {#purpose}

The primary goals of the DIPS Risk Management Program are:

1. To prevent ethical and legal violations by promoting proactive education, supervision, and documentation practices.  
2. To minimize exposure to professional liability by ensuring clinicians adhere to established standards of care.  
3. To ensure prompt, structured response to clinical, ethical, or legal incidents.  
4. To integrate risk management into supervision, case formulation, and program policy.

### **Core Risk Management Principles** {#core-risk-management-principles}

1. **Professional Boundaries:**  
    Clinicians must maintain appropriate therapeutic boundaries at all times, including limits on self-disclosure, dual relationships, and non-therapeutic contact outside of sessions.  
2. **Documentation Standards:**  
    All clinical notes must reflect accurate, timely, and objective records of treatment. Entries should include date, duration, modality, diagnosis, interventions used, risk assessments, and follow-up plans. Supervisors must review and co-sign trainee notes when applicable.  
3. **Informed Consent:**  
    Clients must receive a clear explanation of the therapeutic process, confidentiality limits, fees, telehealth procedures, supervision structure, and crisis protocols. Signed consent must be obtained prior to treatment and revisited whenever significant changes occur in treatment modality or risk status.  
4. **Confidentiality and HIPAA Compliance:**  
    Client information must be stored and transmitted in compliance with federal and state confidentiality laws. This includes secure use of electronic health records, encrypted telehealth platforms, and restricted data access.  
5. **Risk Assessment and Crisis Management:**  
    All clinicians are trained in suicide, violence, and abuse risk assessment. Immediate consultation with a supervisor is required when risk is identified. Emergency procedures—including mandated reporting and coordination with emergency services—must be followed in accordance with state law.  
6. **Supervision and Consultation:**  
    Supervision is the primary mechanism of risk reduction in training. All trainees must consult with their supervisors regarding boundary crossings, ethical dilemmas, and high-risk cases. Supervisors, in turn, maintain active communication with the program’s Clinical Director and, when indicated, the risk management department of our liability insurer.  
7. **Incident Reporting:**  
    Any critical event—such as client suicide attempt, breach of confidentiality, or boundary concern—must be documented and reported to both the supervisor and Clinical Director within 24 hours. The Clinical Director, in consultation with the Risk Management Services representative, will determine whether to notify the insurer.  
8. **Use of Telehealth:**  
    Clinicians must comply with all telehealth laws and regulations governing the jurisdiction where both client and provider are located. Secure video platforms, informed consent for telehealth, and identity verification are required for all remote sessions.  
9. **Ethical and Legal Consultation:**  
    The practice maintains an open line with our liability carrier’s Risk Management Services for consultation on emerging ethical or legal concerns. Clinicians are encouraged to contact these services—directly or through the Clinical Director—for guidance on issues such as confidentiality, mandated reporting, consent, or record requests.

# Cancellation Policy {#cancellation-policy}

At DIPS, we recognize that unforeseen circumstances may sometimes prevent clients from attending their scheduled appointments. However, consistent attendance is essential for effective treatment and allows us to reserve clinical time appropriately.

**Policy:**

* Clients are expected to provide **at least 48 hours’ advance notice** if they need to cancel or reschedule an appointment.  
* Appointments canceled or missed **under 48 hours’ notice** will be billed  **$75**  
* Insurance companies do not reimburse for canceled or missed sessions. The client is fully financially responsible for payment of this fee.  
* Exceptions may be made at the **clinician’s discretion** in the event of emergencies, sudden illness, or other circumstances beyond the client’s control. Supervisee’s must clear waived cancellation fees with their supervisors. 

**Implementation for Clinicians:**

* Remind clients of the policy during the intake session and ensure it is reviewed as part of the **Informed Consent Form**.  
* When a late cancellation or no-show occurs:  
  * Document the missed session in the clinical record, noting the reason (if provided) and whether the fee was charged or waived.  
  * Generate the charge in SimplePractice as a **“Late Cancellation / No-Show Fee”** rather than as a therapy CPT code, to avoid insurance claim errors.  
    * Be sure to change both the top of the calendar (red) to Late Cancellation/No-show AND the Service Code to Cancellation or No-show (you should see the $75 charge apply)  
* Supervisors may review patterns of repeated late cancellations or no-shows to determine if treatment planning adjustments are necessary (e.g., discussing barriers to attendance, changing session times, or considering referral if attendance remains inconsistent).

This policy supports both **continuity of care** and the clinic’s ability to provide services to all clients by protecting clinicians’ time.

**At Intake:**

This allows us to offer that time to another client who may be waiting. If a session is canceled with less than 48 hours’ notice, or if you miss a session without canceling, we do charge the full session fee. Insurance doesn’t cover missed sessions, so this is billed directly to you. If this becomes a pattern, we can talk about ways to make scheduling easier for you."*

# Clinical Documentation Standards {#clinical-documentation-standards}

Accurate, timely, and complete documentation is essential to providing high-quality care, ensuring continuity of treatment, and meeting professional, ethical, and legal standards. Clinical records at DIPS serve multiple functions: they support effective supervision, facilitate coordination of care, provide a legal account of services rendered, and uphold compliance with New York State and federal regulations (including HIPAA). All documentation must reflect professional judgment, be respectful in tone, and include only information relevant to treatment.

**Minimum Documentation Requirements:**

* **Intake Assessments**: Use Initial Clinical Evaluation template. Completed at the start of treatment, including demographic, clinical, medical, and risk history.  
* **Progress Notes**: Must be completed within **24 hours** of each session. Notes should summarize the session content, clinical impressions, and interventions.  
* **Behavioral Health Treatment Plans**:

  * Patient Chart → Drop down menu “new” → Diagnosis and treatment plan → select same diagnosis or update it → click “save” → select “behavioral health treatment plan”  
  * Created **within the first 2–3 sessions** (or within 30 days of intake, whichever is sooner).  
  * Reviewed and updated **every 90 days** (or sooner if there are significant clinical changes).  
  * Should include clear goals, measurable objectives, interventions, and signatures from both provider and client (when possible).

* **Risk Assessments**: Conducted at intake and updated whenever there are new concerns regarding self-harm, harm to others, or safety.  
* **Discharge Summaries**: Required within **30 days of ending treatment** and should include reason for discharge, treatment progress, and recommendations for follow-up.

**Emergency Contact:** Please make sure each of your clients has an emergency contact with their name, relation to the client, phone number, and address filled out.

**Insurance Compliance Note:** Many insurers require documentation to show *medical necessity*. Treatment plans and notes must clearly connect the diagnosis, goals, and interventions, demonstrating that services are clinically appropriate and necessary. Failure to maintain up-to-date documentation can result in claim denials or audits.

### Progress Notes {#progress-notes}

Regardless of how the notes are written, you must review your note to make sure it is compliant according to the guidelines we present below. **The first note should always use the “Initial Clinical Evaluation” template. All following progress notes should be written as SOAP notes.** These are the items that we've seen insurance companies most commonly flag during audits:

1. Reference the client's current diagnosis, including what information continues to support it or why it has changed

2. Reference the treatment plan, with commentary on progress toward the overall goal  
   1. "Scott's treatment plan lays out a goal to reduce anxiety at work and in his social life/environments. Progress today: regressing."  
3. Indicate whether symptoms have improved, maintained or escalated  
4. Include a risk assessment, and if a risk is identified, a safety plan  
5. Include a mental status exam, with at least 3 categories present

Insurance companies occasionally review documentation, like treatment plans and progress notes, to verify the services they pay for. Keeping compliant notes is the best way to ensure your clients’ claims get processed as smoothly as possible.

10 items most commonly missing from documentation

1.  A statement of progress toward specific goals and objectives with a reference to the treatment plan

   ❌ Tom is making progress toward his goals.  
   ✅ Tom made progress toward his treatment plan goal of managing social anxiety, as shown by two successful public outings where he practiced his new mindfulness and breathing techniques.  
   ✅ Scott's treatment plan goal is to learn three new coping skills for anxiety that work in his social life/environments. Progress today: regressing. Needs new interventions.  
   

2. Location of service, including whether it was in-person or telehealth, and an indication that telehealth sessions were performed using a HIPAA-compliant audio/visual platform  
* ❌ \[The note includes no mention of the location of the service.\]  
  ❌ \[The session details say in-person, but the notes suggest the meeting was virtual.\]  
  ✅ I had a telehealth session with Jane where I was in my private office and Jane was calling in by herself from her bedroom. We used Simple Practice’s HIPAA-compliant audio/visual software.  
*   
3. A complete mental status exam, with at least 3 categories\* present (e.g., affect, appearance, behavior, mood, orientation, speech, thought content, thought process)  
   ❌ Aurora's mental status appeared stable.  
   ✅ Aurora's affect was sad and her speech was flattened, but her thought process showed concrete reasoning and her memory was sound.

   Note: \*Initial Assessment or Intake codes like 90791 and 90792 should include a full mental status exam. Simple Practice’s “Initial Clinical Evaluation” includes the full exam.

4. A description of symptoms that support the diagnosis  
   ❌ Oscar has Generalized Anxiety Disorder.  
   ✅ Oscar's diagnosis of Generalized Anxiety Disorder is supported by the following symptoms: Oscar reports feeling irritable, and constantly finding feelings of worry difficult to control…  
5. Person-centered details like client quotes and specific behavior  
   ❌ Sarah came into the session. She was sad.  
   ✅ Sarah said, "I feel stressed and sad when I wake up." She pointed out that her outfit was 3 days old and her hair was disheveled.  
*   
6. A recommendation for follow-up care and the associated level of service (for psychiatric diagnostic evaluations using billing code 90791\)  
   ❌ \[The documentation provides no details on future sessions or what the level of care should be going forward.\]  
   ✅ Having diagnosed Antoine with Major Depressive Disorder, I have recommended he continue to see me every 2 weeks to assess progress on goals and interventions. We plan to discuss a possible referral for medication intervention.  
*   
7. A problem statement that includes a diagnosis  
   ❌ \[The documentation jumps straight into details of the session without any context on the initial problem or official diagnosis.\]  
   ✅ Alyssa originally sought out therapy following frequent panic attacks \- at work and often in public. After diagnosing her Social Anxiety Disorder, we have been considering some interventions to help her manage public settings.  
*   
8. A risk assessment, and if risk is identified, a corresponding safety plan  
   ❌ \[The client brought up a risk, but there is no risk assessment.\]  
   ❌ \[There is a risk assessment, but no corresponding safety plan.\]  
   ✅ Tom has acknowledged a temptation to engage in self-harm. As a result, we have worked together on a safety plan to avoid further harmful action, with the following steps:...  
   ✅ Patient denies all areas of risk. No contrary clinical indications present.

   

9. A provider signature and credentials  
   ❌ \[Note has handwritten or digital signature, and no comment on the provider's credentials.\]  
   ✅ Note signed by provider /s/ Jane Doe, Licensed Marriage and Family Therapist

10. A CPT code that matches the session time, provided that particular code is billed by time  
    ❌ \[Session start and stop times are 35 minutes apart, but provider bills CPT code 90837 (only for sessions 53+ minutes).\]  
    ✅ \[Session length was 63 minutes, and provider billed CPT code 90837.\]

During the first session, providers will complete a diagnostic evaluation of the client. From this evaluation, the provider develops a [treatment plan](https://help.headway.co/hc/en-us/articles/13204794675476-Keeping-Treatment-Plans-on-Headway-) with specific goals for the client. This plan creates a thread from the initial evaluation through the progress notes, where providers track how well the client is progressing toward these goals over time.

### Differences between progress and psychotherapy notes {#differences-between-progress-and-psychotherapy-notes}

Progress notes communicate the important details of a client’s care and diagnosis and are a key element of a client’s medical records. They include the details of the session including session start and stop time, treatment modalities, symptoms, progress toward goals, and plan. Progress notes are legally required for every session billed to insurance.

Psychotherapy notes are solely for the purpose of the provider who writes them. These notes include details that might help providers remember the specifics of a session, and are not intended to be seen by anyone else. In Simple Practice, you can find a place for Psychotherapy Notes under the progress note section. 


### What to avoid in your progress notes {#what-to-avoid-in-your-progress-notes}

Progress notes are part of your clients' medical records. In accordance with HIPAA, they can request to see them anytime. With this in mind, language in the notes should be culturally sensitive, precise, and free of moral judgment. Avoid phrases like "I think" or "it seems" as well as absolutes like "always" and "never." If you quote a client directly — especially if they use language that is inappropriate for the note — make sure to use quotes around that section. 

### Guide to CPT Codes {#guide-to-cpt-codes}

CPT codes (Current Procedural Terminology) are the **billing codes used by health insurance companies** to describe the services provided to clients. Each psychotherapy session is assigned a CPT code that reflects the **type of service** (e.g., individual, family, crisis) and the **length of time spent** in session. These codes ensure that services are billed accurately, consistently, and in compliance with both **insurance requirements and federal regulations**. At DIPS, providers are responsible for selecting the code that best reflects the session delivered and for maintaining documentation that supports its use.

At DIPS, clinicians most often use the following CPT codes when billing for psychotherapy services. Providers must ensure that documentation matches the **service delivered** and meets insurance requirements.

Initial Intake / Diagnostic Evaluation

* **90791** – Psychiatric diagnostic evaluation (no medical services)  
  * Used for **intake sessions** when evaluating a new client.  
  * Includes history, mental status exam, and treatment planning.  
  * No medical evaluation (labs, prescribing) is included.

Individual Psychotherapy

* **90832** – Psychotherapy, 16–37 minutes  
* **90834** – Psychotherapy, 38–52 minutes  
* **90837** – Psychotherapy, 53+ minutes (often scheduled as a 55-minute session at DIPS; requires justification of medical necessity for weekly use)

Couples & Family Therapy

* **90846** – Family therapy without client present  
* **90847** – Family therapy with client present (often used for **couples therapy**)  
  * Document clearly whether therapy is **focused on the relationship/family system** versus **an individual client with family present**.

Crisis Psychotherapy

* **90839** – Psychotherapy for crisis, first 30–74 minutes  
* **90840** – Psychotherapy for crisis, each additional 30 minutes

We find that clients tend to benefit from the longer 60 min session (53+ min), which we usually schedule as a 55 minute time slot to give a break between patients. We find that this 55 minute format gives more time to manage the often complex and multifaceted problems our clients face. (Hint: Use Notetaker in Simple Practice to cut down on time writing notes in between sessions). Since the standard therapy time is 45 min (38-52 min), we need to justify the use of more time. Use your clinical judgement to create a statement that justifies the use of a 60 minute session. For example, you might say, “Client needed additional time to access emotional content to avoid the possible need for a higher level of care due to increased emotional dysregulation. Given no changes to the client’s clinical presentation, weekly treatment is warranted in order to address and reduce symptoms.” This kind of language should also be used in treatment plans. 

# Termination and Transfer of Care {#termination-and-transfer-of-care}

At DIPS, termination and transfer of care are considered clinical interventions and must be conducted ethically, with attention to client safety and continuity of treatment.

**Reasons for Termination**

* Achievement of treatment goals.  
* Client decision to discontinue care.  
* Clinical judgment that treatment is no longer beneficial or appropriate.  
* Non-adherence to treatment (e.g., repeated no-shows, failure to pay, refusal to engage).  
  * After 3 documented outreach attempts with no response, message can be sent via email or through Simple practice that we will close the case  
    * Note the date that the case will be closed and that they can reapply  
  * Tip: If client chronically reschedules, you can tell them “We need to have a session by this date or I will need to close your case.” Discuss further with your supervisor.   
* Clinician is leaving the practice or changing roles.

**1\. Planned Termination**

* Clinicians are expected to discuss termination well in advance when treatment goals are met or therapy is no longer indicated.  
* Termination should be processed in-session, allowing clients space to reflect, integrate progress, and plan for continued growth.  
* Clinicians should provide at least **two sessions of advance notice** before ending treatment, unless safety concerns require immediate referral.

**2\. Clinician-Initiated Termination**

Clinicians may initiate termination when:

* The client’s needs fall outside the clinician’s competence or scope of practice.  
* The client requires a higher level of care than DIPS can provide.  
* The client is noncompliant with treatment expectations, repeatedly misses/cancels sessions, or engages in behavior that compromises therapeutic work.  
* When clinicians initiate termination, they must:  
  * Provide reasonable notice (typically 2–4 weeks).  
  * Offer appropriate referrals to other providers or higher levels of care.

**3\. Documentation** 

Document all efforts to facilitate continuity of care.

* A **discharge summary note** must be completed in the client’s chart  
  * You can find this note by going to the patient’s chart → click “New” → click “Other document” → Select “Discharge Summary Note”  
  * Reason for termination.  
  * Progress made toward treatment goals.  
  * Referrals provided or next steps.  
  * Risk assessment at time of termination.

**4\. Transfer of Care**

* If a client requires a higher level of care (e.g., hospitalization, IOP, residential treatment) or a different type of provider (e.g., psychiatry, specialized therapy), the clinician must:  
  * Discuss clinical rationale with the client.  
  * Provide at least **three appropriate referral options**, documented in the chart.  
  * Coordinate directly with the receiving provider, when possible, with a signed release of information.  
* For internal transfers (to another DIPS provider), the clinician must:  
  * Consult with Dr. Inzinna.  
  * Ensure continuity of documentation (treatment plan, progress notes).  
  * Facilitate a warm handoff whenever possible (e.g., joint session, case summary shared).

**5\. Emergencies & Abrupt Terminations**

* If treatment must end suddenly (e.g., clinician illness, leave, or resignation), DIPS leadership will coordinate interim coverage and assist clients with continuity of care.  
* Clients should not be left without treatment options. Supervisors and administrative staff will maintain a referral list and assist with transitions.

**Ethical & Legal Considerations**

* Abrupt termination is prohibited except when the client is engaging in dangerous or threatening behavior toward the clinician.  
* Clients may not be terminated solely due to inability to pay without reasonable referral assistance and documentation of alternatives provided.  
* Clinicians must remain available for reasonable crisis support until the transfer of care is complete.

**Insurance Requirements**

* When terminating or transferring care, clinicians must ensure documentation reflects medical necessity up to the point of termination.  
* Some insurers may request evidence of referrals offered if treatment is ended by the provider.

Please see referral resources at the end of this manual. Dr. Inzinna and supervisors can assist in providing referrals. 

# Psychiatric Consultation {#psychiatric-consultation}

At DIPS, we recognize that many clients may benefit from a combined approach of psychotherapy and psychiatric care. Our practice does not provide prescribing services directly; however, we facilitate access to psychiatric consultation and collaborate with prescribing providers as appropriate.

A psychiatry referral or consultation may be recommended when:

* A client presents with symptoms suggesting that medication evaluation may be clinically beneficial (e.g., severe mood disorder, psychosis, significant anxiety, ADHD, or other conditions where medications are evidence-based treatments).  
* A client is not progressing in treatment as expected, and psychiatric input may help guide treatment planning.  
* A client is experiencing acute safety concerns (e.g., suicidal ideation, mania, or severe psychosis) that require medical intervention.  
* A client is already prescribed psychiatric medication and requires coordination of care.

Referral and Coordination Process

* Clinicians may recommend a psychiatry consultation during treatment planning or at any point during care.  
* With client consent, the clinician may provide referrals to trusted psychiatrists or psychiatric nurse practitioners, either in-network or out-of-network.  
* Clients are informed that they may also consult with their primary care physician about medication needs.  
* With a signed release of information, our clinicians will coordinate directly with the prescribing provider to ensure continuity of care.  
* Documentation of the referral, client decision, and coordination efforts must be entered into the clinical record.

Scope and Limitations

* Psychologists and other non-prescribing clinicians at DIPS cannot provide medication management, advise on medication dosage, or instruct clients to start, stop, or change psychiatric medication.  
* Clinicians may, however, provide psychoeducation about psychiatric medication, help clients track effects and side effects, and support adherence to the treatment plan developed with the prescriber.  
* Any urgent concerns regarding medication (e.g., adverse reactions, suicidal ideation) should be referred immediately to the prescribing provider, the client’s primary care provider, or emergency services (911 or nearest emergency department).

Ethical and Legal Standards

* All referrals to psychiatry must be clinically justified and documented.  
* Coordination must respect client confidentiality and comply with HIPAA, requiring a signed release of information.  
* NYS Office of the Professions and APA Ethics Code emphasize working within one’s scope of competence and collaborating appropriately with medical providers to support client care.

Psychiatry Consultation Referral Protocol

Step 1: Identify the Need

* ☐ Assess whether symptoms, risk level, or treatment progress indicate potential benefit from psychiatric evaluation (e.g., severe mood, psychosis, suicidality, ADHD, lack of progress).  
* Document clinical rationale for recommending psychiatry in your session note.

Step 2: Discuss with Client

* Explain the role of psychiatric consultation and how it may support treatment.  
* Clarify that DIPS clinicians cannot prescribe or manage medication.  
* Provide at least two referral options (e.g., trusted psychiatrists, in-network providers, or primary care physician).

Step 3: Obtain Consent

* If the client agrees, provide referrals and contact information.  
* Obtain a signed **Release of Information (ROI)** if coordination with the prescriber is expected.  
* Document client’s decision (accept/refuse psychiatry referral).

Step 4: Make the Referral

* Share referral information directly with the client (written, secure message, or verbal).  
* If ROI is signed, forward a referral note and relevant clinical information to the prescriber.  
* Note in documentation: name of prescriber, date of referral, and information shared.

Step 5: Coordinate Care

* Follow up with client on whether appointment was scheduled/attended.  
* With ROI, communicate periodically with the prescriber (e.g., medication changes, progress updates).  
* Document all coordination efforts.

Step 6: Manage Emergencies

* If client presents with urgent medication concerns (e.g., severe side effects, suicidal intent, psychotic decompensation):

* Direct client to contact prescriber immediately.  
* If unsafe, advise ER visit or call 911\.  
* Document actions taken.

**Tip 1:** Our Release of Information (ROI) form can be shared with a client in the “Files” section of their chart.

**Tip 2:** Talking to medical providers can be intimidating. Your client has a right to quality care and you have a right to talk to their provider with their permission. Prep for calls by having questions ready. Rely on your supervisor for support.
