## Prompt 1 — Evidence Base & Mechanisms of Action

**Empirical Evidence Base (as of 2026)**

**RCTs and Meta-Analyses vs. Trauma-Focused CBT and Prolonged Exposure:**

EMDR demonstrates **comparable efficacy** to trauma-focused CBT (TF-CBT) and prolonged exposure (PE) for PTSD treatment. A 2020 network meta-analysis of 90 trials (6,560 individuals) found EMDR yielded the largest effect size for PTSD symptom reduction (SMD -2.07; 95% CrI -2.70 to -1.44), followed by TF-CBT (SMD -1.46; 95% CrI -1.87 to -1.05), with sustained effects at 1-4 month follow-up.[1] A 2023 meta-analysis of 157 RCTs (11,565 patients) found **no significant differences** in efficacy between EMDR, TF-CBT, and other trauma-focused interventions, though TF-CBT showed slightly higher dropout rates (RR=1.36) compared to non-trauma-focused interventions.[2] An individual participant data meta-analysis (2024) of 8 RCTs (346 patients) found no significant difference between EMDR and other psychological treatments in reducing PTSD symptoms, achieving response, or attaining remission.[3] A 2025 meta-analysis examining loss of PTSD diagnosis found EMDR showed the highest rates of diagnosis loss at **86%** in non-military/veteran samples, compared to 75% for cognitive therapy and lower rates for other interventions.[4]

**Guideline Recommendations:**

The **2023 VA-DoD Clinical Practice Guideline** provides a **strong recommendation** for three trauma-focused psychotherapies: cognitive processing therapy (CPT), EMDR, and prolonged exposure (PE), with preference for psychotherapy over pharmacotherapy.[5] The **2025 APA Clinical Practice Guideline** gives EMDR a **conditional recommendation** ("suggests" rather than "recommends"), placing it below PE, CPT, and trauma-focused CBT, which received strong recommendations.[6] The **WHO, NICE, and ISTSS** all recommend EMDR as a first-line treatment for PTSD.[7][8]

**Proposed Mechanisms of Action:**

Three primary mechanisms have been proposed with varying empirical support:[9][10]

**Working Memory Taxation:** The **most empirically supported** mechanism. A 2018 systematic review found "reasonable empirical support" for the working memory hypothesis.[10] Laboratory studies show eye movements during distressing memory recall produce strong effects (d=0.74), and therapy studies comparing EMDR with versus without eye movements show moderate superiority for eye movements (d=0.41).[6] However, a 2020 meta-analysis of 10 dismantling studies found **no advantage** for EMDR with eye movements versus exposure without eye movements.[6]

**REM-Like Reconsolidation/Slow Wave Sleep:** A 2017 hypothesis proposes similarity between delta waves during slow wave sleep and those recorded during EMDR bilateral stimulation, suggesting EMDR may facilitate memory reconsolidation similar to sleep-dependent memory processing.[11] This remains largely theoretical with limited direct empirical support.

**Interhemispheric Coordination/Default Mode Network:** A 2018 neurobiological hypothesis suggests bilateral predictive eye movements activate the default mode network (DMN) and cerebellum, facilitating memory retrieval, relaxation, and associative learning.[9] Neuroimaging studies are beginning to provide preliminary evidence, but this remains an emerging area requiring more sophisticated research.[10]

**Evidence Outside PTSD:**

**Depression:** A 2024 meta-analysis of 25 RCTs (1,042 participants) found EMDR effective for depression with a **large effect size** (Hedges' g=0.75), with greater effects in severe cases.[12] A 2021 meta-analysis of 11 RCTs (567 participants) found EMDR superior to non-trauma-focused CBT (d=0.66) and inactive controls (d=1.19) for depression.[13] An RCT found EMDR plus treatment-as-usual superior to treatment-as-usual alone in inpatient settings, with 50% achieving full remission (BDI-II).[14]

**Other Conditions:** A 2021 systematic review of 90 studies found positive effects for EMDR in addictions, somatoform disorders, anxiety disorders, OCD, chronic pain, eating disorders, and personality disorders, though methodological rigor varied.[15][16] Evidence remains preliminary for most non-PTSD applications.[17][15][16]

## Prompt 2 — The 8-Phase EMDR Protocol

**Phase 1: History Taking and Treatment Planning**

**Clinical Task:** Obtain comprehensive trauma history, assess suitability for EMDR, identify target memories for processing, and develop treatment plan.[8][18]

**Duration:** 1-2 sessions typically, longer for complex presentations.

**Decision Points:**

- Determine if patient is appropriate candidate for EMDR
- Identify contraindications (active psychosis, severe dissociation without stabilization, acute suicidality)
- Select initial target memories using three-pronged approach: past events, present triggers, future templates[18]

**Phase Completion:** Treatment plan established with identified target sequence.

**Phase 2: Preparation**

**Clinical Task:** Establish therapeutic alliance, explain EMDR process, teach self-regulation techniques (Safe/Calm Place, Container, Resource Development and Installation).[8][19]

**Duration:** 1-4 sessions; **extended for complex PTSD, dissociative disorders, or affect dysregulation**.[20][21][22]

**Decision Points:**

- Assess patient's ability to tolerate distress
- Determine if additional stabilization needed
- Ensure patient has adequate affect regulation skills

**Phase Completion:** Patient demonstrates ability to self-soothe and return to baseline after accessing distressing material.

**Phase 3: Assessment**

**Clinical Task:** Access target memory and identify its components:[8][18]

- Image representing worst part of memory
- **Negative Cognition (NC):** Maladaptive self-belief (e.g., "I am powerless," "I am in danger")
- **Positive Cognition (PC):** Desired adaptive belief (e.g., "I am in control now," "I am safe now")
- **Validity of Cognition (VOC):** Rate PC from 1 (completely false) to 7 (completely true)
- **Emotions** associated with memory
- **Subjective Units of Disturbance (SUDS):** Rate distress from 0 (none) to 10 (worst imaginable)
- **Body sensations** where distress is felt

**Duration:** 10-15 minutes per target.

**Phase Completion:** All components identified and baseline ratings obtained.

**Phase 4: Desensitization**

**Clinical Task:** Process traumatic memory using bilateral stimulation (typically eye movements) while patient holds memory in awareness.[8][18]

**Procedure:**

- Patient focuses on image, NC, emotions, and body sensations
- Therapist initiates bilateral stimulation (sets of 24-36 eye movements typically)
- After each set, therapist asks "What do you notice now?" or "What comes up?"
- Patient reports whatever emerges; therapist says "Go with that" and continues bilateral stimulation
- Continue until SUDS ≤ 1 or processing plateaus

**Duration:** Variable; 30-60 minutes typical, may require multiple sessions.

**Decision Points:**

- Monitor for abreactions (manage with grounding, slower processing)
- Assess for blocking beliefs or feeder memories
- Determine if additional targets need addressing

**Phase Completion:** SUDS ≤ 1 when accessing target memory.

**Phase 5: Installation**

**Clinical Task:** Strengthen positive cognition and link it to target memory.[8][18]

**Procedure:**

- Patient holds target memory with PC
- Bilateral stimulation applied in sets
- Continue until VOC = 7 (or ecologically valid maximum)

**Duration:** 10-15 minutes.

**Phase Completion:** VOC = 6-7.

**Phase 6: Body Scan**

**Clinical Task:** Identify and process residual somatic disturbance.[8][18]

**Procedure:**

- Patient holds memory and PC while mentally scanning body from head to toe
- If tension/discomfort noted, apply bilateral stimulation until body sensations clear
- If new distress emerges, return to Phase 4

**Duration:** 5-10 minutes.

**Phase Completion:** No residual body tension when holding memory and PC.

**Phase 7: Closure**

**Clinical Task:** Return patient to equilibrium, provide psychoeducation about between-session processing.[8][18]

**Procedure:**

- Use relaxation techniques (Safe Place, guided imagery)
- Explain that processing may continue between sessions
- Instruct patient to keep log of disturbances, dreams, memories
- Ensure patient is stable before leaving

**Duration:** 5-10 minutes.

**Phase Completion:** Patient calm and grounded.

**Phase 8: Re-evaluation**

**Clinical Task:** Assess treatment effects, patient stability, and identify next targets.[8][18]

**Procedure:**

- Review between-session experiences
- Re-assess previously processed targets (should remain at SUDS 0-1, VOC 7)
- Identify new targets or aspects requiring processing
- Assess present triggers and future templates

**Duration:** Beginning of each subsequent session.

**Phase Completion:** All targets processed, present triggers desensitized, future templates installed.

**Standard Protocol Script Elements:**

- "Bring up the picture (or worst part)"
- "What words go best with that picture that express your negative belief about yourself now?"
- "When you bring up that picture/incident, what would you like to believe about yourself now?"
- "When you think of the incident, how true do those words [PC] feel from 1-7?"
- "When you bring up that picture and those words [NC], what emotion do you feel now?"
- "On a scale of 0-10, where 0 is no disturbance and 10 is the highest disturbance, how disturbing does it feel now?"
- "Where do you feel it in your body?"
- After each set: "What do you notice now?" or "What comes up for you?"
- "Go with that" (to continue processing)

## Prompt 3 — Assessment & Target Selection

**Adaptive Information Processing (AIP) Model**

The AIP model, developed by Francine Shapiro, posits that **psychopathology results from inadequately processed memories** of adverse experiences stored in dysfunctional neural networks containing disturbing feelings, thoughts, beliefs, and physical sensations.[18][23] EMDR facilitates accessing these traumatic memory networks, enhancing information processing and forging new associations between traumatic memories and adaptive information, resulting in cognitive reformulation, emotional desensitization, and physiological relief.[18][24]

**Target Identification and Sequencing**

**Three-Pronged Approach:**[18]

1. **Past:** Disturbing memories contributing to present dysfunction
2. **Present:** Current triggers that elicit disturbance
3. **Future:** Templates for adaptive future coping

**Touchstone Memory Concept:**

The "touchstone" or "index" memory is the **earliest, most representative, or most emotionally charged memory** in a thematic cluster. Processing the touchstone often leads to generalization effects across related memories in the same network.[23] Therapists identify touchstones through:

**Floatback Technique:** Ask patient to notice current distressing emotion/sensation, then "float back" to earliest memory with similar feeling. This identifies foundational memories.

**Affect Scan:** Systematically review life periods (early childhood, school years, adolescence, adulthood) to identify disturbing memories and thematic patterns.

**Target Sequencing Strategy:**

1. Begin with most accessible/least overwhelming memory (build mastery)
2. OR begin with touchstone/earliest memory (address foundation)
3. Process chronologically within theme
4. Address present triggers
5. Install future templates

**Negative Cognitions (NC) — Standard List:**

**Responsibility/Defectiveness:**

- I am not good enough
- I am worthless/inadequate
- I am shameful/bad
- I am a disappointment
- I am permanently damaged
- I deserve only bad things
- I am different (don't belong)

**Safety/Vulnerability:**

- I am in danger
- I cannot trust anyone
- I am not safe
- I cannot protect myself

**Control/Choicelessness:**

- I am powerless/helpless
- I am out of control
- I am weak
- I cannot succeed
- I cannot stand up for myself
- I cannot get what I want
- I have to be perfect (to be accepted)

**Positive Cognitions (PC) — Standard List:**

**Responsibility/Defectiveness:**

- I am good enough (as I am)
- I am worthy/valuable
- I am honorable/lovable
- I am fine as I am
- I can have (learn) ___
- I deserve good things
- I am okay just the way I am
- I belong/I am acceptable

**Safety/Vulnerability:**

- I am safe now
- I can trust (myself/select others)
- It's over; I am safe now
- I can (learn to) protect myself

**Control/Choicelessness:**

- I have power/control now
- I am in control
- I am strong/capable
- I can succeed
- I can make my needs known
- I can get what I want
- I can be myself (make mistakes) and still be accepted

**Validity of Cognition (VOC) Scale:**

**1 = Completely false** → **7 = Completely true**

Measures how true the positive cognition **feels** (not intellectually, but emotionally/viscerally) when holding the target memory. Initial VOC typically 1-3; goal is 6-7 (or ecologically valid maximum—e.g., "I am completely safe" may only reach VOC 5-6 realistically).

**Subjective Units of Disturbance (SUDS) Scale:**

**0 = No disturbance** → **10 = Worst disturbance imaginable**

Measures current emotional distress when accessing target memory. Goal is SUDS 0-1 before moving to Installation phase.

## Prompt 4 — Bilateral Stimulation Parameters

**Modality Comparison: Eye Movements vs. Taps vs. Tones**

**Eye Movements:** The **original and most studied** form of bilateral stimulation. A 2012 RCT with 12 PTSD patients found **eye movements superior to tones** in reducing memory vividness and emotionality in first EMDR session.[25] The study strongly supported the hypothesis that eye movements outperform tones, while it remained unclear if tones add benefit beyond recall alone.[25]

**Tones (Auditory):** A 2006 pilot RCT (21 patients, single-event PTSD) found all three stimulation types (alternating bilateral, simultaneous bilateral, continuous bilateral) produced clinically significant SUDS reductions, but **alternating stimulation resulted in faster reductions** when starting with new target memories.[26] However, the 2012 study found tones **inferior to eye movements**.[25]

**Taps (Tactile):** Less studied than eye movements or tones. The 2006 study used vibrations/taps and found clinical effects, though alternating stimulation was superior.[26]

**Dismantling Studies:**

A 2013 meta-analysis found that for **11 laboratory studies**, eye movements showed strong superiority over no eye movements (d=0.74). For **15 therapy studies** comparing EMDR with versus without eye movements, eye movements were moderately more effective (d=0.41), with larger effects when therapists adhered to treatment manual.[6] However, a **2020 meta-analysis** by Cuijpers et al. of 10 dismantling studies found **no advantage** for EMDR with eye movements versus exposure without eye movements, including 5 PTSD studies.[6] The 2025 APA guideline notes this inconsistency, concluding that while EMDR may be effective, **it remains unclear whether eye movements themselves are necessary**.[6]

**Parameters:**

**Set Length:** Typically **24-36 bilateral movements** per set, though this varies based on patient response.[8][10]

**Speed:** Approximately **1-2 movements per second**; adjusted to patient comfort and processing needs.[9]

**Number of Sets:** Variable; continue until SUDS decreases or processing plateaus. May require 10-30+ sets per target.

**Required vs. Optional:**

**Required for fidelity:** Bilateral stimulation of some form (eye movements, taps, or tones) during desensitization and installation phases.[27]

**Optional/Flexible:** Specific modality can be adapted to patient preference and clinical presentation. Eye movements appear to have **modest advantage** but are not absolutely required for therapeutic effect.[6][25][26]

## Prompt 5 — Indications, Contraindications, & Stabilization

**Indications:**

**Strong Evidence:**

- PTSD (single-incident and chronic)[1][2][3][5][7]
- Acute stress disorder[5][28]
- Recent traumatic events (within 3 months)[28]
- Depression (particularly with trauma history)[12][13][14]
- Complex PTSD[20][21][22]

**Emerging Evidence:**

- Anxiety disorders[15][16]
- Phobias[15]
- Chronic pain[15][16]
- Substance use disorders[15][16]
- Personality disorders[29][20]
- Bipolar disorder (adjunctive)[16][17]
- OCD[15]

**Contraindications:**

**Absolute:**

- Active psychosis (untreated/unstable)
- Severe dissociative disorders without adequate stabilization
- Acute suicidal crisis requiring immediate intervention
- Severe cognitive impairment preventing engagement

**Relative (require extended Phase 2):**

- Complex PTSD with severe affect dysregulation[20][21][22]
- Dissociative disorders (DID, OSDD)[21]
- Borderline personality disorder[20]
- Severe substance use (active intoxication)
- Unstable medical conditions
- Recent eye surgery or retinal problems (use taps/tones instead)

**Populations Requiring Extended Phase 2 Preparation:**

**Complex PTSD:** Characterized by PTSD symptoms plus disturbances in self-organization (DSO): negative self-concept, emotion dysregulation, interpersonal difficulties.[20][21][22] Requires:

- Extended stabilization (weeks to months)
- Resource Development and Installation (RDI)[19]
- Affect regulation skills training
- Relationship/trust building
- Phased approach may be beneficial[21][22]

**Dissociative Disorders:** Require:

- Screening for dissociation (DES, MID)
- Grounding techniques
- Parts work/ego state therapy integration
- Progressive Approach protocols[30]
- Slower processing pace

**Somatic Vulnerability:**

- Chronic pain patients
- Medically complex patients
- Those with low distress tolerance

**Resourcing Techniques (Phase 2):**

**Safe/Calm Place:**[8]

- Patient identifies real or imagined place of safety/calm
- Elaborate sensory details (visual, auditory, tactile, olfactory)
- Identify positive emotions and body sensations
- Apply bilateral stimulation to strengthen resource
- Practice accessing between sessions
- Use for grounding during/between sessions

**Container:**[19]

- Visualize secure container (box, vault, safe)
- Place disturbing material "in container" between sessions
- Reinforces sense of control over intrusive memories
- Bilateral stimulation to strengthen

**Resource Development and Installation (RDI):**[19]

- Identify positive memories, relationships, qualities, metaphors
- Elaborate sensory and emotional components
- Apply bilateral stimulation to strengthen and "install"
- Build library of resources before trauma processing
- Particularly important for Complex PTSD/DESNOS
- 2002 case studies showed preliminary efficacy[19]

**Reported Adverse Events:**

A **2025 systematic review** of 51 RCTs found that **only 9 studies (18%) mentioned adverse effects**, with just one employing systematic assessment.[31] In the 5 studies reporting adverse events, effects were typically **mild and temporary** (increased distress, emotional activation, headaches, fatigue).[31] The review concluded that **adverse effects are underreported** in EMDR research, complicating risk-benefit assessment.[31] A 2024 review noted EMDR is "generally safe" but called for standardized adverse effects monitoring.[7]

**Abreaction Management:**

**Abreaction:** Intense emotional/physiological response during processing.

**Management Procedures:**

- **Slow processing:** Shorter sets, more frequent check-ins
- **Grounding:** Return to Safe Place, orient to present
- **Cognitive interweave:** Therapist introduces adaptive information if processing stalls
- **Titration:** Process small "doses" of memory
- **Fractionation:** Break memory into smaller components
- **Resource installation:** Strengthen resources before continuing
- **Session extension:** Allow time for stabilization before ending

## Prompt 6 — Protocol Variants for Complex Presentations

**Recent Traumatic Episode Protocol (R-TEP):**

**Indication:** Recent trauma (within 3 months)[28]

**Key Features:**

- Abbreviated protocol for acute trauma
- Minimal verbalization required
- Can be delivered in 1-3 sessions
- Processes recent memory before consolidation

**Evidence:** A 2024 meta-analysis of 11 RCTs found beneficial effects of early EMDR interventions (including R-TEP) on post-traumatic symptoms at post-treatment and 3-month follow-up.[28] Appears safe and effective for early intervention.[28]

**Group Traumatic Episode Protocol (G-TEP):**

**Indication:** Group treatment for shared or individual traumas[32]

**Key Features:**

- Resource-based group intervention
- Structured worksheet guides self-administered bilateral stimulation
- Patients process targets simultaneously in group
- 3-4 sessions typical

**Evidence:** A 2025 study (Study A: 44 outpatients; Study B: 23 inpatients) found significant reductions in PTSD, depression, and general symptoms with long-lasting effects.[32] G-TEP effectively alleviates symptoms, can be stand-alone or integrated into multimodal treatment, and prevents chronification.[32] **Empirical support emerging.**

**EMDR 2.0:**

**Indication:** Adaptation to enhance efficiency[33]

**Key Features:**

- Modifications to standard protocol
- Specific adaptations not fully detailed in literature

**Evidence:** Currently being studied in ENHANCE RCT (130 patients with PTSD) comparing EMDR, EMDR 2.0, and Flash Technique.[33] **No published efficacy data yet** as of 2026.

**Progressive Approach (Gonzalez & Mosquera):**

**Indication:** Complex trauma, dissociative disorders, severe early interpersonal trauma[30]

**Key Features:**

- Integrates structural dissociation theory with AIP model
- Extended stabilization phase
- Group therapy format option
- Combines psychoeducation on emotion regulation, dissociation, and trauma with EMDR procedures

**Evidence:** A 2017 case-control study compared treatment-as-usual (TAU: psychoeducation only) versus TAU+EMDR in severely traumatized patients.[30] TAU+EMDR group showed significant improvements in 4/5 variables (general health, satisfaction, subjective well-being, session usefulness) versus 2/5 in TAU group. **100% of TAU+EMDR patients improved** versus 70.6% in TAU group.[30] **Preliminary evidence supports use in complex/dissociative populations.**

**Attachment-Focused EMDR (Parnell):**

**Indication:** Attachment trauma, relational trauma, developmental trauma

**Key Features:**

- Emphasizes therapeutic relationship as healing resource
- Addresses attachment wounds and relational patterns
- Integrates attachment theory with EMDR

**Evidence:** **No RCT data identified** in current search. Primarily clinical/theoretical framework.

**Flash Technique:**

**Indication:** Highly distressing memories, patients who cannot tolerate standard EMDR[34][33]

**Key Features:**

- Minimal engagement with traumatic memory
- Patient focuses on positive focus (not trauma) during bilateral stimulation
- Brief "flashes" to trauma
- Designed to reduce distress without full processing

**Evidence:** A 2022 RCT (39 participants, traffic accidents) found Flash Technique superior to mhGAP stress management for anxiety, intrusion, avoidance, total traumatic stress, and psychological quality of life at 1-month follow-up.[34] **First clinical RCT** demonstrated efficacy.[34] Currently being compared to standard EMDR and EMDR 2.0 in ENHANCE trial.[33] **Emerging evidence; promising for highly distressed patients.**

**Summary of Empirical Support:**

**Strong Evidence:** R-TEP (early intervention)

**Emerging Evidence:** G-TEP, Flash Technique, Progressive Approach

**Insufficient Evidence:** EMDR 2.0 (under study), Attachment-Focused EMDR

**Complex/Dissociative Populations:** Progressive Approach has preliminary support; standard EMDR with extended Phase 2 also effective[30][20][21]

**Single-Incident Trauma:** Standard 8-phase protocol, R-TEP for recent trauma

## Prompt 7 — Case Formulation Example

**Case: Single-Incident PTSD from Motor Vehicle Accident**

**Patient:** 32-year-old female, 6 months post-MVA (T-bone collision at intersection). Meets DSM-5 criteria for PTSD. No prior trauma history. Symptoms: intrusive memories of impact, nightmares, hypervigilance while driving, avoidance of intersection, startle response to car horns.

**Phase 1: History & Treatment Planning (Session 1)**

**Trauma History:**

- Index trauma: MVA 6 months ago
- No prior trauma
- Good pre-trauma functioning
- Strong social support

**Target Identification (Three-Pronged):**

**Past:** MVA memory (touchstone—only significant trauma)

**Present Triggers:**

- Driving near accident site
- Hearing car horns
- Riding as passenger

**Future Templates:**

- Driving confidently through intersections
- Responding calmly to unexpected sounds while driving

**Phase 2: Preparation (Session 1-2)**

**Psychoeducation:** EMDR process, AIP model, what to expect

**Resourcing:**

- **Safe Place:** Patient's back porch overlooking garden; sensory details elaborated; bilateral stimulation applied; practiced accessing
- **Container:** Visualized locked box to "store" intrusive memories between sessions

**Assessment:** Patient demonstrates ability to access Safe Place and return to baseline. Ready for processing.

**Phase 3-6: Assessment & Processing (Sessions 3-5)**

**Target 1: MVA Memory (Touchstone)**

**Session 3:**

**Assessment (Phase 3):**

- **Image:** Seeing other car coming toward driver's side
- **NC:** "I am going to die" / "I am not safe"
- **PC:** "I am safe now" / "It's over, I survived"
- **VOC:** 2
- **Emotion:** Terror, helplessness
- **SUDS:** 9
- **Body sensation:** Chest tightness, rapid heartbeat

**Desensitization (Phase 4):**

- Set 1: Patient reports seeing impact, feeling terror
- Set 2: Hears screeching brakes, glass breaking
- Set 3: Remembers pain, confusion
- Set 4: Recalls bystanders helping, ambulance arriving
- Set 5: Remembers being told "You're going to be okay"
- Sets 6-10: Processing continues; SUDS gradually decreases
- End of Session 3: SUDS = 5; incomplete processing

**Closure (Phase 7):** Safe Place exercise; log provided for between-session observations

**Session 4:**

**Re-evaluation (Phase 8):** Patient reports less intense nightmares; SUDS now 4 when accessing memory

**Desensitization continued:**

- Sets 11-18: Further processing; associations to gratitude for survival, recognition of safety measures taken (seatbelt, airbag)
- End of Session 4: SUDS = 1

**Installation (Phase 5):**

- PC: "I am safe now; it's over"
- VOC: 6 → 7 after 3 sets

**Body Scan (Phase 6):** No residual tension

**Closure:** Stable, calm

**Target 2: Present Trigger—Driving Near Accident Site**

**Session 5:**

**Assessment:**

- **Image:** Approaching intersection
- **NC:** "Something bad will happen"
- **PC:** "I can handle this; I am safe"
- **VOC:** 3
- **SUDS:** 7

**Desensitization:** 8 sets; SUDS → 0

**Installation:** VOC → 7

**Body Scan:** Clear

**Target 3: Present Trigger—Hearing Car Horns**

**Session 5 (continued):**

**Assessment:**

- **Image:** Hearing sudden horn
- **NC:** "I'm in danger"
- **PC:** "I am safe; it's just a sound"
- **VOC:** 4
- **SUDS:** 6

**Desensitization:** 6 sets; SUDS → 0

**Installation:** VOC → 7

**Body Scan:** Clear

**Phase 3-6: Future Templates (Session 6)**

**Future Template 1: Driving Confidently Through Intersections**

**Assessment:**

- **Image:** Approaching intersection, checking both ways, proceeding calmly
- **PC:** "I can drive safely and confidently"
- **VOC:** 5
- **SUDS:** 2 (mild anticipatory anxiety)

**Processing:** 4 sets; SUDS → 0; VOC → 7

**Future Template 2: Responding Calmly to Unexpected Sounds**

**Assessment:**

- **Image:** Hearing horn, taking breath, recognizing it's not a threat
- **PC:** "I can stay calm; I am in control"
- **VOC:** 6
- **SUDS:** 1

**Processing:** 2 sets; SUDS → 0; VOC → 7

**Phase 8: Re-evaluation & Termination (Session 7)**

**Re-assessment of All Targets:**

- MVA memory: SUDS = 0, VOC = 7; patient reports memory feels "like something that happened, not something happening"
- Driving near site: SUDS = 0; patient drove through intersection twice without distress
- Car horns: SUDS = 0; no startle response
- Future templates: Confident, calm

**Symptom Assessment:**

- No intrusive memories
- No nightmares
- Driving without avoidance
- Hypervigilance resolved
- No longer meets PTSD criteria

**Treatment Plan:** Discharge with plan to contact if symptoms re-emerge

**Session-by-Session Summary:**

**Session 1:** History, treatment planning, psychoeducation, Safe Place

**Session 2:** Preparation completion, resourcing

**Session 3:** MVA memory processing (partial; SUDS 9→5)

**Session 4:** MVA memory completion (SUDS 5→0; VOC 2→7)

**Session 5:** Present triggers processing (both completed)

**Session 6:** Future templates installation

**Session 7:** Re-evaluation, termination

**Total:** 7 sessions (typical for single-incident PTSD)

## Prompt 8 — Training Pathways & Scope of Practice

**EMDRIA-Approved Training Pathways:**

While the current literature search did not retrieve specific EMDRIA position statements, the available evidence describes the following training structure:[7][27][29]

**Basic Training:**

**Requirements:**

- EMDRIA-approved training program
- Typically 40-50 hours of didactic instruction
- Covers all 8 phases, AIP model, protocols
- Supervised practice during training
- Completion required before independent EMDR practice

**Evidence:** A 2025 RCT noted therapists had "completed an accredited Dutch Basic EMDR training program" before delivering EMDR.[29] Training adherence to protocol is associated with better outcomes.[6][27]

**Consultation:**

**Requirements (based on clinical literature):**

- Post-Basic Training supervised practice
- Case consultation with EMDRIA-approved consultant
- Typically 10-20 hours consultation recommended
- Review of case conceptualizations and session recordings

**Evidence:** The 2025 RCT described monthly group supervision by EMDREA-approved consultants with video review, plus additional supervision on request.[29] Protocol adherence was high (89.5%).[29]

**Certification:**

**Requirements (inferred from literature):**

- Completion of Basic Training
- Completion of consultation hours
- Demonstration of competency
- Ongoing continuing education

**Consultant-in-Training:**

**Requirements:** Advanced training pathway for those seeking to become EMDRIA-approved consultants (full pathway not detailed in retrieved literature).

## Follow-up Q1 — Which Populations Benefit Most from EMDR vs. TF-CBT and PE

Overall, **no patient population has been definitively shown to benefit more from EMDR than from TF-CBT or PE** — meta-analyses consistently find comparable efficacy across these modalities for PTSD.[1][2] However, several population-specific patterns emerge:

**Civilian/non-military populations** appear to derive the greatest benefit from EMDR. A 2025 meta-analysis found EMDR achieved the **highest rate of PTSD diagnosis loss at 86%** in non-military/veteran samples, compared to 75% for cognitive therapy and lower rates for other interventions.[F3] In contrast, the VA-DoD guideline notes that the EMDR evidence base has **no studies in active-duty service members and few in veterans**, limiting conclusions in military populations.[F4] A JAMA review of military-related PTSD RCTs found that meta-analyses of mostly civilian studies show large effects for CPT, PE, and EMDR, with "generally comparable outcomes," but military populations consistently show lower response rates across all modalities.[F2]

**Children and adolescents** may preferentially benefit from EMDR for comorbid anxiety and depression. A 2022 meta-analysis found EMDR reduced **depression symptoms better than CBT** (SMD −2.43, p=0.001) and **anxiety better than CBT** (SMD −3.99, p<0.001).[F5]

**Patients who prefer less verbal processing** may be better suited to EMDR, as it requires less narrative recounting of trauma than PE. EMDR treatment is also generally **shorter in duration with lower burden on patient time** compared to TF-CBT.[F1] The NICE guidelines specifically recommend EMDR for adults with PTSD who have a preference for it over TF-CBT.[F6]

## Follow-up Q2 — EMDR vs. Pharmacotherapy Side Effect Profiles

EMDR and psychotherapies generally have a **fundamentally different and more favorable side effect profile** compared to pharmacological treatments for PTSD. A 2019 JAMA Psychiatry network meta-analysis of 12 RCTs (922 participants) directly comparing psychotherapy and pharmacotherapy found **no difference in acceptability** (dropout rates) between approaches at end of treatment, but psychotherapy showed **superior long-term outcomes** (SMD −0.83; 95% CI −1.59 to −0.07 at last follow-up).[F7]

**EMDR adverse effects** are notably **underreported**. A 2025 systematic review of 51 RCTs found only 9 studies (18%) even mentioned adverse effects, with just one using systematic assessment.[F8] When reported, effects were typically **mild and temporary**: transient emotional distress, headaches, fatigue, vivid dreams, and increased disturbance between sessions.[F8] The review highlighted that this underreporting "complicates interpretation of EMDR's risk-benefit profile."[F8]

**Pharmacotherapy adverse effects** are better characterized. SSRIs (sertraline, paroxetine, fluoxetine) carry well-documented side effects including sexual dysfunction, weight gain, gastrointestinal symptoms, insomnia/somnolence, and discontinuation syndrome.[F9] Paroxetine in particular has higher discontinuation rates due to tolerability issues.[F9] A 2025 pragmatic RCT (700 patients) comparing SSRIs with written exposure therapy found comparable efficacy (within-group effect sizes: 1.00 for SSRIs, 0.83 for psychotherapy), but pharmacotherapy requires ongoing medication use whereas psychotherapy effects are sustained without continued treatment.[F10]

The VA-DoD guideline explicitly states that the benefits of CPT, EMDR, and PE outweigh potential harms, "which were small," and recommends psychotherapy over pharmacotherapy when available.[F4]

> "These results suggest superiority of psychotherapeutic treatments over pharmacological treatments; network, but not pairwise, meta-analyses suggest superiority of combined treatments over pharmacological treatments in improving PTSD symptom severity in the long term."
> — Merz J, et al., JAMA Psychiatry 2019

## Follow-up Q3 — EMDR Outcomes Across Non-PTSD Conditions

There are **notable differences in the strength of evidence and magnitude of effect** for EMDR across depression, phobias, chronic pain, and addiction:

**Depression** has the **strongest evidence** outside PTSD. A 2024 meta-analysis of 25 RCTs (1,042 participants) found a **large effect size** (Hedges' g=0.75), with greater effects in severe cases.[F11] A 2021 meta-analysis of 10 EMDR RCTs showed EMDR was superior to non-trauma-focused CBT (d=0.66) and inactive controls (d=1.19).[F12] An RCT found 50% of inpatients achieved full remission (BDI-II).[F13] EMDR appears particularly effective when depression is linked to identifiable adverse life events.[F14]

**Substance use disorders** show **moderate emerging evidence**. A 2025 meta-analysis of 14 studies found EMDR produced significant effects on craving (g=0.55), PTSD symptoms (g=0.69), depression (g=0.64), and anxiety (g=0.72) in SUD populations, but **no significant effect on addiction severity** (g=0.14).[F15] A 2025 RCT (209 patients with co-occurring SUD-PTSD) found EMDR added to SUD treatment significantly reduced PTSD severity (B=−8.85, p=0.003) compared to SUD treatment alone, **without worsening substance use outcomes**.[F16]

**Chronic pain** shows **promising but preliminary evidence**. A 2025 systematic review of 9 studies (7 RCTs) found all reported significant improvements in pain symptoms, with three RCTs showing large effects and two showing moderate effects.[F17] EMDR also improved associated psychological distress, anxiety, and depression in most studies.[F17] However, EMDR has not been established as routine clinical practice for pain management, and protocols vary considerably across studies.[F18][F17]

**Phobias and anxiety disorders** have the **least robust evidence**. A 2021 systematic review of 90 studies reported positive effects for EMDR in anxiety disorders, but noted that RCTs are scarce and methodological rigor varies.[F19] The available evidence suggests EMDR primarily improves trauma-associated symptoms in these conditions, with only "partial symptomatic improvement" of the primary disorder itself.[F20]
