# Chronic Pain Management: VR, EEG, HRV, ACT, and Broader Psychotherapy

Comprehensive overview of best evidence across five domains in chronic pain management.

## Virtual Reality (VR)

A large meta-analysis of 122 RCTs (9,138 patients) found VR significantly reduced pain (SMD = −0.65), with effects similar across acute, chronic, and procedural pain, and greatest benefit in patients with moderate-to-severe pain and younger age. [1] For chronic pain specifically, the mechanisms differ from acute pain: distraction drives acute pain VR (97.8% of studies), while embodiment-based approaches are more common in chronic pain (54.5%). [2]

A notable advance is VR neuroscience-based therapy (VRNT), which integrates pain neuroscience education into immersive VR. An RCT in chronic back pain showed significant reductions in pain intensity (g = 0.63) and interference (g = 0.84), mediated by reduced kinesiophobia and catastrophizing, with corresponding changes in prefrontal and somatomotor brain connectivity on fMRI. [3] For chronic musculoskeletal pain, both immersive and nonimmersive VR-assisted active training reduce back and neck pain symptoms, though evidence for knee and other regional pain remains inconclusive. [4]

## EEG — Biomarkers and Neurofeedback

Resting-state EEG shows consistent cross-sectional differences in chronic pain: increased theta and beta power compared to healthy controls, with some evidence of decreased alpha-beta power. [5][6][7] However, longitudinal studies have not reliably linked EEG spectral changes to pain relief, though one study found that improvements after interdisciplinary pain therapy were associated with increased global network efficiency at theta frequencies. [8] For neuropathic pain specifically, increased θ-band and high-β power with decreased α-β power has been observed, though pain intensity correlations remain inconsistent. [6]

EEG neurofeedback as a treatment shows promising but still preliminary evidence. A meta-analysis of 9 RCTs found a clinically meaningful short-term effect on pain intensity (low-to-moderate confidence), with protocols typically reinforcing alpha or sensorimotor rhythms and suppressing theta/beta bands. [9] A pilot RCT of infraslow neurofeedback targeting the pregenual anterior cingulate cortex showed 53% of participants achieving clinically meaningful pain reduction at 1-month follow-up. [10] Combined VR-neurofeedback has been explored in case reports with sustained analgesia reported at 1 year. [11]

## Heart Rate Variability (HRV)

Meta-analytic evidence consistently demonstrates reduced parasympathetic (vagal) HRV in chronic pain populations, with a moderate-to-large effect (particularly high-frequency HRV), heavily influenced by fibromyalgia studies. [12] A 2026 meta-analysis of 26 studies (3,641 participants) confirmed impaired vagal modulation (g = −0.52) and identified condition-specific autonomic phenotypes: fibromyalgia shows systemic dysregulation requiring longer recording protocols, while regional pain conditions display more localized HRV alterations detectable with shorter recordings. [13]

HRV biofeedback as treatment has mixed results. A recent RCT in co-occurring PTSD and chronic pain found significant reductions in pain interference (24.9%, d = −1.14) after 6 weeks of HRV biofeedback. [14] A combined HRV biofeedback-VR device reduced acute analgesic use by 65% and depression in chronic migraine. [15] However, a Phase II RCT in fibromyalgia found no significant differences between HRV biofeedback and standard care, and a systematic review concluded that while HRV biofeedback is feasible and safe, evidence of efficacy in chronic pain specifically remains limited. [16][17]

## Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)

ACT has a robust evidence base with multiple meta-analyses. A three-level meta-analysis of 33 RCTs (2,293 participants) found small-to-medium effects favoring ACT at post-treatment for pain intensity (g = 0.44), physical function (g = 0.59), depression (g = 0.43), anxiety (g = 0.43), and quality of life (g = 0.45), with effects maintained at follow-up. [18] Face-to-face ACT yields significantly larger effects than internet-delivered ACT, and patients with chronic headache and fibromyalgia benefit more than those with nonspecific pain. [18] The therapeutic mechanism is psychological flexibility — the ability to engage in valued activities despite pain — which has been supported as a mediator of outcomes. [19] An overview of 9 systematic reviews confirmed ACT improves pain-related functioning, depression, anxiety, pain acceptance, and psychological flexibility across multiple time points up to 12 months. [20]

## Broader Psychotherapy Landscape

The Cochrane review of 75 studies (9,401 patients) established CBT as the best-supported psychological treatment, with small but consistent benefits for pain, disability, and distress at moderate certainty. [21] However, newer approaches targeting nociplastic pain mechanisms show larger effects:

- **Pain Reprocessing Therapy (PRT):** In a landmark RCT, 66% of participants were pain-free or nearly pain-free after 4 weeks (vs. 10% usual care), with large effect sizes (g = −1.74 vs. usual care) maintained at 1-year follow-up. PRT works by reattributing pain to reversible brain processes rather than tissue damage. [22]
- **Emotional Awareness and Expression Therapy (EAET):** An RCT in older veterans showed EAET produced significantly greater pain reduction than CBT, with 63% achieving ≥30% pain reduction vs. 17% with CBT. [23][24]

A head-to-head comparison of CT, MBSR, and BT (N = 521) found all three produced similar improvements over treatment as usual, with benefits typically emerging by session 6, suggesting that common therapeutic factors may be as important as specific techniques. [25]

## Integration Across Domains

The convergence of these fields is an active frontier. VR platforms are being combined with EEG neurofeedback and HRV biofeedback to create multimodal interventions. [11][15][26] EEG studies have elucidated the neural mechanisms of VR analgesia, showing that VR exploration increases pre-stimulus gamma oscillations while VR mindfulness increases alpha oscillations — distinct pathways to comparable pain relief. [27] The shared finding of autonomic dysregulation (HRV) and altered cortical oscillations (EEG) in chronic pain supports a central sensitization model that newer psychotherapies like PRT and EAET directly target. [28]
