# VR Integration with Biofeedback, Wearables, EMDR, IFS, and Insurance

Source: OpenEvidence research synthesis.

## Bottom Line

VR combined with biofeedback and physiological monitoring shows enhanced therapeutic outcomes compared to VR alone. Heart rate variability (HRV) is the most commonly integrated parameter. [1][2][3][4]

## Heart Rate Variability Biofeedback

### Enhanced Engagement and Stress Buffering

VR-based HRV biofeedback using slow-paced breathing in immersive nature environments was comparable to standard HRV biofeedback for cardiac coherence and vagal tone. The VR implementation also:

- Buffered perceived stress in subsequent stressor tasks
- Increased relaxation self-efficacy
- Reduced mind wandering
- Preserved attentional resources [3]

### Mindfulness Enhancement

VR combined with biofeedback showed significantly greater improvements in receptive awareness and attentional focus compared to VR alone or traditional audio-guided mindfulness. All groups showed reductions in stress, anxiety, and heart rate. [2]

A brief 5-minute interactive VR mindfulness session with real-time HRV biofeedback (where HRV modulated the virtual environment through fog dissipation and visual transformations) significantly improved anxiety, mindfulness states, and HRV. Traditional mindfulness showed no significant HRV changes. [4]

### Clinical Outcomes

Higher presence in VR-biofeedback was associated with greater reductions in anxiety (beta = -0.24, p < 0.05). [5]

VR-based biofeedback showed significant decreases in:

- Depression (70.0% reduction in MADRS)
- Anxiety (29.5% reduction in STAI)
- Stress (61.7% reduction in VAS)

No significant difference compared to conventional biofeedback with a therapist. [6]

## EEG Neurofeedback Integration

### Efficacy Levels

VR-based EEG neurofeedback (per TGECEPI criteria) is:

- Probably efficacious for attention
- Possibly efficacious for emotions, mood, pain, relaxation, impulsiveness, memory, self-esteem, creativity, empathy, meditation, and tiredness [7]

### Advantages

Integrating immersive VR into neurofeedback systems can overcome limitations including:

- High number of sessions required
- Repetitive therapeutic tasks
- Difficulty sustaining task objectives

VR increases motivation, interest, and adherence through immersion and sense of presence. [7]

### Emerging Applications

Real-time biofeedback with EEG is being investigated to augment VR-assisted therapy for auditory verbal hallucinations in schizophrenia. Enables continuous adaptation of therapy based on physiological responses while allowing self-regulation training. [8]

## Most Common Biofeedback Parameters

- Heart rate or HRV were the most frequently used biofeedback parameters (50.0% of studies)
- Most VR-biofeedback interventions (72.2%) employed natural environments (e.g., island scenery)
- Outcomes were mainly anxiety (44.4%), stress (44.4%), or pain reduction (11.1%)
- VR-biofeedback reduced these at least as much as classical biofeedback [1]

## Advantages of VR-Biofeedback Integration

- Higher motivation and involvement compared to traditional biofeedback [1]
- Enhanced attentional focus: helps participants focus on the present moment, reduces mind wandering [3]
- Real-time personalization: continuous adaptation based on physiological responses [8]
- Improved emotion regulation: supports self-regulation training, improves tolerability [8]

## Current Limitations

No clear evidence that VR-biofeedback is more effective than traditional biofeedback for clinical outcomes. Results suggest VR-biofeedback advantages are in motivation, user experience, involvement, and attentional focus. [1]

Further research needed to assess the specific impact of VR and gamification. Testing broader clinical and younger samples would allow stronger conclusions. [1]

## VR with EMDR

### 3MDR (Multi-Modular Motion-Assisted Memory Desensitization and Reconsolidation)

Primary VR-EMDR integration approach. Based on EMDR principles. Designed to reduce avoidance behavior in PTSD patients. [9][10]

### 3MDR Methodology

Patients walk toward individualized trauma-related symbolic images in a CAVE (Cave Automatic Virtual Environment) system. Combines physical movement with visual exposure to personalized trauma cues. [9]

One treatment protocol exists for 3MDR. Two RCTs, one pilot study, and three case studies demonstrate its application. [9]

### Efficacy for Treatment-Resistant PTSD

Preliminary findings indicate promise for 3MDR in reducing PTSD symptoms. Includes benefits for emotional regulation and moral injury, particularly for treatment-resistant PTSD. [10]

Further research with larger samples and controlled studies is needed. [10]

### VR-Graded Exposure Therapy (VR-GET)

- VR-GET: significantly larger effect size for PTSD symptoms (g = 1.100, p = 0.001) compared to controls
- Conventional VRET: no significant difference (g = -0.279, p = 0.970) [11]

Findings support immersive PTSD treatments. Results need cautious interpretation due to substantial military service personnel studies. [11]

### Research Gaps

- Influence of spatial or social presence on efficacy was not examined
- Empirical evidence for efficacy on PTSD patients with imagination difficulties was lacking [9]

## Internal Family Systems (IFS) with VR

No direct research exists on IFS integration with VR. Emerging VR applications align conceptually with IFS through multi-perspective exploration and identity work.

### Conceptually Aligned VR Applications

- Avatar Therapy: individuals with auditory hallucinations have dialogue with voices embodied in avatars controlled by therapists. Promotes symptom externalization and self-identity exploration. Conceptually similar to IFS "parts work." [12]
- Explore Your Meanings Tool: immersive multi-perspective exploration of self-identity in 3D spaces. Easy to visualize differences between perceived and ideal self. Aligns with IFS concepts of different "parts" of self. [12]
- Multi-Perspective Framework: recent VR psychotherapy frameworks include a "Perspective" dimension (Self, Other, Witness, Multi-perspective) that could theoretically support IFS-style work with different internal parts. [13][12]

### Innovation Opportunity

Lack of research on IFS-VR integration represents a significant market gap. VR's unique ability to provide embodiment experiences and multi-perspective visualization could facilitate dialogue between different "parts" in IFS therapy.

## Insurance Reimbursement for VR Therapy

Severely limited. Only 17.6% of providers report the ability to bill for VR sessions (2021 data). [14]

### Current Billing Status

- No specific VR CPT codes: VR interventions are not specifically enumerated in CPT [15]
- Telehealth codes: some providers use standard psychotherapy CPT codes (90832, 90834, 90837) with telehealth modifier "95" if VR is delivered remotely. Requires interactive audio and video telecommunications for real-time communication [16][17]
- Incidental services: like telephone conversations, VR-based communications are often considered "incidental" by insurers. Not always reimbursed separately. Some insurers preclude separate fees [18]

### Barriers to Reimbursement

- Cost as primary barrier: inability to bill for VR services is a significant obstacle [14]
- Lower reimbursement rates: when insurers pay for technology-based services, they sometimes reimburse at lower rates than face-to-face treatment [18]
- Limited coverage: not all health insurance payers reimburse VR therapy or internet-based services [18]

### Future Reimbursement Possibilities

- Category III CPT Codes in Development: American Medical Association is developing "Category III" CPT codes to track emerging technologies. Might be used to set reimbursement policy for digital health [15]
- Remote Patient Monitoring (RPM) Codes: unclear whether current RPM codes might be used for monitoring mental health symptoms or physiologic states [15]
- Value-Based Payment Models: many payers are shifting from "volume" to "value" payment models. The Medicare Access and CHIP Reauthorization (MACRA) Act of 2015 incentivizes technology in reimbursement calculations [15]
- State-Level Variations: telemedicine services are generally covered through Medicaid at the state level. Recent state legislation has passed for telemedicine reimbursement, varying significantly by state [18]

### Regulatory Flux

Legal and regulatory issues around billing have been modified recently to allow additional flexibilities, particularly during COVID-19. At the end of the emergency, some regulations may return to pre-pandemic enforcement. Others will remain flexible or undergo permanent changes. [19]

Providers should stay current on reimbursement trends. Track utilization and outcomes data to inform regulatory changes. [19]

## Recommendations for a VR Therapy Product

1. Document outcomes data: track and share utilization and outcomes data to inform regulatory changes and demonstrate value to payers [19]
2. Design for hybrid billing: structure to integrate with existing evidence-based psychotherapy CPT codes rather than requiring new codes
3. Consider self-pay models: given limited insurance coverage, direct-to-consumer or employer-sponsored models may be more viable initially
4. Prepare for value-based contracts: position for alternative payment models that reward outcomes rather than volume
5. Monitor state-level changes: stay current on state Medicaid and telemedicine legislation
