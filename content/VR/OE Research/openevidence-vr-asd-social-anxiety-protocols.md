# VR Protocols for Autism Social Skills and Social Anxiety Disorder

Source: OpenEvidence research synthesis.

## Bottom Line

- **ASD social skills:** Pooled effect sizes are large. SMD 1.43 (social), SMD 2.45 (emotional) in children/adolescents. Most protocols use 4 to 10 weekly sessions of 60 minutes. Critical gap: almost no protocols use real-time AI adaptation despite expert consensus that individualization is the priority.
- **Social anxiety:** VRET produces large effect sizes vs waitlist (Hedges g = -1.17), no significant difference from in-person CBT, and gains hold at 12 months (g = -0.74) across 22 studies, n = 703.
- **Self-guided VR works:** Therapist-free SAD protocols hit g = -0.54 to -1.11. iCBT + VR achieves 15 to 23% remission vs 3% waitlist.
- **Adaptive VR is the frontier:** Real-time anxiety monitoring that adjusts scenario difficulty (Orskov protocol) and AI-driven personalization are early-stage but represent the closed-loop direction the field is moving.
- **Implementation gap:** Only 17.6% of providers can bill for VR. Cost is the most-cited barrier. Knowledge, business case, and clinical champions are the three priority domains for adoption.

## Part 1: ASD Social Skills Protocols

### 1. Immersive VR Task-Based Training (Adaptive Skills)

Yan et al. enrolled 33 individuals with high-functioning ASD (ages 8 to 18, IQ >= 80) in weekly 1-hour immersive VR sessions over 6 to 10 weeks. [3]

Protocol: 36 structured tasks completed twice, covering:

- Social interaction scenarios (greeting, turn-taking, conversation)
- Emotional face recognition tasks
- Daily living skill simulations
- Executive function challenges (Go/No-Go, n-back)

Outcomes: in-VR task scores and completion times (primary), parent-reported ABAS-II, ABC, BRIEF (secondary). Task scores improved 5.5%, completion times dropped 29.6%, ABC Relating subscale fell 43.2%. [3]

### 2. Metaverse-Based PEERS Program (Group Peer Interaction)

Lee et al. adapted PEERS (Program for the Education and Enrichment of Relational Skills) into a metaverse platform for children with high-functioning ASD. [4]

Protocol: 4 weekly sessions, each with theoretical instruction, then VR practice, then homework, then feedback.

- Session 1: social rules and consequences
- Session 2: situations based on behavior, team activities (virtual soccer)
- Session 3: responding to negative behavioral experiences and emotions
- Session 4: knowing and accepting individual differences

This is one of the few protocols enabling real peer-to-peer interaction (multiple children as avatars), not child-to-virtual-character. Outcomes on SRS-2 and CBCL. All SRS-2 subscales improved significantly. [4]

### 3. Emotion Recognition and Conversation Training

Across the broader literature, expert consensus on priority skill domains: [5]

- Assessing non-verbal responses (facial expressions, body language)
- Initiating, maintaining, and leaving conversations
- Perspective-taking (theory of mind)
- Emotional regulation

CBT techniques embedded in VR content: psychoeducation, modeling (virtual character demonstrates correct behavior), relaxation, reinforcement, perspective-taking questions. [5]

### 4. Job Interview and Vocational Training (Adults)

For adults with ASD, simulated job interviews and practical workplace tasks improved confidence, social understanding, and everyday skills. [6]

### Protocol Distinctions by Functioning Level

- **High-functioning ASD:** Immersive VR (HMD), complex multi-step social scenarios, conversation practice, perspective-taking. [7]
- **Low-functioning ASD:** Non-immersive VR (desktop/tablet), basic skills (eye contact, turn-taking, emotion identification). Lower cost, more flexible. [7]

## Part 2: Social Anxiety Disorder Protocols

A meta-analysis of 15 RCTs (720 participants) confirmed VR therapy produces large effect size vs waitlist (Hedges g = -1.17). No significant difference from in-person CBT. [8] A separate meta-analysis of 22 studies (n = 703) showed sustained efficacy at 12 months (Hedges g = -0.74). [9]

### 1. VR-CBT with Graded Exposure (Bouchard Protocol)

Landmark three-arm RCT (n = 59): VR exposure vs in vivo exposure vs waitlist. [10]

- 14 weekly individual CBT sessions (60 min each)
- Standard CBT components: cognitive restructuring, psychoeducation, behavioral experiments
- Exposure component replaced with VR
- VR scenarios: social gatherings, conversations with virtual characters, performance situations
- Primary outcome: Liebowitz Social Anxiety Scale (LSAS)

Result: VR more effective than in vivo at post-treatment on LSAS, more practical for therapists. Gains maintained at 6 months. [10]

### 2. Self-Guided VR Exposure (Zainal Protocol)

Self-directed VR exposure (no therapist) in 44 adults with SAD. [11]

- 4+ sessions of self-guided VR exposure
- Scenarios: simulated job interviews, social interactions
- Participants controlled own pace and duration
- Effect sizes: Hedges g = -0.54 to -1.11 vs waitlist
- Gains maintained at 3 and 6 months
- Cybersickness decreased over sessions, presence increased [11]

### 3. 360 Degree Video VRET for Public Speaking (Reeves Protocol)

51 participants with high public speaking anxiety. Used 360 degree video, not computer-generated environments. [12]

- Two active conditions: 360 degree video of audiences vs empty rooms
- Participants wore HMDs, delivered speeches to virtual environment
- Both conditions produced significant reductions in public speaking anxiety (eta squared = 0.71 to 0.90), social anxiety, and fear of negative evaluation
- Improvements maintained at 10 weeks
- Key finding: empty room condition also worked. The act of practicing in a realistic space matters. [12]

### 4. iCBT + VR Exposure (EASE Online Protocol)

Pan et al. tested 14-week internet-based CBT integrated with VR exposure in 329 Hong Kong adults with SAD. [13]

- Web-based and app-based formats
- Combined structured CBT modules with VR exposure exercises
- Remission: 15 to 23% in treatment vs 3% in waitlist
- Reliable improvement: 34 to 40% vs 11% in waitlist
- Gains maintained at 3 and 6 months [13]

### 5. Adaptive VR with Real-Time Anxiety Monitoring (Orskov Protocol)

Real-time physiological monitoring adapts VR exposure difficulty on-the-fly. [14]

- 10 weekly 60-minute individual sessions
- VR content individually tailored to participant fears
- System tracks anxiety during exposure, adjusts scenario difficulty
- Compared against standard CBT with in vivo exposure
- Primary outcome: Social Interaction Anxiety Scale (SIAS) [14]

### 6. Controllable Social Stress Paradigm (Hartanto)

Hartanto et al. demonstrated VR social dialogues can be systematically manipulated by varying ratio of positive-to-negative feedback from virtual characters. [15]

- Scenarios: blind date, job interview, neutral environment
- Virtual characters gave programmable positive or negative dialogue responses
- More negative feedback caused higher self-reported anxiety, higher heart rate, shorter verbal responses
- Strong dose-response (r = 0.91 for anxiety, r = 0.76 for heart rate)
- Provides mechanistic basis for graded exposure hierarchies in VR [15]

### 7. Third-Wave Therapy + VR (Bravo Protocol)

Acceptance-based and mindfulness approaches combined with immersive VR exposure in 50 participants with SAD (including comorbid psychosis subgroup). [16]

- 8 weekly sessions, ~60 min each
- Integrated mindfulness skills, intolerance of uncertainty work, experiential acceptance with VR social exposure
- Large effect sizes regardless of comorbidity
- Predictors of improvement: reductions in intolerance of uncertainty, experiential avoidance, and frequency of exposure exercises [16]

## Part 4: Future Directions

### 1. AI Integration and Adaptive Systems

A 2026 systematic review of 23 studies found AI applications for VRET include conversational agents, ML for outcome prediction, and personalization of exposure cues. Field is early-stage. Over half of ML studies set goals or evaluate results without therapist or patient involvement. [17]

Current AI applications: emotion detection, conversational agents (GPT-4-powered therapy avatars), clinical decision-support embedded in VR. [18] XAIA combined spatial computing, VR, and GPT-4 to deliver AI-driven therapy for anxiety and depression. Acceptable and safe, but some participants preferred human interaction. [19]

For ASD: no existing VR system uses real-time AI to adapt social scenarios based on the child's behavioral responses, despite expert consensus that individualized interventions are the priority. [4][5]

### 2. Real-Time Biofeedback and Physiological Monitoring

Scoping review of 18 studies: VR-biofeedback (most commonly HR/HRV) showed at least equivalent efficacy to classical biofeedback, with advantages in motivation, user experience, and attentional focus. [20] RCT (n = 131) of VR-based biofeedback for depression and anxiety: 70% MADRS reduction, 64% PHQ-9 reduction, comparable to therapist-led biofeedback. [21]

Adaptive VR (Orskov) is in trial. [14] Neuro-VR pilot trial is testing VR avatar therapy augmented with real-time EEG biofeedback for schizophrenia. [22] These represent the leading edge of closed-loop feedback that current commercial products lack.

### 3. Methodological Gaps

- **Small samples, high bias risk:** Over 50% of included studies in a 2025 meta-analysis (55 studies, n = 3,031) high risk of bias. GRADE certainty generally low to very low. [23]
- **No long-term follow-up:** SAD VRET sustained at 12 months but may become inferior to in vivo at later follow-up. Durability unclear. [9]
- **No standardized protocols:** Hardware, software, duration, outcomes vary widely. Limits replication. [24][8]
- **Underrepresentation:** Female participants, very young children underrepresented in ASD studies. Adults with ASD understudied. Most studies in high-income countries. [25][18]

### 4. Scaling Beyond the Clinic

Self-guided VR is a major direction. Zainal pilot RCT: g = -0.54 to -1.11 with gains at 6 months. [11] iCBT + VR (EASE Online, n = 329): 15 to 23% remission. [13] Could expand access dramatically.

Implementation barriers:

- 17.6% of providers can bill for VR sessions [26]
- Cost is most-cited barrier
- Clinician acceptability 84%, perceived appropriateness 69%, but only 59% rated VR feasible to implement [27]
- Theory-informed implementation roadmap: address knowledge gaps, build business case, recruit clinical champions [28]

### 5. Third-Wave Therapies and Transdiagnostic Approaches

Bravo et al.: mindfulness + intolerance of uncertainty + VR exposure produced large effect sizes for SAD regardless of comorbidity (including psychosis). Suggests transdiagnostic model. [16] Chowdhury and Khandoker argue Pavlovian extinction model has inherent limits (high attrition, relapse). Gold-standard SAD treatment requires paradigm shift toward neural markers and third-wave approaches. [29]

### 6. Gamification

Future VR treatments expected to incorporate game mechanics, narratives, reward systems to sustain motivation and reduce dropout. [30] PEERS metaverse demonstrated peer-to-peer interaction in game-like environments (virtual soccer) is feasible. Points toward scalable social platforms. [4]

### 7. Augmented and Mixed Reality

Meta-analysis: AR interventions for ASD showed promising efficacy (Hedges g = 0.92), evidence base smaller than VR. [31] AR + VR mixed-reality could enable VR-learned skills to transfer more naturally to real-world settings. [30]

## Future Directions Summary

| Direction | Current State | What Is Needed |
|---|---|---|
| AI-driven personalization | Early-stage; conversational AI, ML prediction | Therapist/patient co-design; real-time adaptive content |
| Real-time biofeedback | Pilot trials (EEG, HRV, GSR) | Closed-loop systems integrated into validated protocols |
| Standardized protocols | No consensus on hardware, software, duration, outcomes | Multi-site RCTs with harmonized measures |
| Self-guided/scalable delivery | Pilot RCTs positive; iCBT+VR feasible | Reimbursement pathways; larger effectiveness trials |
| Third-wave + VR | Proof-of-concept for transdiagnostic SAD | Larger RCTs; comparison with standard CBT |
| Gamification | Metaverse PEERS for ASD; conceptual for SAD | Integration into evidence-based curricula |
| AR/mixed reality | Promising effect sizes for ASD (g = 0.92) | More RCTs; real-world transfer studies |
| Long-term outcomes | VRET may fade vs in vivo at 12+ months | Multi-year follow-up; booster session protocols |

VR therapy has efficacy but lacks the infrastructure (standardized protocols, AI-driven adaptation, reimbursement, long-term data) needed to move from research tool to scalable clinical product. [32][33]
