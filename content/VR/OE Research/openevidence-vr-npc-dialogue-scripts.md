# NPC Dialogue Scripts: Three Scenarios x Three Difficulty Tiers

Below are full NPC scripts for all three scenarios across all difficulty tiers. These are designed around the core evidence-based principles: **graded exposure hierarchy** with systematically varied positive-to-negative dialogue feedback ratios,[1] **natural pauses** that force the user to sit with anxiety, and prompts that target the two core SAD fear domains: fear of negative evaluation and fear of scrutiny.[2][3] The user should be speaking aloud in response to every NPC line that ends with a question or expectant pause.

---

## SCENARIO 1: JOB INTERVIEW

The key finding from Hartanto et al. is that systematically varying the ratio of **negative to positive NPC feedback** within a dialogue directly controls anxiety, heart rate, and verbal output length in a dose-dependent manner (r = 0.91 for SUD scores).[1] The scripts below implement this gradient across tiers.

### Tier 1: Comfortable Challenge (1 interviewer, warm office)

NPC: Interviewer ("Sarah"), seated, smiling, relaxed posture.

> **[Sarah leans forward slightly, warm smile]**
>
> "Hi! Thanks so much for coming in today. Please, have a seat. Make yourself comfortable."
>
> *[3-second pause. User sits.]*
>
> "Before we get started, can I get you some water or anything?"
>
> *[3-second pause]*
>
> "Great. So, this is really just a chance for us to get to know each other a little. Nothing too formal. Sound good?"
>
> *[4-second pause for user response]*
>
> "Perfect. So, tell me a little about yourself. Whatever you'd like to share."
>
> *[15-second pause. Core exposure moment. User speaks.]*
>
> **[Sarah nods throughout, maintains eye contact]**
>
> "That's really interesting. I can tell you've had some great experiences."
>
> "What made you interested in this kind of role?"
>
> *[12-second pause for user response]*
>
> **[Sarah nods]**
>
> "That makes a lot of sense. I think that's a really thoughtful answer."
>
> "If you could describe one thing you're really good at, what would it be?"
>
> *[12-second pause]*
>
> **[Sarah smiles]**
>
> "I love that. Honestly, I think you'd be a great fit for the team."
>
> "Do you have any questions for me about the role?"
>
> *[10-second pause]*
>
> "Those are great questions. Well, I think we've covered a lot. It was really nice meeting you. We'll be in touch soon!"
>
> **[Sarah stands, extends hand for handshake]**

Design rationale: ~90% positive feedback. Minimal evaluative pressure. Long response windows without interruption. This mirrors the low-stress condition in the Hartanto dialogue stressor paradigm.[1]

---

### Tier 2: Moderate Challenge (2 interviewers, conference room)

NPCs: "David" (lead, neutral-to-warm) and "Karen" (note-taker, minimal eye contact).

> **[David gestures to a chair across the table. Karen is already writing something.]**
>
> "Thanks for coming in. I'm David, and this is my colleague Karen. She'll be taking some notes if that's alright."
>
> *[3-second pause]*
>
> "So, we've looked over your background. Tell us, what specifically drew you to apply here?"
>
> *[15-second pause for user response]*
>
> **[David nods slowly. Karen writes without looking up.]**
>
> "Okay. Interesting."
>
> *[2-second silence. Intentionally ambiguous feedback.]*
>
> "Can you walk us through a time you had to deal with a difficult situation at work, or at school, and how you handled it?"
>
> *[20-second pause. Longer exposure window.]*
>
> **[David tilts head slightly]**
>
> "And what would you say you learned from that?"
>
> *[12-second pause]*
>
> **[Karen looks up briefly, then back to notes]**
>
> "Mm-hmm."
>
> **[David]**
>
> "Where do you see yourself in five years?"
>
> *[12-second pause]*
>
> **[David's expression is neutral. Neither approving nor disapproving.]**
>
> "That's fair. Let me ask you this: what would you say is your biggest weakness?"
>
> *[15-second pause]*
>
> **[David nods]**
>
> "Alright. I appreciate your honesty."
>
> **[Karen]** *(first time speaking, slightly flat tone)*
>
> "Just one more: why should we choose you over other candidates?"
>
> *[15-second pause]*
>
> **[David]**
>
> "Okay. Good. We have a few more people to see, but we'll let you know either way. Thanks for your time."
>
> **[Handshake. Karen gives a brief, polite nod without standing.]**

Design rationale: ~50/50 positive-to-neutral feedback. Karen's minimal engagement creates ambiguity, a core anxiety trigger in SAD (fear of negative evaluation without clear evidence).[3] The "biggest weakness" question is a classic anxiety-provoking prompt.

### Tier 3: Full Challenge (4 to 5 interviewers, boardroom)

NPCs: "Michael" (lead, formal), "Priya" (skeptical), "Tom" (distracted), "Lisa" (neutral), "James" (challenging).

> **[User enters a large boardroom. Five people are seated along one side of a long table. Michael is reviewing papers. Tom is checking his phone. No one looks up immediately.]**
>
> *[5-second pause. User stands waiting.]*
>
> **[Michael looks up]**
>
> "Oh, yes. Have a seat."
>
> *[No smile. Gestures vaguely at a chair.]*
>
> "We're running a bit behind, so let's get right into it. Tell us why you're here."
>
> *[12-second pause]*
>
> **[Priya raises an eyebrow slightly but says nothing. Tom is still on his phone.]**
>
> **[Michael]**
>
> "Okay. I'm looking at your background and... help me understand. What exactly qualifies you for this position?"
>
> *[15-second pause]*
>
> **[James leans back, arms crossed]**
>
> "Can I jump in? I'm curious: have you actually done this kind of work before, or is this more... aspirational?"
>
> *[15-second pause]*
>
> **[Lisa nods politely but doesn't speak]**
>
> **[Priya]**
>
> "I want to follow up on that. Can you give us a specific example? A real one."
>
> *[20-second pause]*
>
> **[Tom puts phone down, looks at user for the first time]**
>
> "Sorry, can you repeat that last part?"
>
> *[User must repeat themselves. 10-second pause.]*
>
> **[Michael, looking at papers]**
>
> "Let me be direct. We've seen a lot of strong candidates. What makes you different?"
>
> *[15-second pause]*
>
> **[James]** *(slight smirk)*
>
> "That's... one way to look at it."
>
> *[3-second uncomfortable silence]*
>
> **[Priya]**
>
> "If you were in this role and you made a major mistake, something visible, how would you handle it?"
>
> *[15-second pause]*
>
> **[Michael checks his watch]**
>
> "Alright. We need to wrap up. Any final thing you want us to know?"
>
> *[12-second pause]*
>
> **[Michael]**
>
> "Fine. We'll be in touch."
>
> **[No handshake offered. Panel members begin talking to each other as user stands to leave.]**

Design rationale: ~70 to 80% negative/neutral feedback. Multiple evaluators create diffuse social threat. Tom's inattention and request to repeat triggers fear of being boring/unimportant. James's dismissive comment and Priya's demand for specifics target fear of negative evaluation. The lack of a closing handshake is an intentional social ambiguity stressor.[1]

## SCENARIO 2: PUBLIC SPEAKING

The self-guided VRET study for public speaking used participant-controlled audience size, audience reaction, and speaker salience as the graded variables.[2] The scripts below implement this with specific audience verbal/nonverbal behaviors.

### Tier 1: Comfortable Challenge (small seminar, 5 to 8 people)

> **[User stands at the front of a small, well-lit room. 6 people sit in chairs arranged in a loose semicircle. A friendly-looking person in the front row speaks.]**
>
> **NPC 1 (front row):** "Whenever you're ready. No rush."
>
> *[User begins speaking. Open-ended, no specific topic required. System displays a simple prompt card: "Talk about something you know well: a hobby, a trip, or something you learned recently."]*
>
> **[Throughout the user's speech, NPCs exhibit:]**
>
> - Nodding at regular intervals
> - Maintaining eye contact
> - One person smiles
> - One person takes notes (positive context: they're interested)
>
> **[At ~3 minutes, NPC 2 raises hand gently]**
>
> **NPC 2:** "That's really interesting. Could you tell us a bit more about that?"
>
> *[10-second pause for user response]*
>
> **[NPC 3 nods]**
>
> **NPC 3:** "I didn't know that. Thanks for sharing."
>
> **[At ~5 minutes]**
>
> **NPC 1:** "That was great. Thanks so much."
>
> **[Light applause from all NPCs]**

---

### Tier 2: Moderate Challenge (lecture hall, 20 to 30 people)

> **[User stands behind a podium with a microphone. ~25 people in tiered seating. Room is brighter. A moderator NPC stands to the side.]**
>
> **Moderator:** "Alright, the floor is yours. You've got about five minutes."
>
> *[User begins speaking. System prompt card: "Present on a topic you care about. Explain it as if teaching someone new."]*
>
> **[Audience behaviors during speech: mixed]**
>
> - Front rows: mostly attentive, some nodding
> - Middle rows: one person whispers to neighbor briefly, then refocuses
> - Back row: one person checks phone, another shifts in seat
> - One person in the middle gets up and quietly leaves at ~2 minutes *(this is a key anxiety trigger: the evidence shows audience departure is a potent stressor)*[2]
>
> **[At ~3 minutes, one audience member raises hand]**
>
> **NPC (middle row):** "Sorry, could you clarify what you meant by that last point?"
>
> *[12-second pause]*
>
> **[At ~4 minutes, another hand]**
>
> **NPC (front row):** "How does that compare to [alternative]? I've heard different things."
>
> *[15-second pause. Mildly challenging but not hostile.]*
>
> **[At ~5 minutes]**
>
> **Moderator:** "We're at time. Let's give a round of applause."
>
> **[Moderate applause. Some audience members are already packing up.]**

---

### Tier 3: Full Challenge (large auditorium, 100+ people, stage)

> **[User stands on a raised stage. Bright stage lights. A large audience stretches back into dim rows. A red recording light is visible on a camera to the side. A moderator introduces the user.]**
>
> **Moderator:** "Next up, we have our speaker. Please welcome them."
>
> **[Polite but brief applause. Some audience members are still settling in, talking to each other.]**
>
> *[User begins speaking. System prompt card: "You're presenting your ideas to a large group. Speak with confidence about something you believe in."]*
>
> **[Audience behaviors: predominantly challenging]**
>
> - Front rows: a few attentive faces, one person frowning slightly (concentrating, but reads as disapproval)
> - Multiple people in middle rows on phones
> - Audible cough from the back
> - Two people in row 5 whisper and one glances at the user
> - At ~2 minutes: a small group in the back laughs quietly (unrelated to the speech, but ambiguous)
>
> **[At ~3 minutes, a hand shoots up aggressively]**
>
> **NPC (row 3):** "I have to push back on that. Where are you getting that information?"
>
> *[15-second pause]*
>
> **[Regardless of user's response:]**
>
> **Same NPC:** "I'm not sure I agree, but... go on."
>
> *[3-second uncomfortable silence before user continues]*
>
> **[At ~4 minutes, another hand]**
>
> **NPC (row 6):** "Can you speak up? It's hard to hear you back here."
>
> *[User must continue speaking. 10-second pause.]*
>
> **[At ~5 minutes]**
>
> **Moderator:** "Let's wrap it up there. Thank you."
>
> **[Scattered applause. Some audience members are already standing to leave. The camera light turns off.]**

Design rationale: The audience challenge question, the request to speak up, the ambiguous laughter, and the scattered applause all target **fear of negative evaluation**, the core cognitive distortion in SAD.[3] The recording light adds performance pressure. The key therapeutic mechanism is that the user survives these moments and nothing catastrophic happens, which is the basis of exposure-based safety learning.[4]

## SCENARIO 3: SOCIAL GATHERING / PARTY

This scenario targets **social interaction anxiety** specifically: the fear of initiating, maintaining, and navigating unstructured conversation, which is distinct from performance anxiety.[5]

### Tier 1: Comfortable Challenge (small get-together, 4 to 6 people)

> **[User is in a living room. Warm lighting, soft music. 5 people are present: 3 chatting on a couch, 2 standing near a kitchen counter with drinks. One person near the counter notices the user.]**
>
> **NPC 1 ("Alex"):** "Hey! You made it! Come on in."
>
> *[Alex walks toward user]*
>
> "I'm so glad you're here. Can I get you something to drink?"
>
> *[5-second pause]*
>
> "So how's your week been? Anything exciting?"
>
> *[12-second pause for user response]*
>
> **[Alex nods, laughs lightly]**
>
> "Ha, I totally get that. Mine's been crazy too."
>
> "Oh, have you met Jordan? Jordan, come say hi!"
>
> **NPC 2 ("Jordan")** *(walks over, friendly)*
>
> "Hey! Nice to meet you. Alex has told me about you. So what do you do?"
>
> *[12-second pause]*
>
> **[Jordan]**
>
> "Oh cool, that sounds really interesting. I've always been curious about that."
>
> "Have you been to one of these before? It's usually pretty chill."
>
> *[10-second pause]*
>
> **[Alex]**
>
> "Oh my god, you have to try these. Jordan made them."
>
> *(gestures to food on counter)*
>
> **[Conversation continues naturally. Other NPCs occasionally glance over and smile but don't approach: low social demand.]**

---

### Tier 2: Moderate Challenge (house party, 15 to 20 people)

> **[User enters a larger space: open-plan living/dining area. Music is louder. ~18 people in clusters of 3 to 4. No one immediately acknowledges the user.]**
>
> *[8-second pause. User stands alone. This is intentional exposure to the "arrival moment" that is highly anxiety-provoking in SAD.]*
>
> **[A group of 3 nearby is mid-conversation. One person ("Sam") notices the user and makes brief eye contact but doesn't speak.]**
>
> *[5-second pause. User must decide to approach or wait.]*
>
> **[If user moves toward the group:]**
>
> **Sam:** "Oh hey, are you a friend of Mike's?"
>
> *[8-second pause]*
>
> **NPC 3 ("Riley"):** "Cool. I'm Riley. So what brings you out tonight?"
>
> *[10-second pause]*
>
> **NPC 4 ("Casey"):** *(to Riley, not the user)* "Oh wait, finish what you were saying before."
>
> **[Riley turns back to Casey. User is momentarily excluded from the conversation. 6-second pause.]**
>
> **[Sam, noticing]:** "Sorry, they always do this. So anyway, what were you saying?"
>
> *[10-second pause]*
>
> **[Later, user moves to get a drink. Another NPC is at the drinks table.]**
>
> **NPC 5 ("Morgan"):** "The good stuff's hidden in the back."
>
> *(slight smile)*
>
> *[8-second pause]*
>
> **Morgan:** "I don't think we've met. I'm Morgan."
>
> *[8-second pause]*
>
> "So do you know many people here?"
>
> *[10-second pause]*
>
> **Morgan:** "Yeah, me neither honestly. These things can be kind of awkward."
>
> *[5-second pause. Moment of shared vulnerability, models that others feel anxious too.]*

Design rationale: The moment of being excluded from conversation (Casey redirecting Riley) is a **micro-rejection**, a core feared outcome in SAD. Sam's recovery models that social ruptures are normal and repairable. Morgan's admission of awkwardness provides a corrective social experience.[5][6]

---

### Tier 3: Full Challenge (crowded networking event, 40+ people)

> **[User enters a large venue: high ceilings, standing tables, loud ambient conversation. 40+ people. Name tags visible on some. No one the user "knows." A bar is on one side.]**
>
> *[10-second pause. User stands alone near the entrance. Multiple people walk past without acknowledging. This extended isolation is the peak anxiety trigger for social interaction fear.]*
>
> **[User approaches a group of 3 at a standing table.]**
>
> **NPC 1:** *(glances at user, then back to conversation without pausing)*
>
> *[5-second pause. User is not acknowledged.]*
>
> **NPC 2:** *(notices user, slight nod)* "...oh, sorry, did you want to jump in?"
>
> *[8-second pause]*
>
> **NPC 1:** "So what do you do?"
>
> *[10-second pause]*
>
> **NPC 1:** "Huh. Okay."
>
> *(turns back to NPC 3)* "Anyway, like I was saying."
>
> *[User is sidelined again. 8-second pause.]*
>
> **[User moves to the bar. Bartender NPC:]**
>
> "What can I get you?"
>
> *[5-second pause]*
>
> **[A person next to the user at the bar ("Taylor") is looking at their phone. Doesn't initiate.]**
>
> *[10-second pause. User must decide whether to initiate with Taylor.]*
>
> **[If user speaks:]**
>
> **Taylor:** *(looks up, slightly surprised)* "Oh, hey. Yeah, I'm just hiding over here honestly."
>
> *(half-smile)*
>
> "These things are exhausting. Do you actually like networking?"
>
> *[10-second pause]*
>
> **Taylor:** "Yeah, same. I always feel like everyone else knows each other already."
>
> *[8-second pause]*
>
> "What's your name?"
>
> *[5-second pause]*
>
> **Taylor:** "Nice to meet you. I'm Taylor. Want to go grab one of those tables? It's quieter over there."
>
> **[Later, at the table, a third person ("Drew") approaches]**
>
> **Drew:** "Mind if I join? I don't know a soul here."
>
> *[5-second pause]*
>
> **Drew:** "So what do you both do?"
>
> *[10-second pause]*
>
> **Drew:** *(to user)* "That's cool. I actually have a friend who does something similar. You should connect. Want me to introduce you?"
>
> *[8-second pause]*
>
> **[Scene ends with the user in a small group that formed organically: modeling that social connection is possible even in intimidating environments.]**

Design rationale: The initial dismissal by NPC 1 is the hardest moment. It directly targets the catastrophic prediction "people will ignore me / I don't belong." Taylor's and Drew's warmth later in the scene provides the **corrective experience** that is central to exposure therapy: the feared outcome (total rejection) does not fully materialize, and connection happens despite initial difficulty.[4][3]

## Cross-Scenario Script Design Principles (Summary)

| Principle | Implementation | Evidence |
|-----------|----------------|----------|
| Graded negative-to-positive feedback ratio | Tier 1: ~90% positive; Tier 2: ~50/50; Tier 3: ~70 to 80% negative/neutral | Dose-dependent anxiety control (r = 0.91) [1] |
| Mandatory silence/pauses | 5 to 20 sec pauses after every NPC prompt; user must sit with the discomfort | Core exposure mechanism: anxiety peaks then habituates [2] |
| User speaks aloud | Every scenario requires verbal output; no silent observation | Verbal production under social pressure is the therapeutic mechanism [1], [3] |
| Ambiguous social feedback | NPCs give unclear reactions (neutral faces, "Hm," looking away) | Targets intolerance of uncertainty, a key SAD maintenance factor [4] |
| Social rupture + repair | Moments of exclusion/dismissal followed by re-inclusion | Models that social mistakes are survivable [5] |
| External social focus cues | Subtle prompt to look at NPC faces, not away | Social focus to increased positive affect and eye contact [6] |
| No catastrophic outcomes | Even in Tier 3, the scenario ends without disaster | Safety learning: feared outcome doesn't occur [5] |

## Audio/Voice Notes for Unity Implementation

- All NPC lines should be **pre-recorded by voice actors** (not TTS) for emotional authenticity. Tone, hesitation, warmth, and dismissiveness are critical to the therapeutic effect.
- Include ambient audio layers scaled by tier: Tier 1 = quiet/music; Tier 2 = moderate chatter; Tier 3 = loud crowd noise, clinking glasses, coughing.
- Pause durations listed above are minimums. The system should wait for the user to finish speaking (detect silence via Quest microphone VAD) before the next NPC line triggers.
