# PCS, TSK-17, and CPAQ-20: Item-by-Item Instrument Detail

Complete item-by-item details, scoring criteria, and psychometric properties for the Pain Catastrophizing Scale (PCS), Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia (TSK-17), and Chronic Pain Acceptance Questionnaire (CPAQ-20).

---

## PAIN CATASTROPHIZING SCALE (PCS)

The PCS was developed by Sullivan, Bishop, and Pivik (1995) and contains 13 items assessing three dimensions of catastrophic thinking about pain: Rumination (4 items), Magnification (3 items), and Helplessness (6 items). [1][2]

**Instructions:** "Everyone experiences painful situations at some point in their lives. Such experiences may include headaches, tooth pain, joint or muscle pain. People are often exposed to situations that may cause pain such as illness, injury, dental procedures or surgery. We are interested in the types of thoughts and feelings that you have when you are in pain. Listed below are thirteen statements describing different thoughts and feelings that may be associated with pain. Using the following scale, please indicate the degree to which you have these thoughts and feelings when you are experiencing pain."

**Response scale:** 0 = Not at all, 1 = To a slight degree, 2 = To a moderate degree, 3 = To a great degree, 4 = All the time

### All 13 Items

**Rumination Subscale (Items 8, 9, 10, 11)**

- Item 8: "I anxiously want the pain to go away."
- Item 9: "I can't seem to keep it out of my mind."
- Item 10: "I keep thinking about how much it hurts."
- Item 11: "I keep thinking about how badly I want the pain to stop."

**Magnification Subscale (Items 6, 7, 13)**

- Item 6: "I become afraid that the pain will get worse."
- Item 7: "I keep thinking of other painful events."
- Item 13: "I wonder whether something serious may happen."

**Helplessness Subscale (Items 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 12)**

- Item 1: "I worry all the time about whether the pain will end."
- Item 2: "I feel I can't go on."
- Item 3: "It's terrible and I think it's never going to get any better."
- Item 4: "It's awful and I feel that it overwhelms me."
- Item 5: "I feel I can't stand it anymore."
- Item 12: "There's nothing I can do to reduce the intensity of the pain."

### Scoring

| Score | Items | Range | Calculation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rumination | 8, 9, 10, 11 | 0-16 | Sum of 4 items |
| Magnification | 6, 7, 13 | 0-12 | Sum of 3 items |
| Helplessness | 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 12 | 0-24 | Sum of 6 items |
| PCS Total | All 13 | 0-52 | Sum of all items |

### Psychometric Properties

- Internal consistency: α = 0.92 (95% CI 0.91-0.93) across 229 studies in meta-analysis; test-retest reliability: ρ = 0.88 (95% CI 0.83-0.93). [3]
- Bifactor analysis (N = 8,370) supports essential unidimensionality — the general factor accounts for 96% of explained common variance, suggesting the total score is the most reliable metric. Subscale-specific variance is minimal (helplessness 7%, magnification 0%, rumination 15%). [2]
- The full 13-item PCS has superior measurement precision at the individual level compared to all short forms (4-item, 6-item, 7-item), which have unacceptably high standard errors for individual clinical decision-making. [4]

### Clinical Cutoffs and Severity

- **≥30:** Clinically relevant level of catastrophizing (the 75th percentile in chronic pain populations), widely used as the threshold for "high catastrophizer" status. [5][6]
- Severity classification has not been formally standardized for the full PCS, but the 75th percentile cutoff of ≥30 is the most commonly used clinical threshold.

### Minimally Clinically Important Change (MCIC)

- In chronic low back pain undergoing multidisciplinary rehabilitation: 8 points (AUC 0.88). For patients with baseline PCS ≥30 ("catastrophizers"), the MCIC is higher at 11 points; for those with PCS <30, it remains 8 points. [5]
- In chronic neck pain after physiotherapy + pain neuroscience education: 12.5 points. [7]
- The helplessness subscale accounts for unique variance in predicting pain severity, interference, quality of life, and depression beyond the other subscales, making it the most clinically informative subscale when subscale-level analysis is desired. [8]

## TAMPA SCALE OF KINESIOPHOBIA (TSK-17)

The TSK was developed by Miller, Kori, and Todd (1991) and contains 17 items measuring fear of movement and (re)injury. Items are rated on a 4-point Likert scale. Four items (4, 8, 12, 16) are reverse-scored. [9][10]

**Instructions:** "Below is a list of phrases which other patients have used to express how they view their condition. Please indicate the extent to which you agree or disagree with each statement by checking the appropriate box."

**Response scale:** 1 = Strongly disagree, 2 = Somewhat disagree, 3 = Somewhat agree, 4 = Strongly agree

### All 17 Items

1. "I'm afraid that I might injure myself if I exercise."
2. "If I were to try to overcome it, my pain would increase."
3. "My body is telling me I have something dangerously wrong."
4. "My pain would probably be relieved if I were to exercise." (R)
5. "People aren't taking my medical condition seriously enough."
6. "My accident has put my body at risk for the rest of my life."
7. "Pain always means I have injured my body."
8. "Just because something aggravates my pain does not mean it is dangerous." (R)
9. "I am afraid that I might injure myself accidentally."
10. "Simply being careful that I do not make any unnecessary movements is the safest thing I can do to prevent my pain from worsening."
11. "I wouldn't have this much pain if there weren't something potentially dangerous going on in my body."
12. "Although my condition is painful, I would be better off if I were physically active." (R)
13. "Pain lets me know when to stop exercising so that I don't injure myself."
14. "It's really not safe for a person with a condition like mine to be physically active."
15. "I can't do all the things normal people do because it's too easy for me to get injured."
16. "Even though something is causing me a lot of pain, I don't think it's actually dangerous." (R)
17. "No one should have to exercise when he/she is in pain."

(R) = Reverse-scored items (Items 4, 8, 12, 16). For these items, scoring is inverted: 1→4, 2→3, 3→2, 4→1.

### Scoring

- **TSK-17 Total:** Sum of all 17 items (after reverse-scoring items 4, 8, 12, 16). Range: 17-68. Higher scores indicate greater kinesiophobia. [9]
- **Two-factor structure (when using the TSK-11, which removes items 4, 8, 12, 16):** [11][12]
  - **Somatic Focus (SF):** Items 3, 5, 6, 7, 9 (range 5-20) — beliefs that there is a serious underlying medical problem
  - **Activity Avoidance (AA):** Items 1, 2, 10, 14, 15, 17 (range 6-24) — beliefs that activity may result in (re)injury or increased pain

### Psychometric Properties

- Internal consistency: α = 0.68-0.91 across versions; test-retest reliability: ICC = 0.77-0.99. [13]
- The systematic review by Dupuis et al. (2023) recommends the TSK-13 or TSK-17 as the preferred versions based on the best balance of validity, reliability, and responsiveness. The TSK-11 has inconsistent validity across studies. [13]
- Minimal detectable change: 11-13% of total score for TSK-17 (approximately 7-9 points); 8% of total score for TSK-13 (approximately 4 points). [13]
- Items 4, 8, 12, and 16 (the reverse-scored items) consistently show problematic psychometric properties — floor/ceiling effects, low item-total correlations, and poor factor loadings — which is why the TSK-13 (removing these 4 items) was developed. [14][15]

### Clinical Cutoffs and Severity (TSK-17)

A commonly used clinical cutoff is **≥37** on the TSK-17 for "high kinesiophobia." [16]

### Severity Levels (TSK-13)

Validated by Neblett et al. (2016) against objective lifting performance and psychosocial measures (N = 912): [17]

| Severity | TSK-13 score |
|---|---|
| Subclinical | 17-22 |
| Mild | 23-27 |
| Moderate | 28-34 |
| Severe | 35-44 |

As TSK-13 severity levels increased, objective lifting performance decreased while pain intensity, depressive symptoms, pain-related anxiety, and perceived disability all increased (all p < 0.001). [17]

### Minimally Clinically Important Change

- In chronic neck pain: 10.5 points on the TSK-17. [7]
- For the French short form (TSK-6, score 0-24): MCID = 2 points. [18]
- Importantly, one study found that the smallest detectable change (SDC) for the TSK was larger than the MCIC, meaning the TSK may not reliably distinguish clinically important change from measurement error at the individual patient level. This is a significant limitation for using the TSK as a sole treatment-response indicator. [19]

## CHRONIC PAIN ACCEPTANCE QUESTIONNAIRE (CPAQ-20)

The CPAQ was originally developed by McCracken (1998) and revised to a 20-item version by McCracken, Vowles, and Eccleston (2004). It measures two facets of pain acceptance: Activity Engagement (AE, 11 items) — pursuit of life activities regardless of pain — and Pain Willingness (PW, 9 items) — recognition that avoidance and control are often unworkable. [20][21]

**Instructions:** "Below you will find a list of statements. Please rate the truth of each statement as it applies to you. Use the following rating scale to make your choices. For instance, if you believe a statement is 'Always True,' you would write a 6 in the blank next to that statement."

**Response scale:** 0 = Never true, 1 = Very rarely true, 2 = Seldom true, 3 = Sometimes true, 4 = Often true, 5 = Almost always true, 6 = Always true

### All 20 Items

**Activity Engagement (AE) Subscale — Items 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 8, 9, 10, 12, 15, 19**

- Item 1: "I am getting on with the business of living no matter what my level of pain is."
- Item 2: "My life is going well, even though I have chronic pain."
- Item 3: "It's OK to experience pain."
- Item 5: "It's not necessary for me to control my pain in order to handle my life well."
- Item 6: "Although things have changed, I am living a normal life despite my chronic pain."
- Item 8: "There are many activities I do when I feel pain."
- Item 9: "I lead a full life even though I have chronic pain."
- Item 10: "Controlling pain is less important than any other goals in my life."
- Item 12: "Despite the pain, I am now sticking to a certain course in my life."
- Item 15: "When my pain increases, I can still take care of my responsibilities."
- Item 19: "It's a relief to realize that I don't have to change my pain to get on with my life."

**Pain Willingness (PW) Subscale — Items 4, 7, 11, 13, 14, 16, 17, 18, 20**

All PW items are reverse-scored (i.e., 0→6, 1→5, 2→4, 3→3, 4→2, 5→1, 6→0).

- Item 4: "I would gladly sacrifice important things in my life to control this pain better."
- Item 7: "I need to concentrate on getting rid of my pain."
- Item 11: "My thoughts and feelings about pain must change before I can take important steps in my life."
- Item 13: "Keeping my pain level under control takes first priority whenever I'm doing something."
- Item 14: "Before I can make any serious plans, I have to get some control over my pain."
- Item 16: "I will have better control over my life if I can control my negative thoughts about pain."
- Item 17: "I avoid putting myself in situations where my pain might increase."
- Item 18: "My worries and fears about what pain will do to me are true."
- Item 20: "I have to struggle to do things when I have pain."

### Scoring

| Subscale | Items | Range | Calculation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Activity Engagement (AE) | 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 8, 9, 10, 12, 15, 19 | 0-66 | Sum of 11 items |
| Pain Willingness (PW) | 4, 7, 11, 13, 14, 16, 17, 18, 20 | 0-54 | Sum of 9 reverse-scored items |
| CPAQ Total | All 20 | 0-120 | AE + PW |

Higher scores indicate greater acceptance (more activity engagement despite pain, and greater willingness to experience pain without attempting to control it). [20][21]

**Note on Item 16:** The Swedish validation study (Wicksell et al., 2009) found that Item 16 had low intercorrelations with other items and recommended its exclusion, resulting in a 19-item version. However, the standard 20-item version remains the most widely used. [22]

### Psychometric Properties

- Internal consistency: α = 0.82-0.88 for total score; AE subscale α = 0.82-0.88; PW subscale α = 0.75-0.83. [23][24][20]
- Test-retest reliability: ICC = 0.81-0.86. [25][24]
- The two-factor structure (AE and PW) has been confirmed across multiple samples and languages via confirmatory factor analysis. [23][20]
- The AE subscale consistently contributes more variance to the prediction of disability, depression, and life satisfaction than the PW subscale. [22][23]
- CPAQ explains more variance in pain intensity, disability, life satisfaction, and depression than the Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia. [22]

### CPAQ-8 Short Form

For repeated measurement during treatment (e.g., weekly monitoring), the CPAQ-8 is recommended. It contains 4 AE items and 4 PW items: [23]

- **AE items:** Item 1, Item 2, Item 6, Item 9
- **PW items (reverse-scored):** Item 7, Item 11, Item 13, Item 20
- Score range: 0-48. Internal consistency: α = 0.75-0.82. Test-retest reliability: r = 0.81. [23][25]
- The CPAQ-8 shows comparable convergent validity to the CPAQ-20, with moderate correlations with pain catastrophizing (r = −0.57), depression (r = −0.45), pain interference (r = −0.43), and kinesiophobia (r = −0.57). [26]

### CPAQ-2 Ultra-Brief Form

For the most rapid repeated assessment (e.g., pre/post each session), the CPAQ-2 uses only two items identified via IRT as the strongest from each subscale: [27]

- Item 9 (AE): "I lead a full life even though I have chronic pain."
- Item 14 (PW, reverse-scored): "Before I can make any serious plans, I have to get some control over my pain."

These two items account for >60% of the variance in both the CPAQ-8 and CPAQ-20. [27]

### Clinical Interpretation

Latent class analysis (N = 914) identified 4 patient clusters based on CPAQ scores: [28]

- **High acceptance (high AE + high PW):** Best functioning across all measures
- **Low acceptance (low AE + low PW):** Worst functioning — highest depression, disability, and pain-related anxiety
- **Discrepant (high AE + low PW):** Intermediate functioning — these patients are active despite pain but struggle with willingness to accept pain sensations
- **Medium acceptance:** Moderate on both subscales

These clusters differ significantly across measures of psychological and physical function in predictable ways, supporting the clinical utility of examining both subscales independently rather than relying solely on the total score. [28]

### Minimally Clinically Important Change

- The MCIC for the CPAQ has been less extensively studied than for the PCS or TSK. The smallest detectable change for the Italian CPAQ-20 was 22 points (total score). [24]
- For the Polish CPAQ-8, the minimal detectable change was 9.34 points. [29]

## RECOMMENDED VERSION SELECTION FOR THE PROPOSED PROTOCOL

| Measure | Use case | Recommended version |
|---|---|---|
| Pain Catastrophizing | Baseline, Week 5, 8, 12, 16, 28, 52 | PCS-13 (full) |
| Kinesiophobia | Baseline, Week 5, 8, 12, 16, 28, 52 | TSK-17 (or TSK-13) |
| Pain Acceptance (formal) | Baseline, Week 8, 12, 16, 28, 52 | CPAQ-20 (full) |
| Pain Acceptance (weekly monitoring) | Weekly during treatment | CPAQ-8 |
| Pain Acceptance (per-session) | Pre/post each session | CPAQ-2 |

The full-scale versions are recommended for all formal assessment timepoints because short forms have unacceptably high standard errors for individual-level clinical decision-making. [4] The CPAQ-8 and CPAQ-2 are appropriate for frequent repeated measurement where respondent burden is a concern, as they account for comparable variance in key outcomes. [27][23]
