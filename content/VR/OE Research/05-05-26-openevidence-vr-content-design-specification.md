# VR Content Design Specification: What the Patient Sees and Doesn't See

Concise, actionable specification of exactly what the patient sees and does not see across the three VR content pillars (graded exposure, pain neuroscience education, gamified movement), grounded in the evidence on visual feedback manipulation, embodiment, and gamified movement.

---

## 1. VR GRADED EXPOSURE TO FEARED MOVEMENTS

### What the patient sees:

- **Natural, non-medical environments** — gardens, trails, workshops, kitchens. The game world provides a reason to move (pick fruit, arrange shelves, explore a path) so the patient is focused on a goal, not on their spine. The VIGOR paradigm specifically designed games that "gradually reinforce increases in lumbar spine flexion to achieve game objectives," promoting "an implicit understanding that they are capable of engaging in significant lumbar spine motion without risk of injury." [1]
- **Visual underestimation of their own movement** (the single most powerful design element). The VR display shows the patient moving 10-20% less than they actually are. Jordán-López et al. (2025) demonstrated that this visual underestimation produced a 20% increase in pain-free ROM during lumbar extension (p = 0.002) and a 5-7% increase during lumbar flexion (p = 0.04), with patients with higher kinesiophobia and disability showing the greatest benefit. The MoOVi illusion (Harvie et al.) confirmed that VR can reliably induce illusory spinal movement — patients perform a large movement but perceive a small one, bypassing the threat threshold. [2][3][4]
- **Objects at progressively challenging positions** — items placed slightly lower (requiring more flexion), further to the side (requiring more rotation), or higher (requiring more extension) across sessions. The patient reaches for game objectives, not "doing exercises." Movement amplitude increases 5-10% per session without the patient being explicitly told.
- **Immediate positive environmental feedback** — when the patient completes a movement, the garden blooms, a score increases, a path opens, a character is helped. Reward is tied to movement completion, not to pain tolerance.
- **A first-person perspective with embodied virtual hands/arms** (no full avatar unless specifically calibrated). The Eccleston et al. DTxP trial used embodied VR specifically for adults with high fear of movement and found greater reductions in fear of movement than sham VR. [5]
- **Mixed-reality video replay** (later sessions). The Window et al. VR-CBT protocol incorporated mixed-reality video capture showing the patient their own performance — seeing themselves move normally and fluidly provides powerful visual disconfirmation of the belief "I move like a broken person." [6]

### What the patient does NOT see:

- **No spines, vertebrae, discs, or anatomical imagery** during movement sessions. Anatomical visuals are reserved exclusively for the education phase. During exposure, any medical imagery reinforces the "structural damage" belief the protocol is designed to extinguish.
- **No pain scales, pain ratings, or pain-related language on screen** during gameplay. Asking "rate your pain" during movement primes attention to pain and reinforces threat monitoring. Pain ratings are collected only before and after sessions, never during.
- **No movement counters, rep counters, or exercise labels** ("You completed 10 forward bends!"). This reframes the activity as exercise/therapy and reactivates the patient identity as "someone doing rehab for their bad back." The game tracks movement silently in the background for the clinician.
- **No warning messages, caution prompts, or "be careful" cues.** No yellow/red zones on a movement gauge. No "you're reaching your limit" indicators. These are safety behaviors that reinforce threat beliefs and undermine inhibitory learning. [2][3]
- **No visual overestimation of movement.** Showing the patient they are moving more than they actually are increases movement-evoked pain and decreases ROM. Jordán-López et al. found that the overstated condition (showing 10% more movement) reduced ROM by 22% compared to the understated condition. [2]
- **No dark, threatening, or high-arousal environments** (no cliffs, combat, falling, loud sudden sounds). The environment must feel inherently safe so the nervous system can downregulate while the body moves.
- **No mirrors or real-time body visualization** that could trigger body-image distress or hypervigilant self-monitoring of posture.

## 2. VR PAIN NEUROSCIENCE EDUCATION (PRT-Consistent)

### What the patient sees:

- **3D animated nervous system** — a visually engaging, simplified model showing a nociceptive signal traveling from the periphery through the spinal cord to the brain. The patient watches the signal arrive at the thalamus, then get processed through the anterior cingulate (threat evaluation), prefrontal cortex (context/meaning), and somatosensory cortex (sensation). This is the core VRNT model, which produced effect sizes of g = 0.63 for pain intensity and g = 0.84 for pain interference, mediated by reduced kinesiophobia and catastrophizing. [7]
- **An interactive "threat dial"** — the patient manipulates a virtual control that adjusts fear, attention, and catastrophizing levels, and watches the pain signal grow or shrink in real time at each brain relay station. This makes the abstract concept of central sensitization tangible and controllable.
- **Side-by-side MRI comparisons** — the patient's own imaging findings displayed next to age-matched asymptomatic controls, demonstrating that disc bulges, degeneration, and protrusions are present in pain-free individuals. This directly implements the PRT physician evaluation component, which includes "a review of available preexisting spinal imaging" to provide personalized evidence for centralized pain. [8]
- **A virtual spine that bends, twists, and loads under force** — demonstrating structural resilience. The spine model should look robust, thick, and strong — not fragile or precarious.
- **Neuroplasticity time-lapse** — neural pathways strengthening and weakening, showing that pain circuits can be rewired. Accompanied by brief video testimonials from recovered patients (a key PRT element). [8]
- **The Reducept-style model** — the de Vries et al. VR application "Reducept" delivers pain education and pain management skills training in VR, showing patients an interactive visualization of their pain system that they can learn to influence. [9]

### What the patient does NOT see:

- **No images of damaged, herniated, or degenerated spines presented as pathological.** When imaging is shown, it must always be framed as normal age-related findings, never as "what's wrong with your back."
- **No red/danger color coding on pain pathways.** Use neutral or cool colors for the nervous system visualization. Red = threat = reinforces the alarm.
- **No language or imagery suggesting pain is permanent, progressive, or degenerative.** The visual narrative must consistently convey reversibility and neuroplasticity.

## 3. GAMIFIED MOVEMENT CHALLENGES

### What the patient sees:

- **Progressive game levels with increasing movement demands embedded in the game objectives, not in explicit difficulty labels.** Level 1 requires reaching to waist height; Level 5 requires reaching to the floor. The patient perceives increasing game complexity, not increasing "exercise difficulty." [10][1]
- **Personal best tracking displayed as game scores** (points, stars, completion time), not as clinical metrics (degrees of flexion, repetitions). The MacIntyre et al. gamified VR graded activity study found high adherence and enjoyment, with significant pain reduction (effect estimate = −1.0, p < .001). [10]
- **Unlockable content** — new environments, characters, or tools that become available as the patient progresses. This leverages intrinsic motivation and curiosity rather than therapeutic compliance.
- **Subtle ambient biofeedback** — the environment responds to the patient's autonomic state (HRV coherence). When relaxed during movement, the sky brightens, music becomes more pleasant, or the garden grows faster. This is never labeled as "biofeedback" — it is simply part of the game world's atmosphere.
- **A post-session "movement map"** (shown only after gameplay ends) — a visual summary of the movements completed, displayed as a journey or achievement, not as a clinical report. The clinician sees the raw kinematic data separately.

### What the patient does NOT see:

- **No exercise instructions** ("Now bend forward 30 degrees"). The game objective creates the movement naturally.
- **No "correct form" overlays or posture guides.** These reinforce the belief that there is a "wrong" way to move that will cause injury — the exact threat belief being targeted. The CFT approach similarly avoids prescriptive movement correction. [11]
- **No failure states tied to movement quality.** The patient cannot "lose" by moving "incorrectly." They can only fail to reach a game objective, which is solved by moving more, not by moving "better."
- **No clinical dashboards visible to the patient during gameplay.** ROM data, movement velocity, HRV trends — all captured silently for the clinician. The patient's experience is purely game-world.

---

## SUMMARY DESIGN RULE

The overarching principle: **everything the patient sees should make movement feel safe, rewarding, and normal; everything that could make movement feel medical, monitored, or dangerous is hidden from the patient's view and reserved for the clinician's dashboard.** The VR environment is not a clinic — it is a world where moving is simply what one does to play, explore, and succeed. The therapeutic mechanism — expectancy violation and threat belief disconfirmation — operates most powerfully when the patient is not thinking about therapy at all. [8][2][3][1]
