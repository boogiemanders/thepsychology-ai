# Condition-Specific Adaptations of the Integrated Chronic Pain Protocol

Comprehensive condition-specific adaptation of the integrated protocol across three major chronic pain populations (fibromyalgia, chronic low back pain, neuropathic pain), organized by the key mechanistic differences that drive treatment modifications.

---

## FOUNDATIONAL FRAMEWORK: PAIN MECHANISM CLASSIFICATION

The protocol must first classify each patient's dominant pain mechanism — nociceptive, neuropathic, or nociplastic — as these categories have distinct autonomic phenotypes, cortical signatures, and differential responses to psychotherapy and biofeedback. [1][2] Many patients have mixed presentations (e.g., chronic low back pain with radiculopathy involves both nociceptive and neuropathic components), and the protocol should weight adaptations accordingly.

---

## 1. FIBROMYALGIA (NOCIPLASTIC PAIN)

### Autonomic Phenotype

Fibromyalgia shows the most severe and systemic autonomic dysregulation of the three populations. The meta-analytic effect size for reduced HF-HRV is largest in fibromyalgia (Hedges' g = −0.69 for HF-HRV) compared to other functional somatic syndromes. [3] A critical methodological finding is that fibromyalgia requires longer HRV recording durations to fully characterize the autonomic impairment — shorter recordings underestimate the deficit, whereas regional pain conditions are adequately characterized by shorter protocols. [4] Fibromyalgia patients also show attenuated HRV reactivity to cognitive stress, with three distinct autonomic clusters: (1) near-normal HRV with low mood disturbance, (2) reduced HRV with moderate anxiety/depression, and (3) severely impaired HRV with the highest psychiatric burden. [5]

**Protocol adaptation — HRV biofeedback:**

- Use ≥10-minute resting-state recordings at baseline (vs. 5 minutes for CLBP) to capture the full extent of vagal impairment. [4]
- Prioritize HRV biofeedback earlier in the protocol (Phase 2, concurrent with psychotherapy from Week 1) rather than reserving it for non-responders, given the systemic autonomic dysregulation.
- However, the Phase II RCT by Carta et al. (N = 64) found that 10 sessions of HRV biofeedback added to standard pharmacotherapy did not demonstrate efficacy on primary outcomes (FIQ, sleep, pain) in fibromyalgia, though secondary analyses showed improvements in perceived energy and walking ability. This tempers expectations and suggests HRV biofeedback in fibromyalgia may function better as an adjunctive autonomic conditioning tool rather than a primary analgesic intervention. [6][7]
- Cluster patients using the Zetterman et al. three-cluster model at baseline: patients in Cluster 3 (lowest HRV, highest psychiatric burden) should receive the most intensive biofeedback protocol and concurrent psychiatric management. [5]

### EEG Phenotype

Fibromyalgia shows a distinct cortical signature: increased beta-band connectivity across distant brain networks and altered EEG microstates (reduced occurrence and coverage of microstate class C), suggesting dominance of endogenous top-down processing and limited processing of novel external events. [8] Multivariate pattern analysis can distinguish fibromyalgia patients from controls with AUC values exceeding 0.75 across all frequency bands, reaching 0.99 in beta and gamma bands when anxiety scores are included. [9] Symptom duration further modifies the EEG phenotype — long-duration fibromyalgia shows distinct beta/gamma alterations compared to short-duration. [9]

Importantly, the affective-motivational dimension of pain (not the sensory-discriminative dimension) correlates with EEG abnormalities in fibromyalgia: alpha power at frontal and central sites positively correlates with affective pain scores, while delta power negatively correlates. [10]

**Protocol adaptation — EEG/neurofeedback:**

- Target alpha upregulation at frontal-central sites rather than the β1/β2 protocol used for neuropathic pain, given that fibromyalgia's EEG signature is dominated by affective processing abnormalities rather than thalamocortical dysrhythmia. [10][8]
- Include anxiety measures (STAI or GAD-7) as covariates in EEG phenotyping, as anxiety dramatically improves classification accuracy. [9]
- Consider α/θ neurofeedback (which specifically reduced anxiety and depression in the Mussigmann et al. neuropathic pain trial) as the preferred protocol for fibromyalgia patients with high anxiety/depression burden. [11]

### Psychotherapy Track

Fibromyalgia patients show the strongest differential response to ACT and EAET compared to other chronic pain populations. The three-level meta-analysis by Lai et al. found that participants with fibromyalgia showed greater benefit from ACT compared to those with non-specific or mixed pain (g = 0.44 for pain intensity). [12] The PROSPER-FM Phase 3 trial (N = 275) demonstrated that self-guided digital ACT significantly improved fibromyalgia symptoms. [13]

EAET has particularly strong evidence in fibromyalgia. The Lumley et al. cluster-RCT (N = 230) found EAET superior to education and comparable to CBT, with advantages over CBT on fibromyalgia symptoms (d = 0.35) and widespread pain (d = 0.37), and a higher rate of 50% pain reduction (22.5% vs. 8.3%). [14] A critical moderator finding: fibromyalgia patients with normal pressure pain tolerance (suggesting more centralized/nociplastic rather than peripheral sensitization) showed dramatically larger pain improvement after EAET (mean = 2.14 points) compared to CBT (0.13 points), while those with low pain tolerance responded similarly to both. [15]

**Protocol adaptation — psychotherapy:**

- **Default track:** EAET for fibromyalgia patients with high trauma/adversity history, high psychiatric comorbidity (depression, anxiety, PTSD), or normal pressure pain tolerance. [14][15][16]
- **Alternative track:** ACT for fibromyalgia patients without significant trauma history who present primarily with experiential avoidance and activity disengagement (low CPAQ scores). [12][13]
- PRT is less well-suited as the primary approach for fibromyalgia because PRT was developed for and tested in primary chronic back pain where the goal is reattribution of pain to brain processes — fibromyalgia patients often already understand their pain lacks a peripheral structural cause, making the reattribution component less novel. [17]

### VR Content Adaptation

The umbrella review by Tang et al. found that VR in fibromyalgia effectively reduced pain (SMD −0.45), fatigue (SMD −0.58), anxiety (SMD −0.50), and depression (SMD −0.62). [18] The broad symptom reduction profile suggests VR in fibromyalgia works primarily through affective and attentional mechanisms rather than movement-specific pathways.

- **Recommended VR content:** Immersive calming/nature environments for autonomic regulation during HRV biofeedback sessions; VR-delivered mindfulness and body-scan exercises; gentle movement-based VR (yoga, tai chi simulations) rather than sport/exercise VR.
- **Avoid:** High-intensity movement VR that may trigger post-exertional symptom flares.

## 2. CHRONIC LOW BACK PAIN (PRIMARILY NOCICEPTIVE/NOCIPLASTIC)

### Autonomic Phenotype

Chronic low back pain (CLBP) shows more localized and less severe autonomic dysregulation than fibromyalgia. The meta-regression by Fernández-Morales et al. confirmed that regional pain conditions display more localized HRV alterations and are adequately characterized by shorter recording protocols. [4] The meta-analytic effect for reduced HRV in chronic pain overall is g = −0.52 for vagal indices, but this is heavily influenced by fibromyalgia studies; CLBP-specific effects are smaller. [19]

**Protocol adaptation — HRV biofeedback:**

- Standard 5-minute resting-state recordings are sufficient for baseline phenotyping. [4]
- HRV biofeedback can remain in the Phase 3 augmentation role (for non-responders at Week 5) rather than being introduced from Week 1, as the autonomic dysregulation is less systemic.
- Focus on resonance frequency breathing as a self-regulation skill to deploy during pain flares and graded exposure exercises, rather than as a primary autonomic restoration intervention.

### EEG Phenotype

CLBP shows thalamocortical network dynamics that are distinct from neuropathic pain. The Tu et al. fMRI study identified two reoccurring dynamic connectivity states in CLBP: one associated with chronic pain pathophysiology (abnormal VL/VPL-postcentral gyrus and dorsomedial nucleus-insula connectivity) and another associated with temporary pain exacerbation (altered VL/VPL-default mode network connectivity). [20] The systematic review by Zebhauser et al. found higher theta and beta power in chronic pain patients generally, but no clear longitudinal associations between EEG changes and pain relief. [21]

**Protocol adaptation — EEG/neurofeedback:**

- EEG phenotyping at baseline is less critical for treatment assignment in CLBP than in neuropathic pain or fibromyalgia, given the weaker and less specific EEG signatures.
- If neurofeedback is used, target individual alpha peak frequency upregulation at central sites, as this has the broadest evidence base across pain conditions. [22]
- Consider neurofeedback as a lower-priority augmentation compared to VR-based graded exposure, which has stronger evidence in CLBP specifically.

### Psychotherapy Track

CLBP is the population with the strongest evidence for PRT. The Ashar et al. RCT demonstrated that 73% of PRT-treated patients were pain-free or nearly pain-free at post-treatment, with effects persisting at 1-year follow-up (g = 0.63 for pain intensity). [17] The mechanism — reattribution of pain from peripheral tissue damage to reversible brain processes — is most applicable when patients believe their back pain reflects structural damage despite the absence of concordant pathology. [17][23]

EAET also shows strong effects in musculoskeletal pain: the Yarns et al. RCT in older veterans found EAET superior to CBT with 63% achieving ≥30% pain reduction vs. 17% for CBT, and patients with higher baseline depression, anxiety, or PTSD showed particularly robust responses to EAET. [16]

The OPTIMIZE SMART trial (N = 749) tested sequential treatment strategies for CLBP, finding that non-responders to initial PT or CBT benefited from switching to the alternative modality. [24]

**Protocol adaptation — psychotherapy:**

- **Default track:** PRT for CLBP patients with low structural pathology concordance (i.e., imaging findings do not explain the pain), high fear-avoidance beliefs (TSK ≥37), and high pain catastrophizing (PCS ≥30). [17]
- **Alternative track:** EAET for CLBP patients with significant trauma history, high psychiatric comorbidity, or those who have already been educated about pain neuroscience but continue to have high emotional distress. [16]
- **Switching rule:** Non-responders to PRT at Week 5 → switch to EAET (or vice versa), consistent with the OPTIMIZE SMART design principle of sequential treatment optimization. [24]

### VR Content Adaptation

VR has the strongest evidence base in CLBP for reducing kinesiophobia (MD −5.46 on TSK) and pain intensity (MD −1.43). [18] Nonimmersive VR-assisted active training showed large effects on pain (SMD −1.79), disability (SMD −0.44), and kinesiophobia (SMD −2.94) that persisted at 6 months. [25] The VR neuroscience-based therapy (VRNT) trial by Čeko et al. demonstrated that integrating pain neuroscience education into VR produced significant reductions in pain intensity (g = 0.63) and interference (g = 0.84), mediated by reduced kinesiophobia and catastrophizing, with corresponding changes in prefrontal-somatomotor brain connectivity. [26]

- **Recommended VR content:** Graded exposure to feared movements (bending, lifting, twisting) in immersive VR environments; VR-delivered pain neuroscience education (consistent with PRT principles); gamified movement challenges that progressively increase range of motion.
- The VR environment should be designed to disconfirm threat beliefs about spinal movement — the core mechanism of both PRT and graded exposure.

## 3. NEUROPATHIC PAIN

### Autonomic Phenotype

Neuropathic pain shows autonomic changes that are more variable and etiology-dependent than fibromyalgia or CLBP. Sciatica patients show markedly lower HF power (p < 0.0001 vs. controls) with a modest negative correlation between HF power and pain duration. [27] However, the autonomic profile depends heavily on the specific neuropathic condition (peripheral neuropathy, radiculopathy, CRPS, central post-stroke pain) and whether sympathetic nervous system involvement is present (as in CRPS).

**Protocol adaptation — HRV biofeedback:**

- For **CRPS:** The American Academy of Pain Medicine guidelines specifically recommend relaxation training in conjunction with thermal and/or EMG biofeedback as a core component of treatment, given the sympathetic nervous system involvement. HRV biofeedback should be introduced early (Phase 2) and combined with thermal biofeedback targeting the affected extremity. [28]
- For **peripheral neuropathy (diabetic, post-herpetic):** HRV biofeedback serves primarily as an adjunctive autonomic conditioning tool. The autonomic neuropathy that often accompanies peripheral neuropathy may limit the magnitude of HRV improvement achievable.
- For **radiculopathy/sciatica:** Standard Phase 3 augmentation timing is appropriate, similar to CLBP.

### EEG Phenotype

Neuropathic pain has the most well-characterized and specific EEG signature, consistent with the thalamocortical dysrhythmia (TCD) model: increased theta power (4-7 Hz), decreased high-alpha/low-beta power (10-20 Hz), and possibly increased high-beta power (20-30 Hz), with a shifted dominant peak frequency. [29] This pattern reflects disrupted thalamocortical oscillatory loops, where reduced thalamic GABAergic inhibition leads to excess theta activity that "replaces" normal alpha rhythms. [30][31] Machine learning can detect spectrally equivalent but spatially distinct TCD patterns across neuropathic pain, Parkinson's disease, tinnitus, and depression. [32] In CRPS Type I, MEG imaging shows delta/theta peaks localized to somatosensory cortex corresponding to pain localization and to orbitofrontal-temporal cortices related to affective pain perception. [33]

**Protocol adaptation — EEG/neurofeedback:**

- Neurofeedback is the **highest-priority biofeedback modality** for neuropathic pain (elevated above HRV biofeedback in the treatment hierarchy).
- The Mussigmann et al. RCT (N = 32) provides the most specific protocol guidance: β1/β2 neurofeedback (targeting the central cortical region contralateral to the pain side) specifically reduced ongoing and evoked pain intensity, while α/θ neurofeedback specifically reduced anxiety and depression. This allows protocol matching: [11]
  - Neuropathic pain with predominant sensory symptoms (burning, shooting) → β1/β2 protocol
  - Neuropathic pain with predominant affective burden (anxiety, depression, sleep) → α/θ protocol
- 12 sessions over 4 weeks is the validated dosing schedule. [11][34]
- ERP assessment may add diagnostic value: neuropathic pain patients show increased N2 and P2 latencies with decreased N2-P2 amplitude, distinguishing them from non-neuropathic chronic pain patients who show no consistent ERP differences from controls. [35]

### Psychotherapy Track

Neuropathic pain responds less robustly to psychological interventions than nociplastic pain, consistent with the presence of identifiable peripheral nerve pathology. The Cochrane review and Lancet series note that effect sizes for psychological therapies are generally small across chronic pain, but the specific evidence base for neuropathic pain is thinner than for CLBP or fibromyalgia. [36][2][37]

PRT is **not** well-suited for neuropathic pain because its core mechanism — reattributing pain from peripheral tissue damage to brain processes — conflicts with the reality that neuropathic pain does involve identifiable nerve damage. [17][38] However, novel psychotherapy approaches targeting nociplastic components (EAET, PRT) may be relevant for the centralized component that often develops secondary to chronic neuropathic pain. [38]

**Protocol adaptation — psychotherapy:**

- **Default track:** ACT, which does not require reattribution of pain causation but instead targets psychological flexibility and values-based action despite ongoing pain. ACT's mechanism (acceptance rather than pain elimination) is better aligned with neuropathic pain where complete pain resolution is less likely. [12][39]
- **Alternative track:** CBT with specific focus on pain coping skills, activity pacing, and sleep hygiene — the traditional evidence-based approach for neuropathic pain management. [36][37]
- For neuropathic pain with high trauma/psychiatric comorbidity: EAET may be considered, as the Yarns et al. trial included patients with musculoskeletal pain broadly and showed that higher baseline PTSD symptoms moderated greater EAET response. [16]
- Psychotherapy should be positioned as **complementary** to neurofeedback rather than the primary intervention, reversing the hierarchy used for CLBP and fibromyalgia.

### VR Content Adaptation

The evidence for VR specifically in neuropathic pain is limited. The case report by Orakpo et al. described sustained analgesia for 1 year after VR-neurofeedback (VR-NFB) fusion therapy in a patient with centralized pain from spondylolisthesis with disc herniations. [40] The meta-regression by Lier et al. found that VR effects did not differ significantly by pain type (acute, chronic, procedural), but the analysis did not separate neuropathic from nociceptive chronic pain. [41]

- **Recommended VR content:** VR as a delivery platform for neurofeedback (VR-NFB fusion), providing immersive visual feedback of brain state changes during neurofeedback training; mirror therapy VR for CRPS and phantom limb pain; graded sensory exposure VR for allodynia desensitization.
- **Avoid:** VR content requiring vigorous physical movement in patients with movement-evoked neuropathic pain (e.g., radiculopathy).

## INTEGRATED CONDITION-SPECIFIC PROTOCOL SUMMARY

| Element | Fibromyalgia | Chronic Low Back Pain | Neuropathic Pain |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dominant mechanism | Nociplastic | Nociceptive/nociplastic mix | Neuropathic |
| HRV recording duration | ≥10 min | 5 min | 5 min (longer for CRPS) |
| HRV biofeedback timing | Phase 2 (early) | Phase 3 (augmentation) | CRPS: early; others: standard |
| EEG signature | ↑ Beta connectivity, altered microstates, affective-pain alpha | Theta/beta elevation, less specific | TCD: ↑ theta, ↓ alpha-beta, shifted peak |
| Neurofeedback protocol | Alpha upregulation (frontal-central); α/θ if high anxiety | Individual alpha at central (low priority) | β1/β2 (sensory) or α/θ (affective), contralateral |
| Default psychotherapy | EAET | PRT | ACT |
| Alternative psychotherapy | ACT | EAET | CBT (pain coping) |
| VR content focus | Calming nature, mindfulness, gentle movement | Graded movement exposure, VRNT | VR-NFB fusion, mirror therapy |
| Treatment hierarchy | Psychotherapy > HRV BFB > EEG NF > VR | Psychotherapy + VR ≈ priority; biofeedback augmentation | EEG NF > psychotherapy > HRV BFB |

## IMPORTANT CAVEATS

These condition-specific adaptations are derived from synthesizing evidence across separate literatures (EEG biomarkers, HRV phenotyping, psychotherapy trials, VR meta-analyses) that have not been tested together in an integrated protocol for any specific pain population. Several key uncertainties remain:

- The HRV biofeedback evidence in fibromyalgia is weak — the only adequately powered RCT found no significant benefit on primary outcomes. The rationale for early HRV biofeedback in fibromyalgia rests on the severity of autonomic dysregulation rather than demonstrated treatment efficacy. [6]
- The EEG phenotyping approach assumes that condition-specific cortical signatures are stable enough to guide individual treatment decisions, but the mega-analysis of 614 patients found limited replicability of pain-EEG associations across datasets. [21]
- The psychotherapy matching recommendations (e.g., EAET for fibromyalgia with normal pain tolerance, PRT for CLBP with low structural concordance) are based on moderator analyses from individual trials, not prospective matching studies.
- Many patients have mixed pain mechanisms — a patient with CLBP and radiculopathy has both nociceptive/nociplastic and neuropathic components, requiring a blended approach rather than strict adherence to one condition-specific track.
