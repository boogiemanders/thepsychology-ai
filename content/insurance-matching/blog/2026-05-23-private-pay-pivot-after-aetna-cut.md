# How Psychologists Are Building Private Pay Panels After the Aetna Cut

A 60-minute session at the new Aetna-via-Alma rate pays $113.

A 60-minute private pay session in a major metro at the doctoral rate pays $200 to $400.

You can do the math from there.

I am a clinical psychologist building thepsychology.ai. I have spent the last several months interviewing doctoral providers about how they structure their panels. There is a clear pattern in the providers who are not stressed about the Aetna cut: they are mostly or fully private pay.

This post is for the providers who want to know what that path actually looks like. It is not a sales pitch for cash-pay practice. It is a clear-eyed look at the trade-offs, the math, and the playbook.

## Why the math works

The basic asymmetry is this: a private-pay practice cuts out three layers of cost that insurance practices carry.

**Layer 1: The platform cut.** Alma is taking around $46 on a $125 Aetna 90834. Headway and Grow Therapy take 20-30% per session. Private pay removes this entirely.

**Layer 2: The payer cut.** Even direct contracts with insurers pay below the cash rate. Aetna direct at $125-$140 for 90834 is below the $200-$400 cash rate. The insurer is taking a margin on every session.

**Layer 3: The administrative drag.** Insurance billing requires eligibility verification, prior authorization (in some cases), claim submission, denial appeals, ERA reconciliation, and credentialing renewals. Reasonable estimates put this at 10-15% of provider time, or equivalent cost if outsourced to a billing service.

Strip those three layers and the per-session economics shift dramatically. A doctoral psychologist seeing 20 cash-pay sessions per week at $250 grosses $5,000. The same psychologist seeing 20 Alma-Aetna sessions at the new $113 rate grosses $2,260.

That is a 121% revenue difference for the same clinical hours.

## The real obstacle is not the math

Most doctoral providers know this math. They have known it for years. The reason they are not all private pay is not arithmetic — it is patient acquisition.

When you take insurance, the insurance company sends you patients. They tell their members you exist. They funnel referrals through their directory. Even if the platform takes a cut, the cut is the price of an acquisition engine.

When you drop insurance, the acquisition problem becomes yours. You have to fill your panel from scratch, from sources you control. This is the actual barrier.

The providers who have built sustainable private-pay practices have solved acquisition. They have not solved it through luck or vague "marketing." They have solved it through repeatable channels.

## The acquisition channels that actually work

Based on what I have seen in private practice owner communities and from interviewing providers directly, here are the channels that consistently produce private-pay clients for doctoral psychologists:

**1. Referrals from psychiatrists and physicians.** This is the highest-quality channel. A psychiatrist who trusts you sends pre-screened patients who are already invested in treatment and already comfortable with cash pay (psychiatry is often cash-pay too). Building this network is slow but durable.

**2. Referrals from other therapists.** Therapists who are full, or who do not work in your specialty, refer out constantly. Be the first name they think of in your niche.

**3. Specialty positioning.** Generalists struggle to fill private pay panels. Specialists do not. Trauma-focused PhDs, OCD specialists with ERP training, neurodivergent-affirming clinicians, perinatal psychologists — niche providers can charge full rate because clients seek them out specifically.

**4. Content and SEO.** Writing about your specialty draws clients who are actively searching for help. A psychologist who writes a clear, well-targeted blog about OCD, ADHD, or postpartum depression can build a steady inbound pipeline. This is slow (6-12 months to compound) but builds an asset that keeps producing.

**5. Targeted matching platforms.** Platforms like Psychology Today produce some volume but compete on convenience and copay. A clinical-fit-focused matching platform that prices for private pay can produce higher-quality matches.

**6. Professional reputation and word of mouth.** Speaking at conferences, contributing to professional listservs, training other clinicians. This is the slowest channel and the most powerful one.

What does not consistently work: paid Facebook ads (CPL too high for most metros), Google Ads on generic terms (cost per click is brutal in mental health), Instagram organic (low intent traffic).

## The mixed-panel playbook

Most doctoral providers do not flip from full insurance to full cash overnight. They transition.

The mixed-panel playbook looks like this:

**Phase 1: Stabilize the foundation.** Keep your existing insurance panel (or platform) full while you build private pay channels. Do not drop revenue you have not yet replaced.

**Phase 2: Raise your private pay rate.** If you are accepting some cash pay clients at a discount, raise the rate to your full private pay rate. Stop subsidizing insurance with cheap cash work.

**Phase 3: Open new private pay slots, not insurance slots.** When existing patients terminate, do not fill those slots from your insurance referral source. Fill them with private pay clients from your acquisition channels. Your panel naturally shifts over 6-12 months.

**Phase 4: Tier your insurance panel.** Keep one or two insurance contracts that pay well in your area (often a regional BCBS or a specific HMO). Drop the ones with poor rates or heavy administrative burden. This preserves accessibility for some patients without committing to a low-rate panel.

**Phase 5: Hold a sliding-scale ratio.** Many private-pay psychologists hold 10-20% of slots at reduced or pro bono rates for low-income clients. This is good practice clinically and ethically, and it does not undermine the panel economics because the rest of the panel pays full rate.

This is not a 30-day transition. It is a 12-24 month rebuild. Done well, it produces a panel that is both more financially sustainable and more clinically aligned with the work you actually want to do.

## What about the access problem

The strongest argument against the private-pay pivot is access. If doctoral psychologists move to cash pay, the patients who needed insurance lose access to the most highly trained providers.

This is a real concern, and it should not be dismissed. The counter-argument is also worth hearing:

When Aetna cuts rates 43.6%, the payer is the one reducing access. They are signaling that they no longer want to pay doctoral psychologists for psychotherapy. Providers who leave the panel are responding to that signal, not creating it.

The sustainable answer for access is not for doctoral providers to absorb 43.6% pay cuts indefinitely. That accelerates burnout and pushes more providers out of the field entirely, which makes access worse. The sustainable answer is a system that pays doctoral providers fairly, and where that does not exist, the option to charge fairly outside the system.

Many doctoral providers carry sliding-scale slots. Many do pro bono work. Many supervise trainees who provide reduced-rate care. The private-pay panel does not have to mean abandoning low-income patients. It can mean choosing how to serve them on terms that do not destroy the practice.

## The mental shift

The hardest part of the private-pay pivot is not operational. It is psychological.

Doctoral training does not prepare you to think of yourself as a business owner. The implicit message in graduate school is that money is gauche, that mentioning fees feels mercenary, that "good clinicians" do not care about reimbursement rates.

The Aetna cut is a useful disruption to that frame. When a payer unilaterally cuts your pay by 43.6%, the polite frame becomes unsustainable. You either renegotiate the relationship or you exit it.

Most providers who have made the private-pay transition describe a similar mental arc: initial discomfort with raising rates, surprise that clients still booked, eventual realization that charging full rate did not undermine the therapeutic relationship — it stabilized it. A practice that can sustain itself can sustain the work.

## What to do this week

If you are serious about exploring the private-pay path, here is a minimum first week:

1. **Calculate your true insurance rate.** Pull your last quarter of Alma EOBs (or platform deposits). Divide by hours worked, not sessions, to include admin time. This is your real per-hour insurance rate. It is usually lower than providers expect.

2. **Research private-pay rates in your market.** Check directories for doctoral psychologists in your specialty and metro. Note the rate ranges. Build a realistic target rate.

3. **Identify your specialty.** "Generalist adult therapy" is a hard private-pay sell. "EMDR-trained trauma psychologist" or "neurodivergent-affirming adult ADHD specialist" is much easier. Write down the niche you can credibly claim.

4. **List your current referral sources.** Where do your patients come from now? How many came from the platform versus from professional referral? This is your starting acquisition base.

5. **Reach out to two referral sources.** A psychiatrist, a primary care physician, or a colleague in your network. Tell them you have new private-pay availability. This is how the pipeline starts.

That is one week of work. None of it requires leaving your current panel. None of it requires committing to a full pivot. It builds optionality.

## The Aetna cut as forcing function

There is a version of this story where the Aetna cut is just a cut. Providers absorb it, complain in the forums, and continue.

There is another version where the cut is a forcing function. Doctoral psychologists who had been postponing the private-pay question stop postponing it. Some leave Alma. Some leave insurance entirely. Some build the mixed model.

The aggregate result, if enough providers respond this way, is a shift in the structural balance of the industry. Payers and platforms hold their leverage as long as providers absorb cuts. When providers stop absorbing them, the dynamic changes.

The 2024 Optum cuts already started this shift. The 2026 Aetna cut is accelerating it.

The providers who are best positioned for what comes next are the ones who have already built optionality. They have direct contracts. They have private pay clients. They have referral sources outside any single platform. They are not betting their practice on any one payer's good behavior.

You can build that. It takes time. The right week to start is the week you noticed the cut.
