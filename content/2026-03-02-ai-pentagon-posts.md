## POST 8 — Reddit (r/psychology, r/ArtificialIntelligence)
**Format:** Text post, discussion-starter
**Angle:** Academic framing, invites debate

---

**Title:** The Anthropic-Pentagon dispute is a real-time Milgram experiment — and the results are predictable

**Body:**

The current standoff between Anthropic and the U.S. Department of Defense is getting covered as a tech/policy story. But the underlying dynamics are textbook social psychology.

**Quick summary:** The Pentagon demanded Anthropic remove safeguards against mass surveillance and autonomous weapons. Anthropic refused. Trump blacklisted them. Hours later, OpenAI signed a deal with the Pentagon.

**Here's why this maps onto classic psychological research:**

**Milgram (1963) — Obedience to authority.** 65% of participants administered what they believed were lethal shocks when instructed by an authority figure. The Pentagon's framing — "national security" — is the modern equivalent of Milgram's "the experiment requires that you continue." Anthropic is in the 35%. OpenAI's position is more complex — they claim the same red lines while accepting the contract.

**Bandura — Moral disengagement.** Autonomous weapons activate multiple disengagement mechanisms simultaneously: diffusion of responsibility (the system decided), displacement of responsibility (I was following the system's recommendation), dehumanization (targets become data points), and distortion of consequences (kill metrics replace human outcomes).

**Construal Level Theory — Psychological distance.** Research consistently shows that increasing distance — temporal, spatial, social, or hypothetical — makes moral judgments more utilitarian and less empathic. Autonomous weapons maximize every dimension of distance simultaneously.

**Consumer behavior angle:** The #CancelChatGPT movement and Claude hitting #1 on the App Store maps onto moral identity theory. Product choices become identity signals. People aren't evaluating AI tools on capability — they're evaluating them on values.

**Discussion questions:**
- Is the Milgram parallel too simplistic, or does it capture the core dynamic?
- Does automation bias (humans agreeing with algorithmic recommendations 85-97% of the time) make "human in the loop" meaningfully different from "fully autonomous"?
- What does it mean when product adoption becomes a form of moral signaling?

Interested to hear perspectives from both the psychology and AI sides.
