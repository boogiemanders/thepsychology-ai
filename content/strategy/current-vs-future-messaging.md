# Current Messaging vs Future Roadmap

## Why this exists

The company now has two different kinds of truth:

1. what the product actually does today
2. what the company may become over time

Those truths should not be marketed with the same weight.

If they are, the homepage starts overpromising, the brand gets blurry, and future strategy docs start acting like shipped product pages.

---

## Current truth

Right now, the clearest real product is:

- EPPP prep
- affordable pricing
- realistic practice
- lessons, quizzes, exams, and study support
- founder-story proof from someone who passed

This is what the root homepage should primarily sell.

This is also what the public blog, resources, metadata, and proof stack should reinforce by default.

---

## Future truth

The broader company direction is still valid, but it is still strategy:

1. EPPP prep is the wedge
2. provider growth is the first expansion
3. therapy matching is the demand-side marketplace layer
4. assessment is a later pillar
5. AI-assisted therapy is much later and depends on trust, data, and validation

That roadmap should shape decisions internally, partnerships, and sequencing.

It should not be marketed as if all of it exists today.

---

## Homepage rule

The homepage should reflect current product reality first.

That means:

- the headline can stay EPPP-first
- the problem should stay EPPP-specific
- pricing should describe the actual current offer
- proof should come from EPPP outcomes, founder credibility, and user experience

The homepage may still hint at the bigger vision, but only in a controlled way:

- lower on the page
- in an "about the company" or "where this goes next" section
- without making the unbuilt products sound available now

Good framing:

- "We started with EPPP prep because licensure is the first bottleneck."
- "Over time, the goal is to support psychologists from training into practice."

Bad framing:

- implying the full lifecycle platform already exists
- presenting provider growth, matching, or assessment as mature offers if they are not
- making the root page act like a multi-product hub before the products exist

---

## Subpage rule

Future-facing pages can exist before full launch if they are clearly framed as one of:

- early access
- waitlist
- concept validation
- partnership conversations
- directional positioning

They should not quietly redefine the root brand promise before the underlying product is ready.

---

## Content rule

Use `content/strategy/` for future-state thinking.
Use `content/research/` for evidence and market inputs.
Use `content/founder-drafts/` for exploratory writing that should not drive product messaging by default.

If a doc describes a future offer, that does not automatically mean the homepage should sell it now.

---

## Practical test

Before adding a claim to the homepage, ask:

1. Can a new visitor use this today?
2. Do we have proof for it today?
3. Would a skeptical user feel misled if they clicked expecting this now?

If the answer is no, no, or yes, it belongs in strategy or in a clearly labeled future-facing section, not the main homepage promise.

---

## Expansion readiness

The next platform can be marketed publicly only when it passes three gates:

1. there is a real end-to-end flow, not just a landing page
2. there is some proof from real usage, not just strategy logic
3. there is a clear conversion event and acquisition path for that audience

Recommended order still stands:

1. provider platform
2. client matching
3. assessment later

Do the product work earlier if you want. Do not market it as fully available until those gates are passed.

---

## Current site actions

For the live site right now:

- keep the root homepage EPPP-first
- keep blog and resources EPPP-first by default
- treat `/provider` and `/client` as early-access or pilot pages until there is a real flow behind them
- remove live-service schema or hard availability claims from prelaunch pages
- use those pages to collect signal, not to imply a finished product
