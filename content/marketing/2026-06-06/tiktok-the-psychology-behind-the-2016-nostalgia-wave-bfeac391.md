# The psychology behind the 2016 nostalgia wave

**TikTok script · Pop Culture**

## Draft

[HOOK, 0-3s, talking head]
There is a reason the entire internet is nostalgic for 2016 right now. It is not the music.

[BUILD-UP, 3-8s]
I am a UCLA-trained clinical psychologist, and nostalgia is actually a well-studied coping mechanism. The data explains exactly why these waves hit when they do, and the timing is not random.

[VALUE, 8-50s]
Nostalgia spikes during periods of uncertainty. When the present feels unstable or incoherent, the brain reaches backward.

Research on this is clear: revisiting nostalgic content increases positive mood and reduces negative affect. You are not just scrolling. Your brain is regulating.

The past feels safer because it is already resolved. You know how 2016 turned out. You do not know how 2026 turns out. So your brain compares both versions of you and gravitates toward the one with a known ending.

When you are saving 2016 playlists and rewatching content from that era, that is your nervous system trying to find solid ground.

The catch: nostalgia only works as a short-term reset. If you stay in it too long, it becomes avoidance.

[PAYOFF, 50-55s]
The next time you catch yourself three hours into a 2016 playlist at midnight, that is not nostalgia. That is your nervous system telling you something in your current life needs attention.

[CTA, 55-60s]
Comment 2016 and I will send you the two-minute grounding exercise I give clients when their brain keeps reaching for the past.
