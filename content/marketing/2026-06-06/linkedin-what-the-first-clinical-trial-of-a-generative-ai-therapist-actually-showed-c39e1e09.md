# What the first clinical trial of a generative AI therapist actually showed

**LinkedIn post · Psychology + AI**

> [!warning] Needs human review
> Verify that the NEJM AI trial at doi/10.1056/AIoa2400802 is specifically the Therabot study, and confirm the study population was mild-to-moderate presentations. The limitation claims (chatbots underperform for moderate-to-severe, cannot detect suicidal ideation) are sourced from the Simply Psychology review article -- cross-check against the primary trial.

## Draft

A clinical trial published in NEJM AI found that a fully generative AI chatbot significantly reduced symptoms of depression, anxiety, and eating disorder risk. Here is what to actually make of that.

The study was Therabot, built at Dartmouth. It is the first fully generative AI chatbot to complete a clinical trial. The results were genuine: significant symptom improvements for participants with major depressive disorder, generalized anxiety disorder, and elevated eating disorder risk.

That is meaningful. But context matters before you share this with clients or colleagues.

What the trial does not show: these were mild-to-moderate presentations. Every study comparing AI chatbots to human therapists for moderate-to-severe conditions shows human therapists produce significantly better outcomes.

What no chatbot can reliably do yet: detect suicidal ideation or psychiatric emergencies.

What we do not know: most of these studies are short-term. We have limited data on whether gains hold at six months.

AI tools for mental health are a real option for access gaps and early intervention. They are not a replacement for clinical judgment. Read the actual trial before recommending or dismissing it.
