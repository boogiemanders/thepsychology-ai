## HOOK (0-3s)
[On screen lower third: Dr. Anders Chan, Licensed Psychologist, UCLA]

"There's a real psychological reason why everyone is suddenly obsessed with 2016. And it has nothing to do with Taylor Swift."

## BUILD-UP (3-8s)
"I study memory and emotional regulation. What's happening with this nostalgia trend is actually documented in the research."

"This is not just sentimentality. This is your brain doing its job."

## VALUE (8-50s)
"Nostalgia functions as an affect-regulation strategy. When the present feels unstable or threatening, your brain reaches back to a period it associates with safety."

"2016 was before a lot of the disruption people are carrying right now. So your brain flags it as a stable anchor point."

"The music, the aesthetics, the memes: they are retrieval cues. They trigger the emotional state your brain stored alongside those memories."

"This is the same mechanism that makes one song bring you to tears out of nowhere. Memory and emotion are encoded together."

"And here is what makes the 2016 trend specific: you do not have to have actually loved 2016. Your brain just needs to perceive it as pre-disruption."

## PAYOFF
"So if you have been playing 2016 playlists or rewatching old videos, you are not being sentimental. You are self-regulating. And that is a healthy response to ongoing stress."
