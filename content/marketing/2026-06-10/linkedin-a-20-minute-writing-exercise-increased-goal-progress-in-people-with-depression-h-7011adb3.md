# A 20-minute writing exercise increased goal progress in people with depression history by 49%.

**LinkedIn post · Psychology**

## Draft

A new study tested whether reframing depression as a source of strength could change how people pursue personal goals.

748 adults with a history of clinical depression. Half completed a brief exercise: reading accounts of others who had framed surviving depression as proof of endurance and perseverance, then writing about their own experience.

Two weeks later, the reframing group reported 64% progress on a personal goal. The control group: 43% (Bauer et al., 2026).

What changed was not mood. It changed an implicit belief: that depression and capability are incompatible.

In the control group, 71% felt that the strengths needed to achieve their goals did not describe people with depression. In the reframing group, that dropped to 52%.

This is worth having in the clinical toolbox. The reframe is specific: surviving depression is not evidence of fragility. It is evidence of a kind of endurance that the control group did not recognize in themselves.

The exercise takes 20 minutes. The research suggests the shift in belief carries at least two weeks out.
