# The research on study strategies has been clear since 2013. EPPP prep still ignores it.

**LinkedIn post · EPPP**

## Draft

The most rigorous review of study techniques ever published rated 10 common strategies by how well the evidence supported them.

Practice testing: high utility. Distributed practice: high utility.

Rereading: low utility. Highlighting: low utility (Dunlosky et al., 2013).

Those two low-utility strategies are the ones most postdocs reach for first.

This is not about effort. Most candidates preparing for the EPPP are working hard. The problem is method.

The EPPP does not test recognition. It tests applied reasoning under timed pressure, using a vignette that gives you no cues about which construct applies. The only way to prepare for that is to practice retrieving knowledge from nothing.

Reading the chapter trains you to recognize information when prompted. Retrieval practice trains you to produce it without a prompt. Those are different cognitive skills, and the exam rewards only one.

One practical shift: before you review content, try to recall it first. Write down what you remember. Then check. The gap between what you thought you knew and what you can actually produce without prompts is your real study plan.
