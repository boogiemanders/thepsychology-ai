# A randomized trial just tested an AI therapist. Here is what clinicians need to know.

**LinkedIn post · Psychology + AI**

## Draft

A peer-reviewed RCT published in NEJM AI tested an AI chatbot called Therabot for mental health treatment. Results showed symptom improvements for depression and anxiety.

Before the headlines run too far: here is what else the research shows.

16% of American adults have already used AI tools for mental health support in the past year. Among people with mental health conditions using AI, nearly half are doing so for psychological assistance.

What AI chatbots do well: psychoeducation, mild symptom support, reaching people who would never book a session.

What they cannot do: reliably detect suicidal ideation or psychiatric emergencies. Maintain treatment gains past eight weeks. Replicate therapeutic alliance, which remains the strongest outcome predictor we have.

Dr. John Torous put it plainly: AI "has no empathy. It doesn't know what you're feeling."

Massachusetts is already considering legislation restricting AI-delivered therapy to licensed professionals.

For clinicians, the question is not whether to ignore AI. It is how to position it correctly within a treatment framework, the same way you would position psychoeducation or a self-help workbook.

The tool is real. The hype is ahead of the evidence.
