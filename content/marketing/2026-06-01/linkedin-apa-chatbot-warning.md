## Draft

The American Psychological Association recently issued a health advisory on the use of generative AI chatbots and wellness apps for mental health.

The concern is clinical, not theoretical. Millions of people are turning to these tools for emotional support, often without knowing whether the tool has been validated, regulated, or tested for harm.

As licensed clinicians, that puts us in a specific position.

Our clients are using these tools whether we ask or not. A 2026 study in JMIR Mental Health, centered on people with lived experience of depression, found they evaluated AI chatbots on three priorities: actionable information, emotional validation with clear limits, and personalization. That is exactly what good therapy delivers. They are turning to AI because access to us is limited.

That gap is the real clinical problem.

The APA advisory is worth reading if you have not. The evidence base for AI in mental health is early, uneven, and heavily shaped by industry funding. Your clients may ask you about these tools directly. You deserve a grounded answer, not just a hedge.

What are you telling your clients when they bring up AI chatbots?
