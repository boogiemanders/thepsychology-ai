# Your Patients Are Already Using AI Between Sessions

**LinkedIn post · Psychology + AI**

## Draft

Clinicians are the most discerning group in the room when a chatbot enters the conversation. That is not a bias. It is a trained skill.

The APA surveyed more than 1,200 licensed psychologists in 2026. A few numbers worth holding.

77 percent have spoken with patients who used AI for mental health support. 39 percent have seen patients self-diagnose with AI. 33 percent report patients using chatbots to assist with therapy. 36 percent have noticed patients developing dependency on chatbots. And 15 percent observed distorted thinking tied to chatbot interaction.

94 percent say chatbots cannot treat mental health conditions with appropriate nuance (American Psychological Association, 2026).

That last number is the clinical consensus. Not a turf defense. A professional judgment from the people doing the work daily.

The question is not whether AI will affect clinical practice. It already has, in what clients bring through the door. The question is how to hold that material without dismissing the client's experience or validating something that may be making things worse.

That is a clinical skill most training programs never built in.

When a patient shares something an AI told them about themselves, how do you handle it in session?
