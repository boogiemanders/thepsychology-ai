# EPPP Changes 2027: What Candidates Studying Right Now Actually Need to Know

If you have been searching for answers about EPPP changes in 2027, you are looking at the right question. The past three years have produced pauses, reversals, new timelines, and a lot of conflicting information online. Here is a plain-language breakdown of what actually happened, what is actually on the table, and what it means for candidates studying right now.

The short answer: the EPPP you are studying for today is unchanged. But the structure of psychology licensure is under active revision, and the 2027 window matters if you plan to test in late 2027 or later.

## What Is the EPPP and What Has It Always Been?

The EPPP is the national licensing exam built by ASPPB and administered by Pearson Vue. It tests general psychology knowledge across the major content domains and is required for psychology licensure in virtually all US states and Canadian provinces (California Board of Psychology, 2026).

The current Part 1 exam is a 225-item multiple-choice test covering domains including biopsychology, learning and memory, social psychology, assessment, diagnosis, clinical interventions, research and statistics, and ethics. That exam has not changed.

## What Was EPPP Part 2, and What Happened to It?

ASPPB was developing a second exam called the EPPP2, formally the Integrated Examination for Professional Practice in Psychology. The goal was to add a skills-based assessment alongside the knowledge exam, testing not just what candidates know but whether they can demonstrate applied clinical competency (California Board of Psychology, 2026).

In May 2023, the California Board of Psychology approved rules to add an EPPP Part 2 Skills Exam with a planned launch date of January 1, 2026. Then in October 2024, ASPPB paused that plan, and the California Board staff paused their related rule work. ASPPB later abandoned the EPPP2 entirely, which forced the Board to revisit what it had previously approved (California Board of Psychology, 2026).

For a fuller explanation of what EPPP Part 2 was and how it differed from Part 1, see [EPPP vs EPPP-2: What's the Difference?](/blog/eppp-vs-eppp-2).

## Why Was It Abandoned?

The California Board meeting materials from February 2026 document the downstream effects but do not detail ASPPB's full reasoning. What is clear is that the abandonment was abrupt and forced state boards, including California, to revisit rules they had already passed.

The broader picture from the February meeting: the California Psychological Association sponsored AB 282 to allow candidates to test after finishing all coursework, and advocated for states rather than ASPPB to control exam prerequisites. The underlying tension was about who has authority over when and how candidates test (California Board of Psychology, 2026).

## What Is the 2027 Timeline About?

Following the abandonment of the original EPPP2 plan, new anticipated implementation dates were pushed to 2027. The California Board discussed how a 2027 launch of some form of exam changes might proceed (California Board of Psychology, 2026).

2027 is the earliest possible implementation window to watch. It is not a confirmed launch date for any specific change. No new exam format has been officially approved or announced in the California Board materials reviewed here.

This means candidates planning to test in 2026 or early 2027 are working under a stable set of requirements. Candidates who plan to test in late 2027 or 2028 should watch for updates from their state board directly.

## What About Eligibility Rules?

One of the more consequential discussions from the February 2026 Board meeting involved eligibility, specifically when candidates can sit for the exam.

Currently in California, candidates can sit for the EPPP after finishing their coursework, before completing all post-doctoral internship hours. A proposed eligibility change would require candidates to finish all post-doctoral internship hours before testing. ASPPB had originally wanted this tied to the EPPP2 requirements. With the EPPP2 abandoned, the eligibility conversation continues separately (California Board of Psychology, 2026).

If this eligibility change passes, future candidates may have to complete their full post-doctoral hours before they can schedule the exam. That would meaningfully shift timelines for anyone currently in internship and considering testing early.

**This is not settled policy.** Watch your state board, not social media, for updates.

## What the Pass Rates Say

The 2025 California pass rates provide important context for all of this. The overall pass rate was 36.5%. First-time candidates passed at 58.7%. Of 819 first-time candidates, 480 passed. The 2025 rates trended lower than the 2021 to 2024 range (California Board of Psychology, 2026).

One of the goals behind the original eligibility change discussion was to let people test sooner after coursework, on the theory that earlier testing might improve pass rates. The data does not yet support that this would move the needle. But it shows why the board is motivated to keep revising the policy (California Board of Psychology, 2026).

For more on how to read pass rate data and what it means for your preparation, see [EPPP Pass Rates: What the 2026 Data Actually Shows](/blog/eppp-pass-rates-2026-update).

## What Should You Do If You Are Studying Right Now?

**Study for the exam that exists.** Nothing in the 2027 discussion changes what you need to master to pass the Part 1 exam in front of you today. The content, format, and scoring are unchanged.

**If you are early in your training, watch eligibility.** If you have not yet completed your post-doctoral hours and plan to test in late 2027 or later, the eligibility rules may shift. Stay in direct contact with your state board for the most current requirements.

**Do not use the uncertainty as a reason to delay.** Candidates sometimes treat a cloudy policy landscape as permission to wait. That is the wrong trade. The methodology for passing the EPPP has not changed, and your score does not benefit from waiting.

**Focus on what predicts passing.** No structural change to licensure requirements changes what has always driven first-time pass rates: targeted practice by domain weakness, active recall over passive reading, and consistent moderate hours without burning out. For a detailed breakdown of the study method that works, see [How to Pass the EPPP on Your First Try](/blog/how-to-pass-the-eppp-first-try).

## The Bottom Line

The EPPP2 was planned, paused, and abandoned. A 2027 implementation window for some form of change is on the table, but nothing is confirmed. The exam you are studying for right now is the same exam it has always been.

Watch for updates from your state board directly. The California Board of Psychology meeting materials are the most reliable primary source for where licensure policy is actually heading. Social media speculation is not.

If you are actively preparing, your job is straightforward: find your weakest domains, prioritize your study time toward them, and practice actively. The policy uncertainty is real. Your study plan does not need to reflect it.
