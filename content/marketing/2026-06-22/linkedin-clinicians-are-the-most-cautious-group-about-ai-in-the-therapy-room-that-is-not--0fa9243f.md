# Clinicians are the most cautious group about AI in the therapy room. That is not a deficiency.

**LinkedIn post · Psychology + AI**

## Draft

Clinicians are the most cautious group about using AI in the therapy room. That is not a deficiency.

A 2026 APA survey found that 77% of psychologists have already spoken with patients who brought AI into the clinical relationship. Patients are using chatbots between sessions, sometimes instead of reaching out. Yet 44% of psychologists have still never used AI to assist their own practice, down from 71% who said the same in 2024 (American Psychological Association, 2026).

That gap is not a failure to adapt. It is professional judgment that rarely gets named as such.

The questions senior clinicians are sitting with are legitimate clinical questions. What happens when a patient forms a therapeutic alliance with a chatbot that has no legal or ethical obligations to them? Who holds the frame when AI advice contradicts the treatment plan? What does informed consent look like when the patient is running a parallel process you do not know about?

Of the clinicians who do use AI, 52% use it for drafting emails. Almost none use it anywhere near the clinical encounter. That boundary is not technophobia. It is discernment.

What is driving your caution, or your openness, about AI in practice?
