# Clinicians are the least guided group on what to do when a patient brings AI to therapy

**LinkedIn post · Psychology + AI**

## Draft

Clinicians are the least guided group on what to do when a patient shows up having used an AI as their therapist. That is not their failing. It is the profession's.

More than a third of psychologists now report patients who treat AI as an additional mental health professional (American Psychological Association, 2026). The clinical question is real. The framework is not.

What research shows: AI chatbots can reduce mild symptoms but do not outperform human therapists for moderate-to-severe depression or anxiety (JAMA, 2026). They cannot reliably detect suicidal ideation.

What most clients do not know: the warmth is engineered. The model is trained to respond to distress in ways that feel attuned. That is not the same as attuned.

Three things clinicians are doing with this in session right now:

1. Asking about it at intake, treating it like any self-help behavior.
2. Using the chatbot content as clinical material. What did it say? What did you want it to say?
3. Reading the disclosure as relational data. The fact that they told you says something.

None of that is in any practice guideline yet.

When a patient first told you they were using an AI for emotional support, what was your clinical instinct?
