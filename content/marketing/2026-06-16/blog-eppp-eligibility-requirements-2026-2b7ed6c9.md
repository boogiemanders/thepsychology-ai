# EPPP Eligibility Requirements: When You Can Test and What Is Changing in 2027

**Blog post · EPPP**

## Draft

EPPP eligibility requirements vary by state, but one question comes up constantly from candidates: can I take the exam before I finish my post-doc? The answer has always been complicated. Right now it is getting more so, because California and the national licensing body are actively debating a change that could affect when you are allowed to test.

Here is what the rules are today, what is on the table for 2027, and what it means if you are studying right now.

## What EPPP Eligibility Requires Today

The EPPP (Examination for Professional Practice in Psychology) is built by ASPPB and administered through Pearson VUE. Most states require candidates to:

- Hold a doctoral degree from an accredited psychology program (or be within a specified distance of completion)
- Meet their state's supervised hours requirements (these vary widely)
- Apply through their state licensing board, which sets the final eligibility criteria

In California specifically, you can currently apply to take the EPPP after finishing your coursework, which means you do not have to wait until you have completed all of your post-doctoral supervised hours. That flexibility matters. For many candidates, testing while in their post-doc, before work and supervision demands peak, is the practical window for exam preparation.

Other states have different rules. Some require post-doc completion before testing; some allow testing earlier. ASPPB issues the exam, but your state board sets the eligibility gate.

## Why California's Rules Are Under Review

In 2023 and 2024, ASPPB was building a second exam called the EPPP Part 2 (or EPPP2), designed as a skills-based component to run alongside the existing knowledge exam. As part of that plan, ASPPB proposed blocking candidates from testing until all post-doctoral internship hours were complete, a significant shift from how California currently handles eligibility (California Board of Psychology, 2026).

At the same time, California's EPPP pass rates were declining. The overall pass rate for 2025 was 36.5%, with a first-time pass rate of 58.7%, trending lower than 2021 through 2024 (California Board of Psychology, 2026). The Board and the California Psychological Association (CPA) looked at those numbers and reached the opposite conclusion from ASPPB: earlier testing, not later, might improve pass rates by giving candidates a better window for focused preparation before post-doc responsibilities take over.

CPA sponsored AB 282, a bill to explicitly allow candidates to test after finishing all required coursework, and to shift control over exam prerequisites from ASPPB back to the individual states (California Board of Psychology, 2026).

The two positions are essentially the reverse of each other:

- **ASPPB's original proposal**: candidates must complete all post-doctoral hours before testing
- **CPA's AB 282 position**: candidates can test after completing coursework, and states, not ASPPB, should set that threshold

Neither position has been finalized. The Board was actively reviewing both at its February 13, 2026 meeting.

## What Happened to the EPPP2

ASPPB abandoned the EPPP2 abruptly after years of development, which forced the California Board to revisit the rule changes it had already approved in anticipation of a two-part exam (California Board of Psychology, 2026). The EPPP you are studying for today, the single knowledge-based exam with 225 multiple-choice questions across eight domains, is unchanged.

The 2027 date that circulates online refers to the Board's discussion of a possible future window for eligibility rule implementation. It is not a confirmed date for a new exam or a confirmed date for changed eligibility rules. It is the earliest possible window the Board was tracking as of February 2026 (California Board of Psychology, 2026).

## What the 2027 Window Actually Means for Candidates

If you are studying now or plan to test in 2025 or 2026, none of the proposed changes affect you. The exam content is the same, the passing score is the same, and your current state's eligibility rules apply.

If you are early in your post-doc or still in your doctoral program, 2027 is a window worth watching. Two scenarios are plausible:

1. Eligibility rules stay roughly the same, perhaps with clearer state-level control over when candidates can test
2. Eligibility rules tighten in some states to require post-doc completion before testing, which would change the planning window for candidates who currently test mid-post-doc

Neither scenario changes the content of the exam or what you need to know to pass it.

## What to Do If You Are Studying Now

The clearest advice is: test under the rules you have. If your state currently allows you to test before your post-doc is complete, that window is open and not closing in 2026.

For California candidates specifically:

- The current eligibility rules remain in place while AB 282 and the Board's review are ongoing
- The 2027 implementation window is the earliest any rule change could take effect
- Nothing from the February 2026 Board meeting requires immediate action for candidates already in preparation

The practical implication: if you are in your post-doc now and have been delaying testing while watching the EPPP2 news, the EPPP2 no longer exists. The exam is stable. There is no strategic reason to wait.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I take the EPPP before completing my post-doc?**
In California, yes, under current rules. In other states, it depends on your state board's specific requirements. Check directly with your state licensing board, as these rules vary and are not set by ASPPB alone.

**Is the EPPP changing in 2027?**
No confirmed changes to the exam itself. 2027 is the earliest possible window for eligibility rule changes the California Board was discussing as of February 2026. The exam content, format, and scoring are unchanged.

**Does the EPPP2 still exist?**
No. ASPPB abandoned the EPPP2. The EPPP remains a single-part exam. See [EPPP vs EPPP-2: What's the Difference](/blog/eppp-vs-eppp-2) for the full history.

**What is California's current EPPP pass rate?**
The 2025 overall pass rate was 36.5%. The first-time pass rate was 58.7%. Both are trending lower than prior years. See [California EPPP Pass Rates](/blog/eppp-pass-rates) for the full data.
