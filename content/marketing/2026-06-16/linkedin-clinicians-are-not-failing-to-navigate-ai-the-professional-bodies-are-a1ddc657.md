# Clinicians are not failing to navigate AI. The professional bodies are.

**LinkedIn post · Psychology + AI**

## Draft

Clinicians are among the most ethically trained professionals in any field. That is not what is protecting most of them from AI's risks right now.

A 2026 paper in Frontiers in Psychology found that professional associations have been slow to issue comprehensive guidance on AI in clinical work, leaving practitioners to navigate new territory without a collective framework. The practical result: the defaults governing AI-assisted documentation or session tools are often the ones the software vendor chose, not ones a professional body reviewed.

This is a structural gap, not a clinical failure. Most psychologists using AI are doing exactly what their training says: reading carefully, going slowly, asking the right questions. They are doing it without institutional backing.

What I see among clinicians navigating this well: they are not waiting. They are building working frameworks practice by practice. Same questions they would apply to any clinical tool. What is this for? What are the risks to the client? How do I document my reasoning?

That is exactly the right instinct. It is also a lot to work out case by case without collective guidance.

What does your current framework for AI in clinical practice look like? Have you talked through this with colleagues, or is it mostly individual navigation?
