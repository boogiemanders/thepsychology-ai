# People are trusting AI with their feelings. Here is what the research says clinicians should know.

**LinkedIn post · Psychology + AI**

## Draft

A WBUR report from May 2026 framed it plainly: many people now trust AI with their feelings. And therapists want to talk about it.

The clinical evidence is catching up. Dartmouth's Therabot just became the subject of the first published randomized controlled trial of a fully generative AI chatbot. The results showed significant symptom improvements in major depressive disorder, generalized anxiety disorder, and elevated eating disorder risk.

That is real evidence. Worth taking seriously.

But the same evidence base draws clear lines. For moderate-to-severe presentations, human therapists consistently outperform chatbots. AI cannot reliably detect suicidal ideation or psychiatric emergencies. Most studies are short-term, with limited data on gains beyond eight weeks.

People are reaching for these tools because access is a real problem. The wait for a human therapist can stretch months. A chatbot is available at 2am.

As clinicians, we do not get to dismiss these tools. We get to define what they are actually good for, and where the line is. The research gives us an honest answer. We should use it.
