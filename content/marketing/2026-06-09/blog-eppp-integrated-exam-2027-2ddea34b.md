## Draft

The EPPP integrated exam is one of the most discussed changes in psychology licensing in years. If you are currently studying for your licensing exam, the headlines have probably reached you: the exam is changing in 2027. New format. New domains. New question types.

Before you rethink your study plan, read this.

The short version: if you are testing before Q4 2027, the exam you are preparing for has not changed. But understanding what is coming matters, whether you are in active prep now or planning your timeline two years out.

## What the Integrated EPPP Actually Is

For decades, the EPPP has been a knowledge-based exam: 225 questions, multiple choice, covering 8 content domains. The Association of State and Provincial Psychology Boards (ASPPB) has committed to replacing it with a single integrated exam that tests both knowledge and applied skills together.

This is not the EPPP2. The EPPP Part 2 (also called the EPPP2 or Skills Exam) was a separate exam proposed for a January 2026 launch, paused in October 2024, and ultimately abandoned (California Board of Psychology, 2026). The integrated EPPP is a different concept: one exam, one sitting, combining what the two-part structure would have separated.

ASPPB approved the new direction in December 2025 and released content specifications in February 2026. The exam is expected to be operational in Q4 2027 (ASPPB, 2026).

## What Is Changing

Based on ASPPB's released specifications, the integrated exam will differ from the current exam in three main ways.

**Format.** Single-day administration at Pearson testing centers. The new exam includes single-best-answer multiple-choice questions, scenario-based question sets, and audio and video question portions. This is a meaningfully different testing experience from the current format.

**Content domains.** The current EPPP covers 8 domains. The integrated exam will assess competencies across 6 content domain areas, with the specific domain names and weightings outlined in ASPPB's February 2026 blueprint (ASPPB, 2026).

**Scoring.** Pass/fail based on a total score across the exam rather than separate component scores.

## What Is Not Changing

The core competencies are not going away. Clinical reasoning, ethical practice, assessment, treatment, biological and social foundations. These are what psychology licensing has always tested. The integrated format tests them in a way that more directly assesses how you apply knowledge under realistic conditions.

If you are currently studying: the content areas, the study methods that work, and the clinical skills you need to pass are unchanged. Diagnostic-first preparation, active recall, and targeted focus on your weakest domains remain the correct approach.

## Why the EPPP Is Changing

California's pass rate data gives context. In 2025, the overall California EPPP pass rate was 36.5%. The first-time pass rate was 58.7%. Pass rates have been trending lower than any year from 2021 through 2024 (California Board of Psychology, 2026).

The original EPPP2 proposal was partly motivated by this: ASPPB believed the knowledge exam alone was not fully assessing readiness for professional practice. The integrated exam is the revised answer to that same concern, packaged in a single sitting rather than two separate exams.

There is also a parallel eligibility question. California's AB 282, sponsored by the California Psychological Association, would let candidates sit the EPPP after finishing doctoral coursework rather than requiring post-doctoral hour completion, and would put eligibility decisions with states rather than ASPPB (California Board of Psychology, 2026). If this passes, when you can test may also shift.

## What to Do If You Are Testing Before 2027

Nothing changes about your current study plan. Prepare for the exam that exists.

The most common reason candidates fail the current EPPP is not unfamiliarity with the format. It is content gaps in two or three specific domains. The [EPPP pass rate data](https://www.thepsychology.ai/blog/eppp-pass-rates) shows this clearly: the gap between first-time and repeat candidates points to a method problem, not a knowledge problem.

The approach that consistently works:

1. Take a diagnostic before you open a textbook. You need data on where you are actually weak, not where you assume you are weak.
2. Weight your study time. For each domain, multiply your weakness score by the domain's exam weight. The highest-scoring domains get 70 percent of your hours.
3. Use practice questions, not passive reading. For how to find and use high-quality practice material, see our guide to [free EPPP practice questions](https://www.thepsychology.ai/blog/free-eppp-practice-questions).
4. Study at 6 to 8 focused hours per week rather than unsustainable 15-20 hour weeks. For a realistic schedule while working full-time, see [how long to study for the EPPP](https://www.thepsychology.ai/blog/how-long-to-study-for-the-eppp).

The first-time pass rate in California is 58.7%. Candidates who pass on the first attempt consistently describe the same preparation: diagnostic-driven, quiz-heavy, targeted to actual gaps.

## What to Watch If You Are Testing in 2027 or Later

ASPPB released the content blueprint in February 2026. Between now and the Q4 2027 launch, watch for:

- Sample testing opportunities before the full launch (ASPPB has indicated these will be available)
- Updated prep materials built for the new format and question types
- State-by-state eligibility guidance, including the outcome of AB 282 in California

ASPPB's official website is the only reliable source for timeline updates. Information from prep companies will lag their announcements.

## The Bottom Line

The EPPP is changing. The integrated exam will assess both knowledge and applied skills in a more realistic format than the current exam. For candidates testing before Q4 2027, the current exam is your focus. For trainees planning further out, understanding the direction helps you build clinical competency now that will carry into the new format.

What does not change: clinical reasoning is clinical reasoning. Study it well and the format question becomes secondary.
