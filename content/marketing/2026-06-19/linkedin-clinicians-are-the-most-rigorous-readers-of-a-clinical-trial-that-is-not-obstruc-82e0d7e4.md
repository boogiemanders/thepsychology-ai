## Draft

Clinicians are the most rigorous readers of a clinical trial. That is not obstructionism.

This week, NEJM AI published what researchers are calling the first randomized controlled trial of a fully generative AI chatbot for clinical-level depression, anxiety, and high-risk psychotic experiences (NEJM AI, 2024).

Coverage calls it proof that AI therapy works.

The clinician's instinct is to ask: what was the control condition? Who was excluded? What happened at follow-up?

Those are not defensive questions. They are the product of evidence-based training. A finding that cannot survive those questions is not a finding. It is a press release.

What makes this study worth tracking is the population gap. AI chatbots are most commonly used by people who have no access to a clinician (JAMA, 2025). If the trial excluded the highest-risk patients, the effectiveness data is technically accurate and practically limited at the same time.

The study adds a data point. It does not resolve the question the field is actually holding.

What is the one question you would want answered about this RCT before it informed your practice?
