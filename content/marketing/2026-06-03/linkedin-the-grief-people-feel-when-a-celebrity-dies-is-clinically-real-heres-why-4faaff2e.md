## Draft

When a celebrity dies, people who have never met them post responses that look, clinically, almost identical to personal grief.

That's not dysfunction. It's a recognized psychological phenomenon, first described by researchers Horton and Wohl in 1956. They called it the parasocial relationship: the one-sided connection that forms when repeated exposure to a media figure causes the brain to register them as socially present.

When that figure dies, the loss is real. The emotional experience tracks the same mechanisms as losing someone you actually knew, at a lower intensity.

Here's the clinical application: some clients will minimize this grief because they feel embarrassed by it. They expect you to confirm that it does not count.

It counts. The mechanism is legitimate. Normalize it and move on to the function it's serving.

What's shifting in 2026: AI-generated content keeps deceased or absent public figures present in feeds well after they are gone. The parasocial relationship does not end. That creates a grief process without a clear endpoint, which is genuinely new clinical territory.

Worth knowing as this becomes more common.
