## HOOK (0-3s)

"Everyone is worried about AI replacing therapists. That's the wrong question."
[CONTROVERSY -- bold reframe forces viewer to resolve the tension; they scroll to find the right question]

## BUILD-UP (3-10s)

"Generative AI just hit 53% population adoption in 3 years, according to the Stanford AI Index 2026. Faster than the personal computer. Faster than the internet. So if you work in mental health, the question is not whether to pay attention to this. It's what to actually pay attention to."
[AUTHORITY ENDORSEMENT -- Stanford AI Index as credibility anchor]
[BURNING QUESTION -- what SHOULD clinicians actually focus on?]

## VALUE (10-48s)

"Here's what the research actually shows.

AI chatbots are good at one specific thing: delivering mental health education. Psychoeducation on CBT, cognitive distortions, emotional regulation skills. They do that reliably.

What they cannot do: detect a psychiatric emergency. The research is direct on this. Current AI therapy tools cannot reliably identify suicidal ideation. That is not a gap that gets fixed next year. It is a structural limitation of systems that process text and generate responses.

So the right question for a clinician is not: will AI replace me. The question is: what specifically do I do that AI cannot do? Assessment. Therapeutic relationship. Crisis response. Adaptive treatment planning that responds to what is actually happening in the room.

That is where your training pays off. That is where you are genuinely irreplaceable."
[EXTREME VALUE -- reframes the AI threat into a specific clinical framework; secret info framing positions this as inside knowledge]

## PAYOFF

"AI is not competing with the clinical relationship. It's competing with the parts of this job that were already rote. Know the difference and you will know exactly where to focus your development."
[PAYOFF -- resolves the hook's reframe; gives clinicians clarity instead of anxiety]
