## Draft

Clinicians are the most precise about what AI misses in a therapy session. That is not technophobia.

An April 2026 APA survey of more than 1,200 licensed psychologists found that 94% say today's chatbots cannot treat mental health conditions with sufficient nuance. 89% said they may inadvertently encourage self-harm (American Psychological Association, 2026).

That same period, research in AJMC reported that 19.2% of US youth have already used AI chatbots for mental health advice, up from 13.1% two years earlier (AJMC, 2026).

The clients are not waiting for our professional consensus.

Here is the practical challenge: not whether AI belongs in mental health, but what to do when someone walks into your office having already received support from a chatbot.

Three things I think belong in a clinical intake now:

1. Ask about AI use. Normalize it, do not shame it.
2. Review what the chatbot said. It may have reinforced a cognitive distortion or missed an indirect signal of risk.
3. Treat the chatbot interaction as clinical data. It tells you the client's language, their framing, and what felt safer to say to a machine first.

The chatbot was in the room before you were. That is the clinical reality in 2026.
