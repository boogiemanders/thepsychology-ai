## Draft

Clinicians are the most ambivalent professionals about AI therapy tools. That is not a weakness in the field. That is the field doing its job.

A report in JAMA found that millions of people in the US are now turning to AI chatbots for mental health support. Chatbots have become one of the most common providers of talk therapy in the country (JAMA, 2026).

The reason is access, not preference. Many people cannot get to a licensed clinician. The wait times are long. The cost is real. The chatbot is available at 2 AM.

Clinicians who dismiss this entirely are missing something important: your clients are already using these tools between sessions. They just may not be telling you.

The ambivalence is useful when it stays calibrated. Not "AI therapy is fine as-is" and not "AI therapy is harmful full stop." The useful question is more specific: which functions are chatbots suited for, which require a human, and what does the handoff look like?

That is a clinical judgment question. Which means it belongs with you, not with a regulation.

What are you doing when a client mentions they have been using a therapy chatbot?
