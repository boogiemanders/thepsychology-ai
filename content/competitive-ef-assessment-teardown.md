# Competitive Intelligence: Executive Functioning Assessment Pain Points

## Purpose
Research compilation of real clinician and patient complaints about current executive functioning assessments (CPT-3, TOVA, Pearson/Q-interactive, general neuropsych). Goal: identify what to build by understanding what's broken.

---

## 1. CONNERS CPT-3 — Weak Points

### Diagnostic Accuracy (The Killer Stat)
- **Only 51.7% of ADHD patients correctly classified** — systematic review across 7 studies
- **Inattentive ADHD: 80.3% classification error rate** — worse than a coin flip
- Hyperactive/mixed: 22.5% error rate — still clinically unacceptable
- Called a "weak or poor predictor" of ADHD in 5/7 studies

### Performance Validity
- Only 2 of 10 embedded validity scores reached "minimally acceptable" accuracy (AUC >= 0.70)
- Rest had sensitivity of just 0.19-0.41 at 0.90 specificity
- Methodology criticized for "mistaking true impairment for noncredible presentation"

### Treatment Monitoring
- "Not the most reliable tool for treatment monitoring — findings should be interpreted with caution"

### Sources
- ADDitude Magazine, Systematic Review in Archives of Clinical Neuropsychology (2023), CPT-3 Embedded Validity Indicators Study (PubMed)

## 2. TOVA — Weak Points

### Software & Technical (Devastating)
- **Windows updates repeatedly break TOVA** — KB5012170 blocked Precision Test Environment on Win 8/10/11. First reported Aug 2022, still being patched July 2024 (2-year ongoing issue)
- **Reboots the entire computer** into a dedicated "Precision Test Environment" — fragile, confusing for non-technical clinicians
- **Requires proprietary hardware**: USB device, physical microswitch with photodiode, VGA cables (VGA is legacy tech in 2026)
- Hardware is not user-serviceable — must be returned to The TOVA Company for repair
- Some laptops lack sufficient bass response for auditory test, requiring external speakers

### Cost
- Kit: **$1,095** (includes only 5 test credits)
- Per-test: **~$30** ($15 credit + $15 interpretive report)
- Upgrade v7/8 to v9: **$495** (must return old hardware)
- Patient cost: **$200-400** per administration, not covered by insurance
- Pricing hidden behind order forms

### Validity (Clinician Quotes from Student Doctor Network)
- Board-certified neuropsychologist WisNeuro: *"There are literally no standalone tests that are clinically useful in ADHD testing. The sens/spec is god-awful terrible."*
- *"Particularly with its poor validity metrics and unacceptable false positive rates."*
- Neuropsychologist AcronymAllergy: *"Computerized testing gives the illusion of objective/'hard' data when in reality, the results are far from being especially helpful."*
- Psychologist R. Matey: *"The literature is pretty clear that the CPT has little, if any, added value."*
- SDN consensus: using CPTs as primary ADHD indicators is *"just sheer incompetence."*
- **30% false positive rate** in normal controls
- **28% false positive rate** in children with other psychiatric disorders
- ROC scores for omission/commission errors: **not significantly above chance** — barely better than a coin flip
- Cannot discriminate ADHD from depression, anxiety, ASD, or other conditions

### Normative Data (Indefensible)
- Adult norms from **1993**, only **250 subjects**, **99% Caucasian**, all from three Minnesota liberal arts colleges
- Child norms also primarily Caucasian from Minneapolis
- **Does not account for IQ variation** — their own manual acknowledges this
- Never updated to reflect current demographics

### Reliability
- **30% of typically developing youth** showed score changes exceeding 90% confidence intervals on retest
- Test-retest ICC as low as **.33 for T-scores** — essentially unreliable for individual decisions

### Patient Experience
- Intentionally boring 21.6-minute clicking task
- False negatives in ADHD patients who can hyperfocus on computer tasks
- False positives from anxiety/panic during testing
- Cannot be administered remotely (major post-COVID limitation)

### Faking
- Students "can easily feign symptoms of ADHD" on the TOVA

## 3. PEARSON / Q-INTERACTIVE — Weak Points

### Q-Interactive (iPad Platform) — Real App Store Reviews

> "Breakdowns in communication between iPads, inexplicable blank screens, freezes during timed subtests, wonky editing glitches" — 1-star, Nov 2025

> "It crashes, it fails to log in. And the customer service department is the worst!" — 1-star, Jul 2025

> "A poorly tested, buggy mess" — 1-star

> "The student's scores may not be the best representation of the child's ability because of technical issues" — clinician forced to write this in reports

> "Given how much money you make off of us, you could get this right" — 2-star

**Specific technical failures:**
- iPads disconnect and reconnect every few seconds during testing
- Blank screens on student iPads during timed tests
- App freezes periodically, "threatening standardization"
- Deleting app to fix login = destroys all old assessment data
- ~4GB storage consumption, causes iPad sluggishness
- Once a test starts, you cannot back out — you pay for tests you didn't administer

### Q-Global (Scoring Platform)
> "Q-Global is a glitchy, unreliable mess" — Student Doctor Network

- Clinicians report "scoring things by hand rather than using it because it was constantly crashing"
- Historical CVLT-II scoring software errors persisted unfixed for extended periods
- Picture Span re-norming issues on iPad versions

### Business Model
- **"Black box" scoring**: *"I don't want to administer anything wherein I cannot control the data and double check the scoring"*
- Moving toward subscription models — gender-corrected norms now require yearly subscriptions
- Per-administration fees ON TOP of annual license costs
- Concerns about Pearson data-mining clinical datasets
- Platform fragmentation — for a single dementia eval, clinicians need multiple different platforms simultaneously

### Test Validity Concerns
- Coding tasks converted from writing to multiple-choice "completely changes the nature of the test"
- Insufficient validation of digital vs. paper equivalence
- Research supporting equivalence "mostly comes from Pearson itself"
- Hardware/software input tracking questioned for fine motor tasks

### Professional Threat
- Expanding digital testing to master's-level and teacher administration threatens doctoral-level psychologist value
- Fears publishers will automate report writing with AI

## 5. SYSTEMIC PROBLEMS (All Executive Function Assessment)

### For Clinicians

**Report writing is a black hole:**
- Pediatric neuropsych: 5-10 hours writing an 11-page report
- Adult neuropsych: 2-3 hours for a 6-page report
- 73% of referral sources say slow turnaround negatively affects patient care
- Clinicians believe referral sources don't even read the full report
- Total professional time per evaluation: 10-20 hours

**Hand scoring chaos:**
- No universal labeling system — a standard score of 75 is labeled differently by every publisher:
  - "borderline" (Wechsler adult)
  - "very low" (WRAT-5, WISC-5)
  - "well below average" (KSNAP)
  - "low" (WJ-IV)
  - "poor" (TONI-3)
  - "below average to mildly impaired" (BQSS)
- "Examiner drift" — clinicians unknowingly modify administration/scoring over time
- Wide variability on "when, what, and how much help to provide during testing"

**Paper persistence:**
- Majority still prefer paper-and-pencil despite decades of computerization
- Reasons: psychometric concerns, technical reliability fears, theoretical disagreements
- When computerized paradigms are adapted from research, tasks are "often too difficult for patients, causing floor effects and low reliability"

**Cost of doing business:**
- Evaluations cost $2,000-$7,000+ without insurance
- Insurance reimburses $500-$1,200 in best-case HMO scenarios
- Many neuropsychologists don't accept insurance — "must spend several hours on the phone with the insurance company"
- Insurance companies "cutting back on what they will pay. Many aren't paying anything at all."

### For Patients

**Wait times:**
- Average: 5-10 months (adults), 1+ year (children)
- NHS UK: 24-36 months, sometimes 10+ years
- One patient waited 2 years, told she "couldn't have ADHD because she was wearing the right clothes for the weather and could make eye contact"
- For every 5 clinicians contacted, only 1 responds (20% response rate)

**Testing experience:**
- 4-8 hours face-to-face in a single session
- Patients find testing "very tiring"
- Prolonged stress may "impact stress levels and mood, potentially skewing results"
- Extended waits cause "lower patient completion rates" — patients give up

**Cost barrier:**
- $2,000-$7,000+ out of pocket
- Even with insurance, co-pays of $500+
- Educational testing rarely covered
- Some insurers won't cover if school-based evaluation was already done

### Ecological Validity (The Fundamental Problem)
- "A patient's performance on one executive function test may have little or no predictive value for how they may perform on another test, let alone in a complex real world situation"
- EF rating scales and performance-based tests show **divergent** validity, not convergent — they don't measure the same thing
- CPT performance "can differ greatly trial-by-trial or day-by-day, with individuals scoring in the normal range on one day and showing underperformance on another"
- Traditional CPTs in controlled labs cannot "reliably mimic the challenges individuals encounter in everyday life"

### Cultural Bias
- Assessments rely on norms that "may not reflect the educational and linguistic experiences of children from different cultural backgrounds"
- TOVA adult norms: 99% Caucasian from 1993
- Cultural preferences around motivation, compliance, interpersonal engagement all affect results

Based on all complaints, here are the biggest gaps:

## 8. VALIDATION ROADMAP (OpenEvidence Research)

### Digital Testing Validity — What Works
- Meta-analysis of 24 studies (N=1,197): teleneuropsychology effect size Cohen's dz = 0.01 (nonsignificant), 77% of tests within 1 point of in-person
- **Trail Making Test**: strong digital validity, r = 0.71-0.77 for TMT-B (but faster on digital → needs new norms)
- **Verbal tests** (digit span, verbal fluency, verbal learning/memory): within 1/10th SD of in-person — strongest equivalence
- **Motor component tests**: more variability, less suitable for digital adaptation
- Digital versions capture additional metrics (eye movements, hand trajectories) unavailable on paper

### Psychometric Properties Required (Current Tools Fall Short)
- 97% of digital cognitive tools assessed construct validity
- Only **32%** reported test-retest reliability
- Only **8%** reported structural validity
- Only **16%** reported responsiveness
- **0%** reported clinical meaningfulness

### Targets
- ICCs ≥ 0.70 individual tests, ≥ 0.80 composites
- Canonical correlations R = 0.87+ achievable with gold standards
- Sensitivity/specificity > 0.80 for dementia detection

### Validation Study Design (Phased)

**Phase 1 — Initial Validation (6-12 months, $100K-$250K)**
- Counterbalanced crossover: digital vs. gold-standard paper batteries
- 50-100 participants per age decade
- Test-retest: 3 administrations over 1 month in unsupervised home settings

**Phase 2 — Diagnostic Accuracy (12-18 months, $200K-$400K)**
- Case-control: healthy controls vs. MCI, dementia, ADHD, TBI, frontal lobe lesions
- 50+ per group
- Calculate sensitivity, specificity, PPV/NPV

**Phase 3 — Normative Data (18-24 months, $300K-$600K)**
- 300-500 participants stratified by age (10-year bands 18-90+), education, sex, race/ethnicity
- Cannot use paper-based norms — digital requires its own
- Sex-specific norms (women's verbal memory advantage delays dementia diagnosis)
- Education quality matters, not just years

**Phase 4 — Longitudinal (24-36 months, $400K-$800K)**
- Follow cognitively normal + MCI individuals 2-3 years
- Assessments every 6 months
- Establish predictive validity and responsiveness to change

**Total: 3-5 years, $1.15M-$2.35M for comprehensive validation**

### Cost-Saving Strategies
- Partner with academic medical centers for recruitment
- Use existing cohort studies for longitudinal validation
- Publish in peer-reviewed journals to build credibility before FDA submission
- Phased market entry: wellness tool → clinical decision support → diagnostic device

## 9. FDA REGULATORY STRATEGY (OpenEvidence Research)

Digital cognitive assessments = **Software as a Medical Device (SaMD)**

### Regulatory Paths

| Path | Timeline | Cost | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Wellness Tool** (no FDA) | Immediate | Low | Brain training, cognitive wellness — no diagnostic claims |
| **510(k)** | 3-6 months post-submission | Medium | Substantial equivalence to predicate device |
| **De Novo** (recommended) | 6-12 months | Higher | Novel low-moderate risk, establishes you as predicate |
| **PMA** | 12+ months | Highest | High-risk diagnostic claims — unlikely needed |

### De Novo Special Controls Required
- Algorithm failure mitigation
- Model bias assessment across demographic groups
- Prevention of clinician overreliance
- Labeling: tool should not be sole basis for diagnosis
- Postmarket surveillance plan

### Clinical Decision Support Exemption
- 21st Century Cures Act exempts most CDS software from medical device definition
- BUT: applies to tools that guide clinicians, NOT tools that generate diagnostic scores
- Our scoring/classification features likely need FDA pathway

## 11. IP & TEST SECURITY (OpenEvidence Research)

### Avoiding Copyright Issues (Build "Inspired By" Tests)
1. **Measure same construct differently** — don't copy TMT format, design novel processing speed + set-shifting task
2. **Use different stimuli** — never replicate items, images, or sequences from copyrighted tests
3. **Leverage digital capabilities** — adaptive testing, gamification, ecological momentary assessment
4. **Document independent development** — records showing tests built from cognitive neuroscience principles, not reverse-engineered

### Test Security Measures
- Patient identity verification
- Prevention of unauthorized screen recording
- Secure data transmission/storage
- Large item banks with randomization (limited item exposure)
- Detection of invalid performance patterns

## 12. PUBLICATION STRATEGY (OpenEvidence Research)

### Order of Publications
1. Initial validation study (equivalence to gold-standard battery)
2. Reliability study (test-retest reliability + RCIs)
3. Diagnostic accuracy study (sensitivity/specificity for clinical conditions)
4. Normative data publication (comprehensive demographic stratification)
5. Longitudinal validation (predictive validity + responsiveness)

### Target Journals
- Journal of the International Neuropsychological Society
- The Clinical Neuropsychologist
- Assessment
- Psychological Assessment
- Journal of Medical Internet Research (JMIR)

### Key Principle
"Availability of a newer test doesn't render prior versions obsolete until published evidence shows improved diagnostic ability" — clinicians won't adopt without robust evidence

## 13. PRIORITY TESTS TO BUILD FIRST

Based on meta-analytic evidence for strongest digital validity:

**Tier 1 — Strongest Evidence (Build First)**
- Digit span (forward/backward)
- Verbal fluency (phonemic and semantic)
- Verbal learning and memory
- Trail Making Test variants (r = 0.71-0.77)

**Tier 2 — Good Evidence**
- Stroop-like tasks
- N-back tasks
- Set-shifting paradigms

**Tier 3 — Avoid Initially**
- Tests with heavy motor components (higher variability)
- Projective tests (no validation evidence for digital)
- Tests requiring physical manipulatives
